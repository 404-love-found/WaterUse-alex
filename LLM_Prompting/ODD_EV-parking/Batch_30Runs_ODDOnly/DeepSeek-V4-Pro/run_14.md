**Action Situation 1: Queue Order Compliance**  
**Tension:** Users must decide whether to follow the formal queue order or bypass it to gain faster access. Following is fair but may result in longer wait if others bypass. Bypassing gives individual advantage but causes collective disorder.  
**Players:** Two EV users (User A, User B).  
**Strategies:** Follow Queue (F), Bypass (B).  
**Payoff Matrix (ordinal: 1=best, 4=worst):**  

| User A \ User B | F       | B       |
|------------------|---------|---------|
| F                | (2,2)   | (4,1)   |
| B                | (1,4)   | (3,3)   |

**Justification:** This is a social dilemma (Prisoner’s Dilemma) where individual rationality leads to collectively suboptimal outcomes. The ODD+D highlights that users compete for fair queue access and that bypassing undermines perceived legitimacy.

**Action Situation 2: Post‑Charging Move‑Out**  
**Tension:** After charging, users can move their vehicle promptly or overstay in the bay. Moving promptly frees the charger for the next user but incurs personal inconvenience. Overstaying is convenient but blocks others.  
**Players:** Two EV users (User A, User B) when each is the occupant.  
**Strategies:** Move Promptly (M), Overstay (O).  
**Payoff Matrix (ordinal: 1=best, 4=worst):**  

| User A \ User B | M       | O       |
|------------------|---------|---------|
| M                | (2,2)   | (4,1)   |
| O                | (1,4)   | (3,3)   |

**Justification:** The ODD+D explicitly mentions that vehicles remaining after useful charging block the bay, causing delays. This creates a second social dilemma distinct from initial queue compliance, focusing on post‑charging behavior.

**Action Situation 3: Rule Enforcement**  
**Tension:** Staff must decide whether to enforce posted rules (costly) or not. Users decide whether to comply with rules or violate them. Enforcement deters violations but requires effort; non‑enforcement saves effort but may encourage violations.  
**Players:** Parking‑lot management staff (Staff) and an EV user (User).  
**Strategies:** Staff: Enforce (E), Not Enforce (N); User: Comply (C), Violate (V).  
**Payoff Matrix (ordinal: 1=best, 4=worst):**  

| Staff \ User | C       | V       |
|---------------|---------|---------|
| E             | (2,3)   | (3,4)   |
| N             | (1,2)   | (4,1)   |

**Justification:** The ODD+D describes staff decisions to enforce rules and users’ compliance decisions as a core interaction affecting queue fairness and legitimacy. The mixed‑motive payoff structure captures the strategic interdependence.

**Action Situation 4: Violation Reporting**  
**Tension:** Users who observe a rule violation (e.g., queue bypass, overstay) must decide whether to report it to staff or ignore it. Reporting helps maintain fairness but involves effort and social risk; ignoring avoids conflict but allows violations to persist.  
**Players:** Two EV users (User A, User B) who witness a violation.  
**Strategies:** Report (R), Ignore (I).  
**Payoff Matrix (ordinal: 1=best, 4=worst):**  

| User A \ User B | R       | I       |
|------------------|---------|---------|
| R                | (2,2)   | (3,1)   |
| I                | (1,3)   | (4,4)   |

**Justification:** Complaint procedures are explicitly mentioned in the ODD+D. Reporting is a public good that underpins the legitimacy of the governance system, creating a volunteer’s dilemma.

**Players:** EV user (User) and Staff.  
**Strategies:** User: Request Priority (R), Not Request (N); Staff: Grant (G), Deny (D).  
**Payoff Matrix (ordinal: 1=best, 4=worst):**

**Justification:** The ODD+D mentions that users may request informal priority and staff may tolerate informal requests, which affects perceived fairness and legitimacy.

**Players:** Two EV users (User A, User B).  
**Strategies:** Reserve (R), Live Queue (L).  
**Payoff Matrix (ordinal: 1=best, 4=worst):**

|------------------|---------|---------|

**Justification:** The ODD+D describes a digital queue and reservation platform as a key institutional entity. Users’ choice between reservation and live queue creates a coordination/anti‑coordination dilemma.

**Players:** Management (M) and representative User (U).  
**Strategies:** M: Invest (I), Not Invest (N); U: Complain (C), Accept (A).

| M \ U | C       | A       |
|--------|---------|---------|
| I      | (3,2)   | (2,1)   |
| N      | (4,4)   | (1,3)   |

**Justification:** The ODD+D includes capacity planning as a submodel where management evaluates whether to add chargers based on demand and complaints. This represents the strategic tension between infrastructure investment and user pressure.
