# Run 9 - Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct-Turbo

### Action Situation Analysis Using IAD Framework

#### 1. Resident vs. Non-Resident Charging Access
**Title:** Resident Priority vs. Fairness
**Tension:** Residents seek priority access to chargers, while fairness requires equal treatment for all users.

**Matrix:**
|                | Non-Residents Wait | Non-Residents Skip |
|----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Residents Wait** | (2, 2)              | (3, 1)              |
| **Residents Skip** | (1, 3)              | (4, 4)              |

**Justification:** Residents may skip the queue if they believe they have priority, while non-residents may wait if they see residents bypassing the queue. The payoff matrix reflects the strategic tension where residents gain more if they skip, but non-residents may perceive the system as unfair if residents do not wait.

#### 2. Staff vs. User Queue Enforcement
**Title:** Staff Enforcement vs. User Compliance
**Tension:** Staff need to enforce queue rules to ensure fairness, while users may resist or circumvent these rules to minimize wait times.

**Matrix:**
|                | Users Cooperate | Users Bypass Queue |
|----------------|-----------------|--------------------|
| **Staff Enforce** | (3, 3)          | (2, 4)              |
| **Staff Ignore** | (4, 2)          | (1, 1)              |

**Justification:** If staff enforce the queue, both parties benefit, but if users bypass the queue, users gain but staff face increased complaints. If staff ignore the queue, users may become more compliant, but staff face fewer complaints but also reduced fairness.

#### 3. Resident vs. Non-Resident Charging Time
**Title:** Resident Charging Priority vs. Visitor Demand
**Tension:** Residents may demand priority charging time, while non-residents need fair access to chargers.

**Matrix:**
|                | Non-Residents Wait | Non-Residents Skip |
|----------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Residents Wait** | (2, 2)              | (3, 1)              |
| **Residents Skip** | (1, 3)              | (4, 4)              |

**Justification:** Similar to the first situation, residents may skip the queue if they believe they have priority, while non-residents may wait if they perceive residents as using more time. The payoff matrix reflects the strategic tension where residents gain more if they skip, but non-residents may perceive the system as unfair if residents do not wait.

#### 4. User vs. Charger Occupancy
**Title:** Charger Utilization vs. User Convenience
**Tension:** Users prefer convenient chargers, while charger utilization needs to be maximized to meet all demand.

**Matrix:**
|                | Use Convenient Charger | Use Less Convenient Charger |
|----------------|------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Charge Faster** | (3, 2)                 | (2, 3)                      |
| **Charge Slower** | (2, 3)                 | (1, 4)                      |

**Justification:** Users may prefer to charge at a faster, more convenient charger, but this can lead to longer wait times for others. The payoff matrix reflects the strategic tension where users gain more convenience but reduce overall charger utilization.

#### 5. Management vs. Resident Network Influence
**Title:** Management Policy vs. Resident Pressure
**Tension:** Management may revise policies based on resident pressure, while residents seek favorable rules to ensure fair access.

**Matrix:**
|                | Residents Pressure | Residents Do Not Pressure |
|----------------|--------------------|----------------------------|
| **Management Changes Policy** | (2, 2)            | (1, 3)                      |
| **Management Does Not Change Policy** | (3, 1)        | (4, 4)                      |

**Justification:** If management changes policies in response to resident pressure, both parties benefit, but if management does not change, residents may become more compliant, but management faces increased pressure. The payoff matrix reflects the strategic tension where residents gain more influence if they pressure management, but management may face reduced fairness concerns.

These action situations capture the key strategic tensions in the shared EV charging model, focusing on local governance, queue fairness, and user-management interactions.
