### Analysis of Distinct Action Situations in the Shared EV Charging Model

#### 1. **Resident vs. Non-Resident Queue Positioning**

**Title:** Resident vs. Non-Resident Charging Priority

**Tension:** Residents may perceive that they should have priority access to chargers over non-residents, given their resident discount. However, non-residents may feel that they should be treated equally if they pay the regular rate.

**Matrix:**

|             | Residents Queue First | Non-Residents Queue First |
|-------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| **Residents Queue First** | (2, 2) (Residents get priority, but non-residents may feel discriminated against) | (1, 3) (Residents get priority, but non-residents experience longer wait times) |
| **Non-Residents Queue First** | (3, 1) (Residents experience longer wait times, but non-residents get priority) | (1, 1) (Both get priority, but may lead to overuse of chargers) |

**Justification:** This situation reflects the strategic tension between fairness and efficiency. Residents may prefer to queue first to ensure they get access, while non-residents may prefer equal treatment regardless of their payment status.

#### 2. **User vs. Management Enforcement of Queue Rules**

**Title:** User vs. Management Queue Enforcement

**Tension:** Users may decide whether to follow queue rules, which can affect their waiting time. Management staff may decide whether to enforce these rules, which can influence user compliance and trust.

**Matrix:**

|             | Users Comply with Queue Rules | Users Bypass Queue Rules |
|-------------|------------------------------|-------------------------|
| **Management Enforces Rules** | (2, 2) (Low enforcement costs, high compliance) | (1, 3) (High enforcement costs, low compliance) |
| **Management Tolerates Bypassing** | (3, 1) (Low enforcement costs, low compliance) | (1, 1) (High compliance, but may lead to queue chaos) |

**Justification:** This situation highlights the strategic tension between the cost of enforcement and the level of compliance. Management may choose to enforce rules strictly to maintain order, but this can lead to higher costs. Users may choose to bypass the queue to reduce their waiting time, but this can lead to chaos and reduced trust in the system.

#### 3. **Resident vs. Resident Charging Norms**

**Title:** Resident vs. Resident Charging Norms

**Tension:** Residents may form informal norms about when to charge and how much to use the chargers. These norms can affect the overall fairness and efficiency of the charging system.

**Matrix:**

|             | Charge Off-Peak | Charge Peak |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Charge Off-Peak** | (2, 2) (Low congestion, fair access) | (1, 3) (High congestion, reduced access) |
| **Charge Peak** | (3, 1) (High congestion, reduced access) | (1, 1) (High congestion, reduced access) |

**Justification:** This situation reflects the strategic tension between charging off-peak (which reduces congestion) and charging peak (which maximizes energy use but can lead to congestion). Residents may prefer to charge off-peak to ensure fair access, but this can lead to higher energy costs for the building.

#### 4. **User vs. Management Information Sharing**

**Title:** User vs. Management Information Sharing

**Tension:** Users may seek information about charger availability and queue positions, while management may want to control this information to maintain order and reduce complaints.

**Matrix:**

|             | Users Share Information | Users Do Not Share Information |
|-------------|-------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Management Controls Information** | (2, 2) (Users get accurate information, management reduces complaints) | (1, 3) (Users get inaccurate information, management faces more complaints) |
| **Management Shares Information** | (3, 1) (Users get inaccurate information, management maintains order) | (1, 1) (Users get accurate information, but management faces more complaints) |

**Justification:** This situation highlights the strategic tension between the accuracy of information and the level of trust between users and management. Users may prefer accurate information to make informed decisions, but this can lead to more complaints from management. Management may prefer to control information to reduce complaints, but this can lead to users seeking alternative sources of information.

#### 5. **User vs. Management Charging Capacity Management**

**Title:** User vs. Management Charging Capacity Management

**Tension:** Users may seek to maximize their charging time, while management may want to limit charging to ensure fair access and prevent overuse of chargers.

**Matrix:**

|             | Users Charge Long | Users Charge Short |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Management Limits Charging Time** | (2, 2) (Fair access, manageable capacity) | (1, 3) (Overuse, reduced access) |
| **Management Allows Long Charging** | (3, 1) (Overuse, reduced access) | (1, 1) (Fair access, but may lead to overuse) |

**Justification:** This situation reflects the strategic tension between maximizing user satisfaction and maintaining fair access to chargers. Users may prefer to charge for longer periods to ensure they have enough energy, but this can lead to overuse and reduced access for others. Management may prefer to limit charging time to ensure fair access, but this can lead to user dissatisfaction.
