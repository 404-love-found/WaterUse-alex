### Title: Strategic Tensions in Shared EV Charging

#### 1. Resident vs. Non-Resident Queue Access

**Tension:**
Residents and non-residents compete for limited EV charging access, with residents receiving a discounted per-kWh rate and non-residents paying the regular rate. The core tension is whether residents should have priority access to chargers, which affects queue fairness and user satisfaction.

**Matrix:**

|           | Non-Resident Follows Queue | Non-Resident Bypasses Queue |
|-----------|----------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Resident Follows Queue** | (Short Wait, Fair) | (Long Wait, Unfair) |
| **Resident Bypasses Queue** | (Long Wait, Unfair) | (Short Wait, Unfair) |

**Justification:**
Residents may believe they have priority access due to their discounted rate, while non-residents may feel entitled to equal treatment. Bypassing the queue can reduce wait time for both, but it undermines fairness and trust in the system.

#### 2. Staff vs. Users on Queue Enforcement

**Tension:**
Parking-lot management staff must decide whether to enforce posted queue rules or tolerate informal requests to maintain order and reduce complaints.

**Matrix:**

|           | User Follows Queue | User Bypasses Queue |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Staff Enforces Queue** | (Low Complaints, Order) | (High Complaints, Chaos) |
| **Staff Tolerates Exceptions** | (High Complaints, Chaos) | (Low Complaints, Order) |

**Justification:**
Strict enforcement reduces violations but can lead to resident complaints, while tolerance can reduce conflicts but undermine queue order and fairness.

#### 3. Resident vs. Non-Resident on Charger Utilization

**Tension:**
Residents and non-residents compete for charger utilization, with residents having more flexible schedules and potentially more frequent charging needs.

**Matrix:**

|           | Non-Resident Uses Charger | Non-Resident Waits |
|-----------|---------------------------|--------------------|
| **Resident Uses Charger** | (Fair Access, Utilization) | (Overuse, Conflict) |
| **Resident Waits** | (Underuse, Conflict) | (Fair Access, Utilization) |

**Justification:**
Residents may use chargers more frequently due to their flexible schedules, leading to potential overuse and conflict with non-residents who may have more urgent needs.

#### 4. Management vs. Residents on Charging Capacity Expansion

**Tension:**
Management must decide whether to expand charging capacity based on resident pressure versus the financial and operational costs.

**Matrix:**

|           | Expand Capacity | Do Not Expand Capacity |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------------|
| **Residents Support** | (Improved Access, Satisfaction) | (Increased Demand, Conflict) |
| **Residents Oppose** | (Current Access, Conflict) | (Current Access, Satisfaction) |

**Justification:**
Expanding capacity benefits residents and can improve system fairness but may not be financially feasible or operationally efficient.

#### 5. User vs. Staff on Informal Priority

**Tension:**
Users may seek informal priority from staff, while staff must decide whether to grant these requests based on enforcement policies.

**Matrix:**

|           | User Seeks Informal Priority | User Follows Queue |
|-----------|------------------------------|--------------------|
| **Staff Grants Priority** | (Short Wait, Conflict) | (Long Wait, Order) |
| **Staff Denies Priority** | (Long Wait, Order) | (Short Wait, Conflict) |

**Justification:**
Granting informal priority can reduce wait times but undermines queue fairness and trust, while denying it can increase conflicts.

#### 6. Resident vs. Non-Resident on Off-Peak Charging Norms

**Tension:**
Residents and non-residents may develop different norms for off-peak charging, with residents potentially using off-peak slots more frequently.

**Matrix:**

|           | Non-Resident Uses Off-Peak | Non-Resident Follows Peak |
|-----------|----------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Resident Uses Off-Peak** | (Fair Access, Utilization) | (Overuse, Conflict) |
| **Resident Follows Peak** | (Underuse, Conflict) | (Fair Access, Utilization) |

**Justification:**
Residents may develop a habit of using off-peak slots, leading to overuse and conflict with non-residents who may follow peak usage norms.

#### 7. User vs. Staff on Charging Faults

**Tension:**
Users may report faulty chargers, while staff must decide how quickly to address these issues, balancing user satisfaction and operational efficiency.

**Matrix:**

|           | User Reports Fault | User Waits for Fault |
|-----------|--------------------|---------------------|
| **Staff Responds Quickly** | (Quick Fix, Satisfaction) | (Delayed Fix, Conflict) |
| **Staff Delays Response** | (Delayed Fix, Conflict) | (Quick Fix, Satisfaction) |

**Justification:**
Quick response to faults improves user satisfaction but may increase staff workload, while delayed response can lead to user frustration and conflict.

#### 8. Resident vs. Non-Resident on Perceived Legitimacy

**Tension:**
Residents and non-residents may have different perceptions of the queue system's legitimacy, with residents believing their discount entitles them to better access.

**Matrix:**

|           | Non-Resident Sees Fairness | Non-Resident Sees Unfairness |
|-----------|----------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Resident Sees Fairness** | (Satisfaction, Order) | (Conflict, Order) |
| **Resident Sees Unfairness** | (Conflict, Order) | (Satisfaction, Order) |

**Justification:**
Residents may believe the system is fair due to their discount, while non-residents may see it as unfair, leading to conflict and reduced satisfaction.

#### 9. User vs. Staff on Charging Duration

**Tension:**
Users may charge their vehicles for longer than necessary, while staff must enforce move-out rules to ensure chargers are available for the next user.

**Matrix:**

|           | User Charges Long | User Charges Short |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------------|
| **Staff Enforces Short** | (Fair Access, Order) | (Conflict, Order) |
| **Staff Tolerates Long** | (Conflict, Order) | (Fair Access, Order) |

**Justification:**
Tolerating long charging times can reduce conflicts but may lead to unfair access for other users, while enforcing short charges can improve fairness but may cause user dissatisfaction.

#### 10. Management vs. Users on Resident Discount

**Tension:**
Management must decide whether to maintain the resident discount, which benefits residents but may be perceived as unfair by non-residents.

**Matrix:**

|           | Maintain Discount | Eliminate Discount |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------------|
| **Residents Support** | (Satisfaction, Access) | (Conflict, Access) |
| **Residents Oppose** | (Conflict, Access) | (Satisfaction, Access) |

**Justification:**
Maintaining the discount benefits residents but may lead to conflicts with non-residents who may perceive it as unfair, while eliminating it can reduce conflicts but may reduce resident satisfaction.
