# Run 2 - deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-V4-Pro

### Action Situation 1: Queue Order Compliance
**Tension:** Users decide whether to follow the posted queue order or bypass it to gain quicker access. Individual bypassing reduces waiting time for the defector but increases waiting time for others, creating a social dilemma.

**Matrix (User A vs User B):**
| User A \ User B | Follow Queue (F) | Bypass (B) |
|------------------|------------------|------------|
| Follow Queue (F) | (3,3)            | (1,4)      |
| Bypass (B)       | (4,1)            | (2,2)      |

**Justification:** Captures the core tension between individual incentives to jump the queue and the collective interest in maintaining a fair and predictable charging order. It is distinct because it focuses on the entry point to charging access.

---

### Action Situation 2: Post-Charging Bay Release
**Tension:** After completing a charging session, a user can either move the vehicle promptly to free the bay or overstay, blocking others. Overstaying provides personal convenience but reduces overall throughput and increases waiting times.

**Matrix (User A vs User B):**
| User A \ User B | Move Promptly (M) | Overstay (O) |
|------------------|-------------------|--------------|
| Move Promptly (M) | (3,3)             | (1,4)        |
| Overstay (O)      | (4,1)             | (2,2)        |

**Justification:** Distinct from queue entry because it addresses the release of a charging resource after use. The strategic tension arises from the temporal mismatch: the cost of overstaying is externalized to waiting users, while the benefit is private.

---

### Action Situation 3: Rule Enforcement (Staff vs. User)
**Tension:** Staff decide whether to invest effort in enforcing queue and move-out rules, while users decide whether to comply. Enforcement is costly for staff, and users prefer to violate if not enforced, creating an inspection dilemma.

**Matrix (Staff vs User):**
| Staff \ User | Comply (C) | Violate (V) |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| Enforce (E)   | (3,3)       | (2,1)       |
| Not Enforce (N)| (4,2)       | (1,4)       |

**Justification:** Captures the direct interaction between the institutional actor (staff) and the users. It is central to understanding how rule enforcement effort and compliance co-evolve, affecting the legitimacy of the governance system.

---

### Action Situation 4: Informal Priority Request
**Tension:** A user can request preferential treatment (e.g., holding a bay) from staff, and staff can grant or deny such requests. Granting creates private benefits but undermines formal queue fairness and staff credibility.

**Matrix (User vs Staff):**
| User \ Staff | Grant (G) | Deny (D) |
|--------------|-----------|----------|
| Request (R)  | (4,3)     | (1,4)    |
| Not Request (N)| (2,2)    | (2,2)    |

**Justification:** Highlights the tension between formal rules and informal practices. It is distinct because it involves a direct request for an exception, which can erode trust in the queue platform if tolerated.

---

### Action Situation 5: Violation Reporting
**Tension:** Users who observe a queue violation or overstay must decide whether to report it to management. Reporting imposes a personal cost but helps enforce rules; ignoring saves effort but allows violations to persist.

**Matrix (User A vs User B):**
| User A \ User B | Report (R) | Ignore (I) |
|------------------|-------------|-------------|
| Report (R)       | (2,2)       | (2,4)       |
| Ignore (I)       | (4,2)       | (1,1)       |

**Justification:** A volunteer’s dilemma where the collectively beneficial outcome (violation addressed) requires at least one user to bear the cost of reporting. It is distinct because it focuses on the monitoring and complaint aspect of shared governance.

---

### Action Situation 6: Charger Capacity Investment
**Tension:** Residents collectively benefit from expanding the number of chargers, but investment requires contributions (e.g., higher fees). Each resident prefers to free-ride on others’ contributions while still enjoying improved capacity.

**Matrix (Resident A vs Resident B):**
| Resident A \ Resident B | Contribute (C) | Free-ride (F) |
|------------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| Contribute (C)         | (3,3)           | (1,4)         |
| Free-ride (F)          | (4,1)           | (2,2)         |

**Justification:** Addresses the long-term governance of shared infrastructure capacity. It is distinct because it involves collective investment decisions rather than day-to-day operational behavior.

---

### Action Situation 7: Resident vs. Non-resident Access Norm
**Tension:** Residents and non-residents may hold conflicting norms about who has stronger claim to limited chargers. Residents might claim priority based on their housing relationship, while non-residents assert equal access based on paying the regular rate.

**Matrix (Resident vs Non-resident):**
| Resident \ Non-resident | Respect Queue (Q) | Demand Priority (P) |
|------------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Respect Queue (Q)       | (3,3)             | (1,4)               |
| Demand Priority (P)     | (4,1)             | (2,2)               |

**Justification:** Captures the normative conflict between user categories. The asymmetry in perceived entitlement can destabilize the queue even without formal rule changes, making it a distinct action situation.
