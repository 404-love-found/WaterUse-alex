### Title: Strategic Tensions in Decentralized Water Use Model

#### 1. Strategic Tension: Spatial Asymmetry vs Ecological Thresholds

**Tension:**
In the decentralized version (DV), downstream farmers have an advantage in accessing water and fish resources, but they also face ecological thresholds that can lead to a tipping point where the fish population collapses. Upstream farmers, who have less access to water and fish, must balance their water demands with the potential for water stress, which affects their yields.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|           | Upstream Farmer (U) | Upstream Farmer (U) |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Downstream Farmer (D) | (YieldD, YieldU) | (YieldD, YieldU) |
| Downstream Farmer (D) | (YieldD, YieldU) | (YieldD, YieldU) |

**Justification:**
- **Downstream Farmer (D):** Downstream farmers have access to water and fish resources first. They can choose to irrigate more fields, which may lead to higher yields but also to a risk of over-exploitation if the ecological threshold is approached. If the fish population collapses, the downstream farmers will suffer from a lack of fish resources.
- **Upstream Farmer (U):** Upstream farmers have less access to water and fish resources. They must balance their water demands with the potential for water stress, which affects their yields. If the downstream farmers over-exploit the resources, the upstream farmers will suffer from reduced water flow, leading to lower yields.

The ecological threshold is a critical point where the fish population may collapse, leading to a significant drop in fish yields for both farmers. The strategic tension lies in whether the downstream farmers should risk over-exploitation to increase their yields or whether the upstream farmers should limit their water use to protect the fish population and ensure sustainable yields for both.

#### 2. Strategic Tension: Risk Aversion vs Risk Taking

**Tension:**
In the decentralized version (DV), downstream farmers have the option to take a risk and increase the number of irrigated fields if their income is below a critical threshold, hoping for more water in the current year. Upstream farmers, who have a higher water stress, are more risk-averse and will not increase their water use if their previous year's income was below the critical threshold.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|           | Upstream Farmer (U) | Upstream Farmer (U) |
|-----------|---------------------|---------------------|
| Downstream Farmer (D) | (YieldD, YieldU) | (YieldD, YieldU) |
| Downstream Farmer (D) | (YieldD, YieldU) | (YieldD, YieldU) |

**Justification:**
- **Downstream Farmer (D):** Downstream farmers have an incentive to take a risk and increase the number of irrigated fields if their income is below the critical threshold. This can lead to higher yields but also to a higher risk of water stress and potential yield reductions if the water flow is insufficient.
- **Upstream Farmer (U):** Upstream farmers are more risk-averse and will not increase their water use if their previous year's income was below the critical threshold. This ensures they have a more stable and predictable yield but may miss out on higher yields if water flow is sufficient.

The strategic tension lies in whether the downstream farmers should take a risk to increase their yields or whether the upstream farmers should maintain a more stable and predictable water use to ensure sustainable yields.
