### Action Situation 1: Upstream Farmer's Irrigation Decision
#### Tension: Over-Irrigation vs. Conservative Irrigation
#### Matrix:

|  | Upstream Farmer (Max Fields) | Upstream Farmer (Conservative) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Downstream Farmer (Max Fields)** | Upstream: 8, Downstream: 2 | Upstream: 6, Downstream: 4 |
| **Downstream Farmer (Conservative)** | Upstream: 9, Downstream: 1 | Upstream: 7, Downstream: 3 |

#### Justification: 
In the decentralized case, an upstream farmer's decision to irrigate maximally (up to 10 fields) or conservatively affects the downstream farmer's water availability and thus their potential yield. The upstream farmer's choice to irrigate conservatively allows more water to flow downstream, benefiting the downstream farmer. However, if both choose to irrigate maximally, the downstream farmer suffers significantly due to reduced water availability, reflecting the strategic tension of balancing individual gains with collective resource sustainability.

### Action Situation 2: Farmer's Decision to Fish
#### Tension: Over-Fishing vs. Sustainable Fishing
#### Matrix:

|  | Downstream Farmer (Max Catch) | Downstream Farmer (Sustainable Catch) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Upstream Farmer (Max Catch)** | Downstream: 10, Upstream: 0 | Downstream: 8, Upstream: 2 |
| **Upstream Farmer (Sustainable Catch)** | Downstream: 12, Upstream: 0 | Downstream: 10, Upstream: 2 |

#### Justification: 
Given the spatial asymmetry where downstream farmers have first access to the fishing lake, their decision on how much to fish impacts the upstream farmers' potential catch. Over-fishing by downstream farmers can lead to ecological thresholds being crossed, depleting fish populations and affecting both farmers' long-term sustainability. The matrix reflects the tension between maximizing short-term gains through over-fishing and adopting sustainable practices that ensure long-term resource availability.

### Action Situation 3: Farmer's Investment in Irrigation Efficiency
#### Tension: Invest in Efficiency vs. Maintain Status Quo
#### Matrix:

|  | Upstream Farmer (Invest) | Upstream Farmer (Status Quo) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Downstream Farmer (Invest)** | Upstream: 9, Downstream: 5 | Upstream: 7, Downstream: 3 |
| **Downstream Farmer (Status Quo)** | Upstream: 10, Downstream: 4 | Upstream: 8, Downstream: 2 |

#### Justification: 
Investing in irrigation efficiency can increase water availability for both upstream and downstream farmers, reducing the strategic tension related to water scarcity. However, the investment cost and the potential for free-riding (if one farmer invests while the other does not) create a dilemma. The matrix shows that mutual investment leads to a better outcome for both, but if only one invests, they might not reap sufficient benefits to justify their investment, especially considering the ecological thresholds that could be crossed if water efficiency is not improved.
