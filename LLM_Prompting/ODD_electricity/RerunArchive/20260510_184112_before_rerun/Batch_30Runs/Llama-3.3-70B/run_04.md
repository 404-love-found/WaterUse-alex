# Run 4 — meta-llama/Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct-Turbo

### Distinct Action Situations in Electricity Governance Model

1. **Capacitor Adoption Assurance Game**
	* Tension: Coordination vs. Free-Riding
	* Matrix/Sequential Representation:
		|  | Farmer 2 Adopts | Farmer 2 Does Not Adopt |
		| --- | --- | --- |
		| Farmer 1 Adopts | 3, 3 | 1, 2 |
		| Farmer 1 Does Not Adopt | 2, 1 | 2, 2 |
	* Justification: Farmers must decide whether to adopt capacitors, which improve voltage stability and pump efficiency. Mutual adoption yields shared benefits, but unilateral adoption provides little private benefit.

2. **Transformer Capacity Authorization Dilemma**
	* Tension: Contribution vs. Free-Riding
	* Matrix/Sequential Representation:
		|  | Farmer 2 Contributes | Farmer 2 Does Not Contribute |
		| --- | --- | --- |
		| Farmer 1 Contributes | 3, 3 | 1, 4 |
		| Farmer 1 Does Not Contribute | 4, 1 | 2, 2 |
	* Justification: Farmers must decide whether to contribute to transformer capacity upgrades. Contribution benefits all connected farmers, but costs fall solely on the contributor.

3. **Mutual Exchange Coordination Game**
	* Tension: Reciprocity vs. Exploitation
	* Matrix/Sequential Representation:
		|  | Staff Reciprocates | Staff Does Not Reciprocate |
		| --- | --- | --- |
		| Farmer Cooperates | 3, 3 | 0, 2 |
		| Farmer Does Not Cooperate | 2, 0 | 1, 1 |
	* Justification: Farmers and staff engage in informal exchanges, where reciprocal benefit arises only when both cooperate. If one side cooperates and the other does not, the cooperating side loses.

4. **Authorization and Investment Asymmetric Coordination Game**
	* Tension: Formal Compliance vs. Informal Exchange
	* Sequential Representation:
		1. Farmer requests formal access.
		2. Staff decides to invest or withhold capacity.
		* If staff invests, farmer gains reliability, but staff bears effort costs.
		* If staff withholds, farmer loses, but staff saves effort.
	* Justification: Farmers and staff interact under formal electricity rules and informal local relationships. Formal compliance can involve authorization fees and staff effort, while informal exchange can involve tolerance of unauthorized access.

5. **Groundwater Extraction Prisoner's Dilemma**
	* Tension: Individual Gain vs. Collective Sustainability
	* Matrix/Sequential Representation:
		|  | Farmer 2 Restraints | Farmer 2 Over-Extracts |
		| --- | --- | --- |
		| Farmer 1 Restraints | 3, 3 | 1, 4 |
		| Farmer 1 Over-Extracts | 4, 1 | 2, 2 |
	* Justification: Farmers must decide how much groundwater to extract. Mutual restraint sustains yields, but unilateral over-extraction offers short-term gain and accelerates depletion.

6. **Social Learning and Capacitor Adoption**
	* Tension: Imitation vs. Independent Decision-Making
	* Sequential Representation:
		1. Farmer observes neighbor's adoption outcome.
		2. Farmer decides to adopt or not based on observed outcome.
		* If neighbor's adoption is successful, farmer is more likely to adopt.
		* If neighbor's adoption fails, farmer is less likely to adopt.
	* Justification: Farmers learn from each other's experiences and may imitate successful neighbors. This process can lead to the diffusion of capacitor adoption.

7. **Enforcement and Maintenance Dilemma**
	* Tension: Formal Enforcement vs. Informal Tolerance
	* Matrix/Sequential Representation:
		|  | Staff Enforces | Staff Tolerates |
		| --- | --- | --- |
		| Farmer Complies | 3, 2 | 2, 3 |
		| Farmer Does Not Comply | 1, 4 | 4, 1 |
	* Justification: Staff must decide whether to enforce formal rules or tolerate informal access. Formal enforcement can improve reliability, but may lead to penalties and reputational risks for farmers. Informal tolerance can reduce costs for farmers, but may lead to overloading and reduced reliability.
