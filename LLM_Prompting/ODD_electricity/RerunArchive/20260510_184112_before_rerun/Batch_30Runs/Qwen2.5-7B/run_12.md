### Title: Capacitor Adoption Assurance Game (AS1)

### Tension: Farmer-Farmer Coordination vs. Individual Rationality

### Matrix Representation:
\[
\begin{array}{c|cc}
 & \text{Farmer 2: Invest} & \text{Farmer 2: Not Invest} \\
\hline
\text{Farmer 1: Invest} & (3, 3) & (5, 1) \\
\text{Farmer 1: Not Invest} & (1, 5) & (2, 2) \\
\end{array}
\]

### Justification:
- **Farmer 1 and Farmer 2** must decide whether to invest in a capacitor. Mutual investment yields shared improvement, increasing voltage stability and pump efficiency. However, unilateral investment provides no additional private benefit, creating a coordination problem. The matrix reflects the tension between mutual cooperation (both invest) and individual rationality (one invests while the other does not).

### Title: Sequential Social-Learning Process in Capacitor Adoption (AS2)

### Tension: Social Learning vs. Rational Decision-Making

### Sequential Representation (Game Tree):
```
[Farmer 1]
  |
  [Farmer 2]
  /     \
(0,0)   [Farmer 1]
        /     \
      (0,0)   [Farmer 2]
                /     \
              (0,0)   (1,1)
```

### Justification:
- **Farmer 1** and **Farmer 2** observe the outcome of a neighbor’s capacitor adoption. The tree represents a sequential process where each farmer makes a decision based on the observed outcome. The first level of the tree shows the initial decision, and the second level shows the subsequent decision based on the observed outcome. The tension lies in whether farmers will adopt a capacitor based on social learning rather than rational decision-making.

### Title: Asymmetric Transformer-Capacity Authorization Dilemma (AS3)

### Tension: Farmer-Farmer Conflict vs. Collective Benefit

### Matrix Representation:
\[
\begin{array}{c|cc}
 & \text{Farmer 2: Authorize} & \text{Farmer 2: Not Authorize} \\
\hline
\text{Farmer 1: Authorize} & (3, 3) & (5, 1) \\
\text{Farmer 1: Not Authorize} & (1, 5) & (2, 2) \\
\end{array}
\]

### Justification:
- **Farmer 1 and Farmer 2** must decide whether to authorize a transformer capacity upgrade. Mutual authorization benefits both by raising voltage quality, but costs fall solely on the authorizer, creating a free-rider incentive. The matrix reflects the tension between mutual cooperation (both authorize) and individual rationality (one authorizes while the other does not).

### Title: Groundwater-Extraction Prisoner’s Dilemma (AS6)

### Tension: Short-Term Gain vs. Long-Term Sustainability

### Matrix Representation:
\[
\begin{array}{c|cc}
 & \text{Farmer 2: Restrain} & \text{Farmer 2: Over-Extract} \\
\hline
\text{Farmer 1: Restrain} & (3, 3) & (1, 5) \\
\text{Farmer 1: Over-Extract} & (5, 1) & (2, 2) \\
\end{array}
\]

### Justification:
- **Farmer 1 and Farmer 2** must decide whether to restrain or over-extract groundwater from the same aquifer. Mutual restraint sustains yields, but unilateral over-extraction offers short-term gain and accelerates depletion. The matrix reflects the tension between mutual restraint (both restrain) and individual rationality (one over-extracts while the other restrains).

### Title: Farmer-Farmer Informal Exchange Dilemma (AS2)

### Tension: Mutual Reciprocity vs. Self-Interest

### Sequential Representation (Game Tree):
```
[Farmer 1]
  |
  [Farmer 2]
  /     \
(0,0)   [Farmer 1]
        /     \
      (0,0)   [Farmer 2]
                /     \
              (0,0)   (1,1)
```

### Justification:
- **Farmer 1** and **Farmer 2** must decide whether to engage in informal exchange or not. Mutual reciprocity benefits both sides, but mismatched expectations can lead to losses. The tree represents a sequential process where each farmer makes a decision based on the observed outcome of the other’s previous decision. The tension lies in whether farmers will engage in mutual reciprocity based on expected future behavior.
