### Title: Distinct Action Situations in the Irrigation Electricity Governance Model

#### 1. Farmer-Farmer Coordination on Capacitor Adoption (AS1)
**Tension:** Mutual Cooperation vs. Free-Rider Dilemma

**Matrix:**
|               | Farmer 2: Invest | Farmer 2: Not Invest |
|---------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Farmer 1: Invest** | (3, 3)          | (0, 4)              |
| **Farmer 1: Not Invest** | (4, 0)         | (2, 2)              |

**Justification:** Both farmers benefit from mutual investment in capacitors, as it stabilizes the voltage and improves overall power quality. However, unilateral investment yields no private benefit, creating a free-rider incentive.

#### 2. Sequential Social Learning on Capacitor Adoption (AS2)
**Tension:** Diffusion of Innovations vs. Social Inertia

**Matrix:**
|               | Farmer 2: Adopt | Farmer 2: Not Adopt |
|---------------|----------------|--------------------|
| **Farmer 1: Adopt** | (3, 3)         | (1, 3)             |
| **Farmer 1: Not Adopt** | (3, 1)        | (2, 2)             |

**Justification:** Farmers observe and imitate successful outcomes. If a neighbor adopts and experiences positive results, the other farmer is more likely to adopt. However, if the adoption does not yield immediate benefits, social inertia may prevent diffusion.

#### 3. Asymmetric Transformer Capacity Authorization (AS3)
**Tension:** Mutual Benefit vs. Free-Rider Dilemma

**Matrix:**
|               | Farmer 2: Authorize | Farmer 2: Not Authorize |
|---------------|------------------|------------------------|
| **Farmer 1: Authorize** | (3, 3)            | (0, 4)                 |
| **Farmer 1: Not Authorize** | (4, 0)          | (2, 2)                 |

**Justification:** Mutual authorization benefits both farmers by improving voltage quality, but the costs fall solely on the authorizer, creating a free-rider incentive. If only one farmer authorizes, the non-authorizing farmer benefits more.

#### 4. Mutual-Exchange Coordination with Staff (AS4)
**Tension:** Informal vs. Formal Exchange

**Matrix:**
|               | Staff: Invest | Staff: Not Invest |
|---------------|--------------|------------------|
| **Farmer: Invest** | (4, 4)       | (3, 3)           |
| **Farmer: Not Invest** | (3, 3)      | (2, 2)           |

**Justification:** Both formal and informal exchanges yield mutual benefits, but the farmer incurs a cost if the staff invest without a formal request. If both parties engage in exchange, they both benefit; otherwise, no additional gain occurs.

#### 5. Asymmetric Authorization and Investment (AS5)
**Tension:** Formal Cooperation vs. Opportunism

**Matrix:**
|               | Staff: Invest | Staff: Not Invest |
|---------------|--------------|------------------|
| **Farmer: Formal Request** | (3, 3)       | (0, 4)           |
| **Farmer: Informal Request** | (4, 0)      | (2, 2)           |

**Justification:** Formal cooperation is collectively optimal but costly for the farmer. Informal requests reduce the farmer’s cost but may save the staff effort or cost. If the staff withhold under a formal request, the farmer incurs a loss. If the staff invest under an informal request, the farmer gains more.

#### 6. Groundwater Extraction Prisoner’s Dilemma (AS6)
**Tension:** Mutual Restraint vs. Short-Term Gain

**Matrix:**
|               | Farmer 2: Restrain | Farmer 2: Over-Extract |
|---------------|------------------|-----------------------|
| **Farmer 1: Restrain** | (3, 3)           | (0, 5)                |
| **Farmer 1: Over-Extract** | (5, 0)          | (1, 1)                |

**Justification:** Mutual restraint sustains yields, but unilateral over-extraction offers short-term gain and accelerates depletion. If one farmer over-extracts, the other gains more but risks depletion.

### Summary
The model captures six distinct strategic dilemmas, each reflecting a unique tension between cooperation, free-riding, social learning, and bounded rationality in the context of irrigation electricity governance and energy efficiency.
