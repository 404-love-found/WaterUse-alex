### Title: Capacitor Adoption Assurance Game (AS1)

### Tension: Coordination vs. Free-Rider Dilemma

### Matrix:
|                  | Farmer B: Adopts Capacitor | Farmer B: Does Not Adopt Capacitor |
|------------------|----------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Farmer A: Adopts Capacitor** | (2, 2)                     | (1, 0)                            |
| **Farmer A: Does Not Adopt Capacitor** | (0, 1)                     | (1, 1)                            |

### Justification:
In the Capacitor Adoption Assurance Game (AS1), two neighboring farmers must decide whether to invest in voltage-stabilizing capacitors. If both farmers adopt capacitors, the local voltage quality improves, benefiting both. However, if only one farmer invests, the benefits are minimal or hard to attribute, making unilateral investment unattractive. This creates a coordination problem where mutual cooperation is Pareto-dominant but risky due to the free-rider incentive, where one farmer can benefit from the other's investment without contributing.

### Title: Transformer Capacity Authorization Dilemma (AS3)

### Tension: Free-Rider Dilemma vs. Informal Exchange

### Matrix:
|                  | Farmer B: Invests in Capacity | Farmer B: Does Not Invest in Capacity |
|------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| **Farmer A: Invests in Capacity** | (2, 1)                        | (1, 0)                                |
| **Farmer A: Does Not Invest in Capacity** | (0, 2)                        | (1, 1)                                |

### Justification:
In the Transformer Capacity Authorization Dilemma (AS3), two farmers decide whether to invest in capacity improvements that raise local voltage quality. If both farmers invest, the improvement benefits both, but costs fall solely on the investor, creating a free-rider incentive. If only one farmer invests, the non-investor benefits more, while the investor bears the cost. Alternatively, farmers can engage in informal exchange, where one farmer can get access without formal cost, but this can create a free-rider incentive and uneven payoffs, as the offerer bears a loss while the abstainer reverts to baseline.

### Justification:
In the Mutual-Exchange Coordination Game (AS4), a farmer and sub-station staff decide whether to engage in informal exchange, where reciprocal benefit arises only when both engage. This creates a coordination problem where mutual cooperation is necessary for benefit, but the risk of non-engagement can lead to suboptimal outcomes for both parties.

### Title: Authorization and Investment Asymmetric Coordination Game (AS5)

### Tension: Formal Cooperation vs. Opportunistic Behavior

### Matrix:
|                  | Sub-Station Staff: Invests in Capacity | Sub-Station Staff: Withholds Capacity |
|------------------|---------------------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| **Farmer: Makes Formal Request** | (1, 2)                                | (0, 0)                                |
| **Farmer: Makes Informal Request** | (0, 1)                                | (1, 1)                                |

### Justification:
In the Authorization and Investment Asymmetric Coordination Game (AS5), a farmer can request formal or informal access to transformer capacity. If the farmer makes a formal request and the staff invest, the farmer incurs a cost but gets more benefit. If the staff withhold, the farmer incurs a loss. If the farmer makes an informal request and the staff invest, the farmer gains more while the staff bear the cost without the formal fee. This creates a tension between legality and opportunism, where mutual formal cooperation is collectively optimal but staff can gain modestly even under formal cooperation due to the investment burden.

### Title: Groundwater Extraction Prisoner's Dilemma (AS6)

### Tension: Individual Benefit vs. Collective Depletion

### Matrix:
|                  | Farmer B: Restraints Groundwater Extraction | Farmer B: Exceeds Groundwater Extraction |
|------------------|--------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|
| **Farmer A: Restraints Groundwater Extraction** | (2, 2)                                    | (1, 3)                                  |
| **Farmer A: Exceeds Groundwater Extraction** | (3, 1)                                    | (1, 1)                                  |

### Justification:
In the Groundwater Extraction Prisoner's Dilemma (AS6), two farmers drawing from the same aquifer must decide whether to restrain or exceed groundwater extraction. Mutual restraint sustains yields, but unilateral over-extraction offers short-term gain and accelerates depletion. This creates a tension where individual short-term benefit can lead to collective depletion, and mutual restraint is necessary for long-term sustainability but risky due to the temptation of short-term gains.

### Title: Sequential Social-Learning Process (AS2)

### Matrix:
|                  | Farmer B: Adopts Capacitor | Farmer B: Does Not Adopt Capacitor |
|------------------|----------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Farmer A: Adopts Capacitor** | (2, 2)                     | (1, 0)                            |
| **Farmer A: Does Not Adopt Capacitor** | (0, 1)                     | (1, 1)                            |

### Justification:
In the Sequential Social-Learning Process (AS2), a farmer observes a peer's outcome and imitates only if the outcome ranks higher. This creates a path-dependent coordination problem where early failed or isolated adoption can discourage later uptake, while visibly successful coordinated adoption can spread through the social network. This tension arises because the diffusion of adoption is not immediate and relies on the success of coordinated trials.

### Justification:
In the Informal Exchange vs. Formal Compliance (AS7), a farmer and sub-station staff decide whether to engage in informal exchange or formal compliance. If the farmer invests in capacity and the staff invest, both benefit. If the staff withhold, the farmer incurs a loss.
