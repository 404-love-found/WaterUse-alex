### Title: Capacitor Adoption Assurance Game

### Tension: Coordination vs. Free-Rider Incentive

#### 2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix

|                   | Farmer B: No Capacitor | Farmer B: Capacitor |
|-------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| **Farmer A: No Capacitor** | (0, 0)                   | (1, -1)               |
| **Farmer A: Capacitor**     | (-1, 1)                  | (2, 2)               |

#### Justification

The Capacitor Adoption Assurance Game captures the strategic tension between coordination and free-riding. Two farmers share the same transformer and must decide whether to invest in a capacitor. If both farmers invest, the local voltage quality improves, benefiting both. However, if only one farmer invests, the benefits are limited and may not be attributed to the individual investment, making it unattractive to invest alone. This creates a free-rider incentive, where non-investors can benefit from the improved voltage quality without bearing the costs. The ordinal payoffs reflect the collective benefit of mutual cooperation (2, 2), the unilateral investment (1, -1) yielding a net loss for the investor, and the mutual non-investment (0, 0) resulting in no improvement.

### Title: Transformer Capacity Authorization Dilemma

### Tension: Formal Compliance vs. Informal Exchange

#### 2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix

|                   | Staff: Formal Authorization | Staff: Informal Exchange |
|-------------------|-----------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Farmer: Formal Request** | (1, -1)                     | (3, 0)                   |
| **Farmer: Informal Request** | (0, 3)                      | (2, 2)                   |

#### Justification

The Transformer Capacity Authorization Dilemma represents the tension between formal compliance and informal exchange. A farmer can request formal authorization, which involves costs and effort from the sub-station personnel. Alternatively, a farmer can seek informal access, which incurs no formal costs but may lead to tolerance or maintenance neglect. Sub-station personnel can enforce formal rules or tolerate informal requests. The ordinal payoffs reflect the mutual benefits of formal cooperation (3, 0), the unilateral benefit for the farmer in informal exchange (2, 2), and the potential losses for the farmer in formal enforcement (1, -1) or the staff in informal tolerance (0, 3).

### Title: Groundwater Extraction Prisoner's Dilemma

### Tension: Individual Benefit vs. Collective Depletion

#### 2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix

|                   | Farmer B: No Extraction | Farmer B: Extraction |
|-------------------|-------------------------|----------------------|
| **Farmer A: No Extraction** | (2, 2)                   | (0, 4)                |
| **Farmer A: Extraction**     | (4, 0)                   | (1, 1)                |

#### Justification

The Groundwater Extraction Prisoner's Dilemma captures the strategic tension between individual benefit and collective depletion. Two farmers share the same aquifer and must decide whether to extract groundwater for irrigation. If both farmers extract, the aquifer is depleted faster, and both face higher pumping costs in the future. If only one farmer extracts, the other benefits from the short-term gain but risks the long-term depletion. The ordinal payoffs reflect the mutual benefit of mutual restraint (2, 2), the unilateral benefit for the extracting farmer (4, 0), and the mutual loss in extraction (1, 1).
