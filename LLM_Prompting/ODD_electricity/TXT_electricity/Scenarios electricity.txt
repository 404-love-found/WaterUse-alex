ACTION SITUATION 1 (AS1)
"DSM Adoption Coordination — The Core Action Situation"
Paper reference: Section 6.1, Table 2

------------------------------------------------------------------------
Location:
Distribution transformer service area, Andhra Pradesh. Each transformer
serves on average 17 pumpsets. Power quality is a shared resource within
this nested unit: each farmer's equipment choice affects all others.

Players:
  F1 — Farmer 1 (existing grid user sharing a transformer)
  F2 — Farmer 2 (existing grid user sharing the same transformer)

Roles:
  F1: Technology adopter / non-adopter (symmetric with F2)
  F2: Technology adopter / non-adopter (symmetric with F1)

Actions:
  I  = Invest in demand-side measures (capacitor and/or ISI-marked pumpset)
  ~I = Do not invest (continue with existing equipment)

Control Rules:
  - If BOTH invest (I,I): voltage stabilises; equipment damage for ALL
    farmers at the transformer drastically reduced; DSM effective.
  - If ONE invests (I,~I) or (~I,I): voltage remains low; the investing
    farmer's capacitor may fail to start the motor or burn out (low voltage
    condition). Unilateral investment yields NO improvement in power quality
    and may result in net loss for the investor.
  - If NEITHER invests (~I,~I): status quo; continued equipment damage at
    current rates; no cost of investment.
  - Critical physical rule: capacitors only correct power factor if ALL
    connected farmers adopt simultaneously. This coordination requirement
    is unknown to most farmers (empirically confirmed by interviews).

Information:
  - Farmers observe neighbours' visible adoption (capacitor presence,
    pumpset type) but cannot observe simultaneous decision-making.
  - Erroneous sensing: farmers misattribute voltage fluctuations; they
    cannot distinguish their own contribution from the utility's or
    neighbours' load. Almost no farmer was aware of the coordination
    requirement (Section 6.1).
  - Sensing of own equipment performance: available but confounded by
    stochastic failures.

Outcomes:
  - Power quality at transformer level (voltage stability, power factor).
  - Motor and transformer burn-out frequency.
  - Repair costs (mean motor repair: INR 2,693; transformer: INR 621).
  - DSM adoption rate across transformer service area.

Payoffs:
  - Shared power quality improvement (benefit) vs. cost of DSM investment.
  - If both invest: full benefit realised; reduced repair costs for all.
  - If only one invests: investor bears cost, gets zero quality benefit
    (voltage still insufficient), risks equipment damage from failed
    capacitor installation.

Strategic Tension:
  ASSURANCE PROBLEM / STAG HUNT (Section 6.1, citing Sen 1967;
  Harsanyi & Selten 1988; Skyrms 2004).
  "Unlike in a dilemma situation, no farmer has an incentive to deviate
  from the Pareto-superior equilibrium, once reached" (paper, p.153).
  Two Nash equilibria: (I,I) payoff-dominant; (~I,~I) risk-dominant.
  Risk-dominant equilibrium prevails empirically due to:
  (a) unknown coordination requirement (cognitive failure), and
  (b) social learning failure (AS2 link).

Temporal Structure:
  Simultaneous annual decision (beginning of irrigation season).
  Repeated over multiple seasons — but coordination failure is self-
  reinforcing because no coordinated trial has ever been observed.

Relevant Rules:
  Boundary: all farmers connected to the same transformer.
  Choice: invest or not invest (no intermediate options in model).
  Payoff: individual payoff depends on combined strategy profile.
  APERC ordered compulsory capacitor use (2001 tariff order) and
  threatened service discontinuation — partial enforcement created
  some adoption despite Nash prediction of zero adoption.

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 2 from paper — ordinal ranks):

                         F2: I         F2: ~I
         F1: I          2, 2           0, 1
         F1: ~I         1, 0           1, 1

Nash Equilibria (bold in original Table 2):
  (I, I) = (2, 2)    — payoff-dominant
  (~I, ~I) = (1, 1)  — risk-dominant (empirically observed outcome)

ODD+D Compliance:
  COMPLIANT. The assurance structure matches Section 6.1. Payoffs match
  Table 2 exactly. Empirical outcome (10% adoption ≈ low Nash) confirmed
  by econometric analysis cited in paper.

ACTION SITUATION 2 (AS2)
"Social Learning in Sequential Adoption"
Paper reference: Section 6.2, Figure 2

------------------------------------------------------------------------
Location:
Farmer social network — village-level peer observation and kinship ties.

Players:
  F1 — Early experimenter (unilateral adopter)
  F2 — Observer / latecomer (decides whether to imitate)
  T  — Technology (outcome: success or failure, determines move)

Roles:
  F1: Demonstrator (involuntary — experiments based on sequential
      adoption heuristic, not aware of coordination requirement).
  F2: Observer (applies imitation rule: adopt only if F1's outcome
      ranks higher than own current state).
  T:  Outcome move — fails if only one farmer adopts (low voltage).

Actions (F2 only — F1 has already moved):
  Imitate: Adopt capacitor/DSM based on observed peer outcome.
  Wait:    Delay adoption (maintain status quo).

Control Rules (Sequential game tree, Fig. 2):
  Step 1: F1 experiments with capacitor (unilateral investment).
  Step 2: Technology (T) evaluates number of adopters:
          - If only F1 adopts → voltage insufficient → capacitor fails
            (may burn out or prevent motor start). T = FAIL.
  Step 3: F2 observes F1's outcome (FAIL) → F1's rank does NOT improve
          above F2's status quo → F2 does NOT imitate.
  Result: One farmer invests, technology fails, no diffusion occurs.
          Equilibrium: one farmer invests; no observer invests. Stalls.

Information:
  F2 observes F1's adoption outcome (visible adoption + outcome).
  BUT: F2 misattributes the failure reason (does not know that
  simultaneous adoption was required; attributes failure to technology
  deficiency or bad luck).
  "Almost no farmer was aware that a simultaneous installation of
  capacitors is required" (Section 6.1, p.153).

Outcomes:
  - Capacitor diffusion rate across social network.
  - Technology adoption trajectory over time.

Strategic Tension:
  NON-STRATEGIC. AS2 is an inter-situational DIFFUSION LINK (ι), not
  a standalone game. "Only when the coordination problem becomes resolved
  in one instance can social learning potentially catalyse adoption."
  AS2 transforms AS1 from a simultaneous game into a sequential game,
  preventing the payoff-dominant (I,I) equilibrium from being reachable.

Temporal Structure:
  Sequential, year-by-year observation cycle. Social learning is the
  dominant technology adoption strategy for agricultural inputs in the
  study area (consistent with Munshi, 2004; Foster & Rosenzweig, 1995).

Relevant Rules:
  Information rule: observe peer's ranked outcome.
  Position rule: early adopter vs. latecomer.
  Link type: informational (parameter ι in adjacency network).

PAYOFF MATRIX: NONE.
AS2 is a diffusion mechanism described by the sequential game tree
(Fig. 2). Do NOT generate a payoff matrix for AS2. Describe only
as a transition submodel linking AS1 → AS3.

ODD+D Compliance:
  COMPLIANT. AS2 is structurally a sequential learning process, not a
  simultaneous strategic game. The paper explicitly separates AS1 (the
  factual game) and AS2 (how the situation is actually dealt with by
  actors through evolved shared routines). "It is crucial to analytically
  separate them in order to understand the outcomes of the core situation."

ACTION SITUATION 3 (AS3)
"Infrastructure Capacity Provision — Asymmetric Dilemma"
Paper reference: Section 6.3, Table 3

------------------------------------------------------------------------
Location:
Village transformer connection point and sub-station. Transformer
capacity is a shared infrastructure resource; provision requires
formal connection payment to the distribution utility.

Players:
  F1 — Farmer with existing authorised connection (has paid previously).
  F2 — Farmer seeking new or informal connection.

Roles:
  F1: Existing authorised user / potential defector from formal system.
  F2: New applicant / potential free-rider.

Actions:
  A  = Authorise (pay formal connection charge; contribute to transformer
       capacity provision).
  ~A = Not authorise (establish informal/unauthorised connection via
       sub-station ties from AS4/AS5; evade connection charge).

Control Rules:
  - (A,A): both pay; transformer capacity sufficient for both; voltage
    adequate; both in formal system. Collectively optimal.
  - (A,~A): F1 pays and provides capacity; F2 benefits from voltage
    improvement without contributing. F2 gets HIGHEST payoff (3);
    F1 incurs cost for capacity that F2 also uses.
  - (~A,A): reverse — F2 pays; F1 free-rides; F1 gets highest payoff.
  - (~A,~A): neither pays; capacity under-provided; load exceeds transformer
    capacity for many (11% of transformers below average pumpset power);
    voltage drops; both in informal system. Nash equilibrium.
  - Physical mechanism: "The provision of sufficient capacity for every
    additional connection becomes necessary" for DSM (AS1) to work.

Information:
  - Partial. F2 observes F1's connection type (formal/informal visible in
    some cases — "open secret"). Cost-sharing arrangements are opaque.
  - Survey: 37% of farmers considered present capacity sufficient for
    additional pumpset. Estimated 20–30% unauthorised connections.

Outcomes:
  - Transformer capacity level (τ).
  - Voltage reliability for all connected farmers.
  - Equity of cost-sharing for infrastructure provision.
  - Load on transformer (affects power quality and AS1 effectiveness).

Payoffs:
  - Voltage improvement benefit (shared by all connected farmers) vs.
    connection charge cost (borne only by the authorising farmer).
  - If only one authorises: authoriser pays for benefit enjoyed by all.
  - "Most farmers have authorised connections (A) and paid for the
    provision of infrastructure capacity, while some have been able to
    set up an informal arrangement to receive an unauthorised connection (~A)."

Strategic Tension:
  ASYMMETRIC PRISONER'S DILEMMA (Section 6.3).
  "The underlying model structure is a Prisoner's Dilemma with asymmetric
  payoffs, resulting in an asymmetric equilibrium."
  Dominant strategy (~A) for both farmers.
  Nash: (~A,~A) = (1,1) — both defect; capacity under-provided.
  Asymmetry: payoffs differ between (A,~A)=(1,3) and (~A,A)=(3,0).

Temporal Structure:
  Repeated with each new connection request cycle. Long-run: evolved
  institutions (AS4 collusion norms, δ) make informal arrangements
  stable — sustained collective failure.

Relevant Rules:
  Boundary: formal utility authorization requirement (APERC regulation).
  Payoff: infrastructure benefit non-excludable; cost excludable (formal
  payment required only of the authorising party).
  Link to AS4/AS5: collusion ties (δ) are prerequisite for ~A option.
  Link to AS1: (~A,~A) outcome reduces τ → AS1 payoffs degraded.
  Link to AS6: rising γ (pumping cost) increases electrical load,
  worsening capacity constraint.

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 3 from paper — ordinal ranks):

                         F2: A         F2: ~A
         F1: A          2, 2           1, 3
         F1: ~A         3, 0           1, 1

Nash Equilibrium: (~A, ~A) = (1, 1).
Dominant strategy: ~A for both players.
Asymmetric payoffs: (A,~A)=(1,3) ≠ (~A,A)=(3,0).

ODD+D Compliance:
  COMPLIANT. Asymmetric PD structure matches Section 6.3 and Table 3.
  Empirically: "an unknown share of unauthorised connections" — confirmed
  by estimates of 20–30%. Institutional mechanism (AS4 enabling ~A)
  explicitly described in paper.

ACTION SITUATION 4 (AS4)
"Mutual Exchange and Collusion"
Paper reference: Section 6.4, Table 4

------------------------------------------------------------------------
Location:
Sub-station office and surrounding village social network. Interaction
occurs within ongoing kinship or evolved personal relations between
farmers and sub-station ground staff.

Players:
  F  — Farmer (grid user seeking connection or service favour)
  U  — Sub-station personnel (utility ground staff with discretionary power)

Roles:
  F:  Client / exchange initiator.
  U:  Gatekeeper / exchange partner.

Actions:
  E  = Exchange (engage in mutual informal exchange: payment, favour,
       or reciprocal service).
  ~E = Not exchange (formal channel only; no personal exchange).

Control Rules:
  - (E,E): mutual exchange established. F receives faster/better service
    or informal connection access; U receives informal compensation.
    "A network link is established if the Pareto-superior equilibrium of
    mutual exchange is reached" (Section 6.4).
  - (E,~E): F offers exchange, U does not reciprocate. F bears loss
    (wasted offer, still receives formal delays); U saves effort with
    baseline payoff.
  - (~E,E): U initiates, F does not reciprocate. U bears effort cost;
    F receives unilateral benefit. Structurally rare.
  - (~E,~E): neither engages in exchange. Formal channel only; no extra
    benefit for either party. Formal compliance equilibrium.
  - APERC stochastic monitoring: "Ties within social networks can be
    subject to fluctuations on their own and can be strengthened or
    reshaped." High monitoring probability reduces expected value of (E,E).

Information:
  - Both parties know their relationship history (repeated interaction,
    trust-based). Collusion is an 'open secret' — "one farmer openly
    admitted not to have authorised his connection" in a group discussion.
  - APERC monitoring is stochastic: neither party knows current
    monitoring intensity with certainty.

Outcomes:
  - Connection access for F (formal or informal).
  - Service quality and speed.
  - Personal gain for U (informal compensation).
  - Prevalence of collusion networks across the district.

Payoffs:
  - Net benefit from reciprocal exchange vs. risk of APERC detection.
  - Unilateral offer incurs loss for the offering party.
  - Mutual abstention is the formal-compliance equilibrium (0,0).

Strategic Tension:
  COORDINATION GAME with two Nash equilibria (Section 6.4, Table 4).
  "The underlying model has a simple but powerful structure, with several
  equilibria of mutual exchange." Unlike a PD, unilateral engagement
  LOSES for the engaging party — mutual participation is required.
  APERC monitoring shifts equilibrium selection: high monitoring → (0,0);
  strong kinship ties → (1,1).

Temporal Structure:
  Repeated game over multiple irrigation seasons. "Exchange ties are most
  likely established and enforced between those actors who can offer equal
  levels of services or commodities, or a kinship relation exists."
  Trust-building is long-term; collusion once established is self-sustaining.

Relevant Rules:
  Social norm rule: reciprocity norms sustain (E,E) equilibrium.
  APERC enforcement rule: stochastic monitoring probability ι/δ shifts
  equilibrium selection between (E,E) and (~E,~E).
  Link to AS3: (E,E) collusion tie is the institutional precondition
  (δ) enabling the ~A option in AS3 and AS5.

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 4 from paper — ordinal ranks):

                         U: E          U: ~E
         F: E           1, 1           0, 1
         F: ~E          1, 0           0, 0

Nash Equilibria: Two in pure strategies.
  (E, E)   = (1, 1) — Pareto-superior collusion equilibrium.
  (~E, ~E) = (0, 0) — formal compliance equilibrium.

Note on payoff scale: The paper uses binary (0,1) ordinal ranks for AS4,
reflecting the minimal structure of the mutual exchange game. The payoff
of 1 represents the gain from mutual exchange; 0 represents the baseline
(no exchange). These are ORDINAL, not cardinal values.

ODD+D Compliance:
  COMPLIANT. Coordination structure with two equilibria matches Section
  6.4 and Table 4 exactly. The paper notes this game "may be omnipresent
  within some social network ties." The δ parameter (collusion norms)
  links AS4 to AS3 and AS5 as described in Section 6.8 and Fig. 4.

ACTION SITUATION 5 (AS5)
"Authorization and Capacity Investment Dilemma"
Paper reference: Section 6.5, Table 5

------------------------------------------------------------------------
Location:
Sub-station, formal vs. informal connection pathway. The formal procedure
requires application and payment of fixed connection fee to the
distribution company. Sub-station personnel control implementation.

Players:
  F2 — Farmer 2 (from AS3, seeking new connection; the potential
        unauthorised connector).
  U  — Sub-station personnel (controlling connection provision and
        capacity investment).

Roles:
  F2: Applicant (formal or informal request pathway).
  U:  Enforcer / provider (invest in capacity or withhold service).

Actions:
  F2: A  = Authorise (pay formal connection fee; follow official procedure).
      ~A = Not authorise (informal/unauthorised connection via AS4 tie).
  U:  I  = Invest (allocate capacity, provide connection, maintain
            transformer; incurs effort cost).
      ~I = Not invest (delay, deny, or withhold capacity; saves effort).

Control Rules:
  - (A,I): formal + investment. F2 pays fee; U provides capacity; grid
    integrity maintained. Collectively optimal outcome.
  - (A,~I): formal request + withhold. F2 pays fee but U does NOT provide
    capacity (can take several months even after formal authorisation due
    to "reduced ground staff"). F2 bears maximum loss (0); U saves effort.
  - (~A,I): informal + invest. F2 gets connection without fee (best for F2
    = 3); U bears full investment cost without formal compensation (worst
    for U = 0). "In addition to formal payments, informal payments are
    often expected."
  - (~A,~I): informal + withhold. Nash equilibrium. F2 gets nothing but
    avoids fee risk; U saves effort. Both at low baseline (1,1).
  - Physical link to AS3: (~A,~I) Nash outcome reduces transformer capacity
    available to all farmers (capacity not invested → τ degrades → AS1
    payoffs reduced).

Information:
  - Asymmetric: U has better information about monitoring probability
    than F2. U knows current vigilance intensity; F2 does not.
  - F2 knows their own financial constraints and social ties (AS4).

Outcomes:
  - Grid connection provision (or denial).
  - Fee revenue for distribution company.
  - Transformer load and capacity (τ parameter).
  - Service delays (some farmers forced to self-repair transformers
    — five villages reported 1–3 maintenance-related deaths in one year).

Payoffs:
  - F2: benefits from connection vs. cost of formal fee and risk of delay.
  - U: formal compensation (fee-based) vs. effort cost vs. informal
    compensation (from AS4 exchange tie).
  - Collective: (A,I) maximises total payoff but is individually unstable.

Strategic Tension:
  ASYMMETRIC COORDINATION with UNIQUE Nash Equilibrium (Section 6.5).
  "The only equilibrium is then (~A; ~I)." (paper, p.155)
  (~A) is dominant for F2; (~I) is dominant for U.
  "This setting is only feasible if the farmer has the choice to get a
  connection without authorization, which depends on established
  connections as in AS4."
  APERC enforcement can shift payoffs: effective monitoring could make
  (A,I) incentive-compatible, but requires enforcement capacity
  beyond the current adjacency network boundary.

Temporal Structure:
  One-shot per connection application; repeated in the aggregate across
  the district over time. The (~A,~I) equilibrium is self-reinforcing:
  widespread informal access normalises the strategy, further eroding
  formal compliance incentives.

Relevant Rules:
  Boundary: APERC requires formal authorisation. AS4 tie is prerequisite
  for ~A option to exist for F2.
  Payoff: formal fee-based compensation vs. informal arrangement vs.
  withheld service.
  Link to AS3: (~A,~I) Nash → capacity under-provided → τ parameter
  degraded → AS1 effectiveness reduced.

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 5 from paper — ordinal ranks):

                         U: I          U: ~I
         F2: A           2, 1           0, 2
         F2: ~A          3, 0           1, 1

Dominant strategies:
  F2: ~A (3>2 when U plays I; 1>0 when U plays ~I).
  U: ~I (2>1 when F2 plays A; 1>0 when F2 plays ~A).
Unique Nash Equilibrium: (~A, ~I) = (1, 1).
Collectively optimal (but unstable): (A, I) = (2, 1).

ODD+D Compliance:
  COMPLIANT. Unique Nash structure matches Section 6.5 and Table 5
  exactly. The paper confirms this equilibrium explains "the informal
  arrangements possible" and is contingent on AS4 (collusion tie as
  prerequisite). The incentive for informal connection is structurally
  embedded in the payoff ordering, not just anecdotal.

ACTION SITUATION 6 (AS6)
"Groundwater Extraction — Dynamic CPR Dilemma"
Paper reference: Section 6.6, Table 6

------------------------------------------------------------------------
Location:
Shared aquifer beneath the Deccan plateau, accessed via individual
electric tube-well pumpsets. No monitoring of individual pump hours.

Players:
  F1 — Farmer 1 (groundwater extractor)
  F2 — Farmer 2 (groundwater extractor sharing the same aquifer basin)

Roles:
  F1: Groundwater extractor / potential restrainer.
  F2: Groundwater extractor / potential restrainer (symmetric with F1).

Actions:
  E  = Extract (pump at unconstrained rate to meet irrigation demand).
  ~E = Restrain (limit pump hours; conserve groundwater).

Control Rules:
  - (E,E): mutual extraction. Groundwater table drops. Pumping cost γ
    increases as wells must be deepened. Electrical load on grid rises.
    "Initially creates the highest outcome, but with a decreasing
    groundwater level, the costs of pumping (γ) increase and the
    equilibrium falls below the payoffs for restraining" (Section 6.6).
  - (E,~E): F1 extracts, F2 restrains. F1 gains maximum (3); F2 suffers
    lowest payoff (0) — groundwater drops due to F1, F2 still pays
    deepening cost without extracting the benefit.
  - (~E,E): reverse — F2 gains maximum; F1 suffers.
  - (~E,~E): mutual restraint. Aquifer recharges. Pumping costs stable.
    Payoff = (1,1). Initially Pareto-inferior to (E,E); eventually
    Pareto-superior as γ increases.
  - Dynamic mechanism: "Although the actors in AS6 and AS3 are identical,
    both action situations are decisively linked through the physical
    electric load requirements of decreasing groundwater levels."

Information:
  - Farmers observe groundwater depth changes in their own well.
  - Cannot monitor other farmers' pump hours (open-access commons).
  - Cannot observe γ trajectory directly — infer from deepening costs.

Outcomes:
  - Groundwater table depth (Fig. 3 in paper shows declining trend).
  - Pumping energy consumption and costs (γ increases over time).
  - Electrical load on grid (affects transformer capacity τ in AS3).
  - Long-term agricultural yield sustainability.

Payoffs:
  - Short-term extraction gain vs. long-term depletion cost (shared
    externality that neither farmer can individually avoid).
  - Paradox noted in paper: successful coordination in AS1 (efficient
    pumpsets via DSM) could AGGRAVATE groundwater exploitation by
    reducing pumping cost per unit water, increasing total extraction.

Strategic Tension:
  DYNAMIC PRISONER'S DILEMMA — CPR extraction (Section 6.6).
  "A bi-matrix model of the dilemma faced by the farmers extracting
  groundwater." E is the dominant strategy throughout (3>1-γ if γ<2;
  even when γ is large, individual defection from restraint is tempting).
  The dynamic character is captured by the γ parameter in the (E,E) cell.

Temporal Structure:
  Repeated annually (each year's extraction affects next year's γ).
  Long-run dynamic: as γ rises, the extraction Nash (E,E) = (2-γ,2-γ)
  eventually falls below mutual restraint (1,1), but individual
  incentive to defect from ~E persists throughout (E always dominant).

Relevant Rules:
  No formal extraction monitoring (open-access commons — APERC has no
  jurisdiction over groundwater pump hours in this context).
  Physical link to AS3: rising electrical load → worsens capacity
  shortfall → degrades τ → reduces AS1 payoffs (DSM less effective).
  Physical link to AS1: paradox — if AS1 coordination succeeds
  (efficient pumpsets), pumping efficiency increases, potentially
  accelerating AS6 extraction (bidirectional causation, AS3 ↔ AS6).

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 6 from paper — ordinal ranks with parameter γ):

                         F2: E           F2: ~E
         F1: E           2-γ, 2-γ        3, 0
         F1: ~E          0, 3            1, 1

Dynamic behaviour of Nash equilibrium:
  - Short term (γ → 0): (E,E) ≈ (2,2) > (1,1). Extraction dominant.
    Nash = (E,E). Pareto-inferior to... well, ~E is not yet preferred.
  - Long term (γ → 2): (E,E) ≈ (0,0) < (1,1). Mutual restraint
    Pareto-dominates, but individual defection incentive (3>1) still
    makes mutual restraint unstable. Classic intertemporal CPR failure.

ODD+D Compliance:
  COMPLIANT. Dynamic PD structure matches Section 6.6 and Table 6
  exactly. The empirical evidence (Fig. 3: steadily declining bore-well
  depth with well age) confirms the rising γ trajectory. The link
  to AS3 via electrical load is explicitly stated in the paper:
  "decisively linked through the physical electric load requirements."

INTER-SITUATION LINKAGES
(from Section 6.8 and Fig. 4, Kimmich 2013)

AS2 → AS1 (informational link, ι):
  Social learning heuristic transforms AS1 from simultaneous to sequential
  game. Blocks path to payoff-dominant (I,I). "Sequential adoption even
  undermines simultaneous experimentation."

AS3 → AS1 (biophysical link, τ):
  Capacity shortfall from AS3 Nash (~A,~A) reduces transformer capacity.
  When τ is insufficient, DSM cannot function even if coordination in AS1
  is achieved (voltage too low for capacitors to work).

AS4 → AS3/AS5 (institutional link, δ):
  Collusion equilibrium (E,E) in AS4 establishes the institutional
  precondition for the ~A option in AS3 and AS5. Without AS4 tie,
  informal unauthorised connections are not feasible for F2.

AS5 → AS3 (actor + institutional link):
  Nash (~A,~I) in AS5 produces the asymmetric payoff structure in AS3
  by reducing available transformer capacity (non-investment by U).

AS6 → AS3 (biophysical link, γ):
  Declining groundwater table raises pumping cost γ → increases
  electrical load per farm → worsens transformer capacity shortfall
  in AS3 → further degrades τ → reduces AS1 payoffs.

AS3 ↔ AS6 (bidirectional causation):
  Sufficient capacity (AS3) can intensify groundwater exploitation (AS6)
  by enabling more pumping. Successful AS1 coordination (efficient DSM)
  also increases net pumping efficiency, potentially accelerating AS6.

AS1 ↔ AS3 (bidirectional causation):
  Successful AS1 coordination reduces electric load (DSM improve
  efficiency), improving effective capacity in AS3. But AS3 is a
  prerequisite for AS1 to work (τ must be adequate first).
