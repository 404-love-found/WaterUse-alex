ODD+D Protocol — Electricity Governance in Irrigation Systems
Source: Kimmich, C. (2013). Linking action situations: Coordination, conflicts, and evolution
in electricity provision for irrigation in Andhra Pradesh, India.
Ecological Economics, 90, 150–158. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.ecolecon.2013.03.017

========================================================================

I. OVERVIEW

------------------------------------------------------------------------
I.i Purpose
------------------------------------------------------------------------

I.i.a What is the purpose of the study?
To analyse how linked action situations affect electricity governance and
energy efficiency in irrigation systems, using game theory and empirical
data from Andhra Pradesh, India. Specifically, the paper examines why
coordination to improve power quality and energy efficiency persistently
fails, by identifying a network of six adjacent action situations (AS1–AS6)
that co-determine outcomes of the core action situation (AS1).

The paper contributes to the analysis of Networks of Adjacent Action
Situations (NAAS) and derives three theoretical propositions concerning:
(1) types of links between action situations, (2) a boundary criterion
for delimiting relevant adjacent situations, and (3) conditions for
bidirectional causation (interdependency) between linked situations.

I.i.b For whom is the model designed?
For scientists and policymakers interested in resource governance,
institutional analysis, energy policy, and the ecology of games in
social-ecological systems. The study is instrumental (Stake, 2000) and
based on a mixed-method research design (semi-structured interviews,
cross-section survey with N=305, econometric analysis, game theory).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
I.ii Entities, State Variables, and Scales
------------------------------------------------------------------------

I.ii.a What kinds of entities are in the model?

Human agents:
  - Farmers (F): smallholder irrigators using electric pumpsets to access
    groundwater. On average 17 pumpsets are connected per transformer.
  - Sub-station personnel (U): utility ground staff with discretionary
    power over connection authorisations and infrastructure maintenance.
  - Regulators (APERC): Andhra Pradesh Electricity Regulatory Commission —
    sets tariffs and formal governance rules; treated as exogenous.

Institutional entities:
  - Flat-rate electricity tariff policy (persistent for three decades).
  - Formal connection authorisation procedure (fixed connection fee).
  - Informal exchange norms and collusion ties between farmers and staff.
  - Social learning routines for technology adoption.

Physical entities:
  - Electricity grid: sub-stations, distribution transformers (~11 kV),
    pumpsets, capacitors.
  - Groundwater: shared aquifer accessed via tube wells.
  - Demand-side measures (DSM): ISI-marked pumpsets (Bureau of Indian
    Standards), BEE-rated pumpsets (Bureau of Energy Efficiency),
    capacitors (power factor correction devices).

I.ii.b By what attributes are these entities characterised?

Farmers:
  - Location (distance to sub-station).
  - Pumpset type: branded/non-branded, ISI-marked, BEE-rated.
  - Capacitor installation status (only 10% successfully installed
    during the research period).
  - Number of motor burn-outs per year (mean: 1.86, SD: 1.64).
  - Costs for motor repair (mean: INR 2,693; range: 200–8,500).
  - Age of pumpset (mean: 7.21 years).
  - Transformer burn-outs per year (mean: 1.02).
  - Costs for transformer repair (mean: INR 621).
  - Authorisation status of grid connection (authorised vs. unauthorised).
  - Social network ties (kinship, village-level).
  - Budget / indebtedness (many farmers heavily indebted from tube-well
    and pumpset investments).

Sub-station personnel:
  - Discretionary power over formal and informal connection authorisation.
  - Exposure to monitoring/vigilance by utility management.
  - Established exchange ties with farmers (collusion networks).

Electricity grid:
  - Transformer capacity (11% of transformers calculated below average
    pumpset power; only 37% of farmers considered present capacity
    sufficient for an additional pumpset).
  - Voltage stability and power quality.
  - Estimated 20–30% unauthorised connections (as reported by regulators,
    farmers, and electrical engineers).

Groundwater:
  - Table depth (steadily declining in the Deccan plateau case study area).
  - Extraction rate per farm.
  - Pumping energy cost γ (increases as groundwater table drops,
    imposing greater electrical load on the grid).

I.ii.c What are the exogenous factors/drivers?

  - Aquifer recharge rates (dependent on monsoon precipitation, geology
    of hard-rock Deccan plateau aquifers).
  - APERC tariff orders and formal enforcement campaigns (e.g., 2001
    order requiring compulsory capacitor use; related distribution
    campaigns; stochastic vigilance monitoring).
  - National and state-level political economy of electricity subsidisation
    ('vote bank' politics; flat-rate tariff persistence).
  - Market for crops and labour (alternative income sources).
  - Tube-well and pumpset market conditions.

I.ii.d How is space included in the model?
Implicitly through farmer locations relative to the transformer service
area and the sub-station. Proximity to the sub-station affects informal
relationship formation with sub-station personnel. Shared transformer
connections define the physical boundary of the coordination externality
in AS1 and AS3. Groundwater basin extent defines the boundary of
common-pool extraction in AS6.

I.ii.e What are the temporal and spatial resolutions?

Temporal:
  - Irrigation decisions: seasonal (linked to monsoon cropping cycles).
  - Electricity billing: monthly flat-rate.
  - Technology adoption: annual or multi-year cycles.
  - Groundwater depletion: dynamic, multi-year (declining water table
    over two decades in the case study area).
  - Interviews conducted 2009–2011; survey cross-section.

Spatial:
  - Village-level transformer service area (core unit for AS1 and AS3).
  - District-level groundwater basin (AS6).
  - State-level regulatory governance (APERC, exogenous).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
I.iii Process Overview and Scheduling
------------------------------------------------------------------------

I.iii.a What entity does what, and in what order?

The model is not a time-stepped simulation but a set of linked
game-theoretic models. The sequencing is conceptual:

1. Farmers simultaneously decide whether to invest in DSM (capacitors,
   ISI-marked pumpsets) — AS1: Coordination/Assurance.

2. Farmers observe peers' adoption outcomes and apply social learning
   rules (imitate if peer outcome ranks higher than own) — AS2: Social
   Learning (adjacent, non-strategic link).

3. When additional farmers seek grid connections, existing farmers and
   new applicants decide whether to pay for formal transformer capacity
   authorisation or to establish informal (unauthorised) connections —
   AS3: Asymmetric Infrastructure Dilemma.

4. Farmers and sub-station personnel decide whether to engage in mutual
   informal exchange (collusion) — AS4: Mutual Exchange Coordination.

5. Farmers choose formal or informal request pathways; sub-station
   personnel choose whether to invest capacity or withhold service —
   AS5: Authorization and Investment Dilemma.

6. Farmers decide how intensively to extract groundwater from the shared
   aquifer; extraction increases electrical load over time — AS6:
   Groundwater Extraction Dilemma.

Each agent is updated through their own decision process; interactions
are defined by game-theoretic equilibrium logic rather than sequential
turn-taking.

II. DESIGN CONCEPTS

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.i Theoretical and Empirical Background
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.i.a Which general concepts, theories, or hypotheses underlie the model?

The paper draws on three theoretical traditions:

1. Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework (Ostrom, 2005):
   Action situations as the core unit of analysis; working components
   (actors, positions, actions, outcomes, information, payoffs, rules).

2. Networks of Adjacent Action Situations (NAAS) (McGinnis, 2011):
   "An action situation Xi is adjacent to Y if the outcome of Xi directly
   influences the value of one or more of the working components of Y."
   The paper derives three propositions:
   - Link proposition: every adjacent AS must be linked through
     biophysical transactions, information, institutions, or actors.
   - Boundary proposition: boundary delimited by range of influence
     of core actors (at least one actor must be involved in both Y and Xi).
   - Interdependency proposition: causal mechanisms between linked AS
     can be unidirectional or bidirectional.

3. Evolutionary game theory and technology adoption (Axelrod, 1981;
   Skyrms, 2004; Foster & Rosenzweig, 1995):
   Social learning strategies (imitation) are evolutionarily dominant
   for many technologies, but fail in coordination problems because
   sequential adoption undermines simultaneous experimentation.

Game model archetypes used:
  - Assurance/Stag Hunt (AS1): two Nash equilibria in pure strategies;
    payoff-dominant (I,I) vs. risk-dominant (~I,~I).
  - Asymmetric Prisoner's Dilemma (AS3): dominant strategy to defect
    (~A); asymmetric payoffs due to infrastructure externality.
  - Mutual Exchange Coordination (AS4): two equilibria (E,E) and (~E,~E);
    collusion vs. formal compliance.
  - Authorization-Investment Dilemma (AS5): unique Nash equilibrium
    (~A,~I); staff's withholding is dominant.
  - Dynamic CPR Dilemma (AS6): extraction (E) initially dominant, but
    rising pumping cost γ shifts equilibrium over time.

II.i.b On what assumptions are agents' decisions based?

  - Bounded rationality (Simon, 1957): agents use heuristics, social
    learning, and experiential rules rather than full optimisation.
  - Ordinal preferences: payoff matrices use ordinal ranks (0, 1, 2, 3)
    rather than cardinal utilities.
  - No adoption of capacitors without understanding the coordination
    requirement (farmers rarely aware that simultaneous installation
    is necessary).
  - Collusion ties are contingent on kinship or evolved personal
    relations with sub-station personnel.
  - APERC monitoring is stochastic (random audit probability).

II.i.c Why are certain decision models chosen?
The game models are derived from qualitative and quantitative empirical
evidence (interviews, survey, econometric analysis). Each game archetype
is chosen to match the observed institutional and physical structure of
the respective action situation:
  - AS1 is an assurance problem (not a PD) because no farmer has an
    incentive to deviate from the high equilibrium once reached (a
    standard-approved pumpset reduces individual equipment damage too).
  - AS3 is an asymmetric PD because the authorising farmer bears the
    full cost of additional transformer capacity, while all farmers
    benefit from the voltage improvement.
  - AS4 is a coordination game because mutual exchange is required;
    unilateral engagement yields a loss for the initiating party.
  - AS5's Nash equilibrium (~A,~I) explains the observed persistence of
    unauthorised connections and under-provision by staff.
  - AS6 is dynamic because the pumping cost parameter γ increases over
    time as the groundwater table drops.

II.i.d Where do empirical data come from?
  - Semi-structured interviews (2009–2011): farmers, repair workshop
    owners, pumpset manufacturers, local retailers, utility managers,
    sub-station staff, village revenue officers, elected village heads,
    APERC members.
  - Cross-section survey: N=305, stratified random sample.
  - APERC tariff orders (2001, 2005, 2010) and public hearing transcripts.
  - Bore-well depth records (Fig. 3 in paper: depth vs. age of bore wells).
  - State-level statistics: 36% of all final electric energy in Andhra
    Pradesh used in agriculture (2007); >2.5 million pumpsets on grid (2008).

II.i.e At what level of aggregation were data available?
  - Individual farmer level (survey variables: pumpset type, burn-outs,
    repair costs, capacitor status, authorisation status).
  - Village level (transformer burn-outs, capacity estimates,
    unauthorised connection prevalence).
  - District/state level (groundwater depth trends, energy statistics).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.ii Individual Decision-Making
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.ii.a What are the subjects and objects of decision-making?

AS1: Each farmer decides whether to invest (I) in DSM (capacitor,
     ISI-marked pumpset) or not (~I). Decisions are simultaneous.
     Object: shared power quality at the transformer level.

AS2: Farmers observe peers' adoption outcomes and decide whether to
     imitate. Object: technology adoption strategy.

AS3: Each farmer decides whether to authorise (A) or not (~A) their
     connection. Object: transformer capacity provision.

AS4: Farmer and sub-station staff each decide whether to engage in
     mutual exchange (E) or not (~E). Object: informal access and
     favours within an ongoing social relation.

AS5: Farmer decides formal (A) or informal (~A) request;
     Sub-station personnel decides invest (I) or withhold (~I).
     Object: grid connection and capacity provision.

AS6: Each farmer decides whether to extract (E) or restrain (~E)
     groundwater pumping. Object: shared aquifer level.

II.ii.b What is the basic rationality behind agent decision-making?
Farmers pursue satisfactory yields and seek to minimise costs under
bounded rationality, using heuristics informed by past experience and
peer observation. Utility staff balance formal compliance and informal
reciprocity, seeking stable exchange relationships and personal gain.
Agents are not assumed to have full information about game structure
(e.g., most farmers do not know coordination requirement of AS1).

II.ii.c How do agents make their decisions?
  - Farmers use social learning (imitation of successful peers) as the
    dominant adoption strategy — but this fails in coordination problems.
  - In AS3 and AS5, farmers exploit informal arrangements via established
    collusion ties (AS4) where available.
  - Sub-station personnel respond conditionally to oversight intensity
    and established exchange relations.
  - In AS6, extraction decisions are driven by immediate water need and
    absence of monitoring of individual pump hours.

II.ii.d Do agents adapt their behaviour to changing state variables?
Yes. As groundwater tables drop (AS6), pumping costs increase, raising
electrical load and making transformer capacity (AS3) even more scarce.
Farmers adapt DSM investment based on observed equipment damage frequency.
Staff adapt enforcement effort to perceived monitoring intensity by APERC.

II.ii.e Do social norms or cultural values play a role?
Yes. Norms of solidarity among farmers (supporting neighbours who cannot
afford connection charges) and norms of reciprocity between farmers and
sub-station staff shape AS3 and AS4 outcomes. Collusion is an 'open
secret' in many villages; kinship and evolved personal ties sustain both
equilibria in AS4.

II.ii.f Do spatial aspects matter?
Yes. Transformer service area determines which farmers share power quality
externalities (AS1) and transformer capacity (AS3). Distance to sub-station
influences informal relation formation with staff (AS4, AS5).

II.ii.g Do temporal aspects play a role?
Yes. Repeated interaction sustains collusion equilibrium in AS4.
Groundwater depletion in AS6 is inherently dynamic: as γ increases over
years, the extraction equilibrium (E,E) eventually yields lower payoffs
than mutual restraint, but individual defection incentive remains.
Memory of past grid failures and equipment damage shapes DSM investment.

II.ii.h How is uncertainty included in decision rules?
  - Farmers cannot know others' simultaneous choices (AS1 coordination
    requirement unknown to most).
  - Farmers cannot observe others' pump hours (AS6: open-access commons).
  - APERC monitoring is stochastic: uncertain detection probability
    alters expected payoffs in AS4 and AS5 collusion strategies.
  - Voltage fluctuations and equipment damage are stochastic, making
    learning to coordinate (AS2) difficult.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.iii Learning
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.iii.a Is individual learning included?
Yes — social learning (AS2): each farmer observes peers' adoption
outcomes and imitates only if the peer's outcome ranks above their own.
This is the empirically dominant adoption strategy for agricultural
technologies in the study area (consistent with Munshi, 2004), but it
fails in coordination problems because sequential adoption prevents
simultaneous experimentation.

II.iii.b Is collective learning implemented?
No explicit collective learning. Norms of solidarity and collusion emerge
from repeated interactions rather than deliberate collective rule-making.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.iv Individual Sensing
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.iv.a What state variables do individuals sense?
Farmers directly observe:
  - Voltage levels and power quality at their transformer connection.
  - Frequency and cost of motor and transformer burn-outs.
  - Peers' visible DSM adoption (capacitor, pumpset type).
  - Groundwater depth in their bore well.
  - Electricity service delays (transformer repair wait times).

Sensing is erroneous:
  - Farmers misattribute voltage fluctuations (cannot distinguish own
    contribution vs. utility's responsibility vs. neighbours' load).
  - Farmers misattribute capacitor performance (if one farmer installs
    without coordination, the capacitor may prevent motor start or
    burn out due to low voltage, leading to negative inference).
  - Almost no farmer was aware that simultaneous installation by all
    connected farmers is required for capacitors to work (Section 6.1).

II.iv.b What variables of other individuals can an individual perceive?
  - Visible DSM adoption by neighbours (capacitor presence, pumpset type).
  - Authorisation/unauthorisation status of connections (in some cases
    common knowledge, e.g., one farmer openly admitted unauthorised
    connection in a group discussion with an electrical engineer).
  - Sub-station personnel can observe which farmers have formal vs.
    informal connections.

II.iv.c What is the spatial scale of sensing?
  - Farmers: local (own transformer service area and immediate neighbours;
    bore-well depth in own and adjacent plots).
  - Extended local: social network peers within the village (kinship ties).
  - Sub-station personnel: transformer service area and sub-station records.

II.iv.d Are sensing mechanisms modelled explicitly?
Not formally. AS2 models the informational link between AS1 and AS3 as
a sequential game tree (Fig. 2 in paper): technology makes a move based
on the number of adopters. The key informational constraint is that no
farmer has experience of coordinated simultaneous adoption — making the
payoff-dominant equilibrium of AS1 unobservable in practice.

II.iv.e Are costs for cognition and information gathering included?
Not modelled explicitly. Bounded rationality is assumed qualitatively;
the cost of understanding the coordination requirement is treated as
prohibitively high for most farmers (evidenced by survey results).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.v Individual Prediction
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.v.a Which data do agents use to predict future conditions?
  - Past electricity service quality and equipment damage frequency.
  - Observed peer adoption outcomes (AS2 social learning).
  - Established exchange relationships with sub-station personnel.
  - Historical groundwater depth trends (bore-well depth data).

II.v.b What internal models do agents use?
No formal predictive models. Farmers apply heuristics and evolutionary
strategies (imitation, trial-and-error). Sub-station personnel apply
conditional rules based on perceived monitoring risk and existing ties.

II.v.c Might agents be erroneous in prediction?
Yes — systematically. The core prediction error is the failure to
recognise the coordination requirement: farmers experiment individually
with capacitors, experience failure (due to unilateral installation),
and infer that DSM are ineffective. This blocks the social learning
path to the payoff-dominant equilibrium (AS2 link to AS1).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.vi Interaction
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.vi.a Are interactions direct or indirect?
Both:
  - Direct: farmer–farmer interaction via shared transformer externality
    (AS1, AS3, AS6); farmer–staff interaction (AS4, AS5).
  - Indirect: farmers interact with the utility and regulatory commission
    through formal institutional rules and enforcement mechanisms.

II.vi.b On what do interactions depend?
  - Shared infrastructure (transformer, aquifer).
  - Social network ties (kinship, trust, established exchange norms).
  - Institutional enforcement (APERC monitoring intensity, stochastic).

II.vi.c How are communications represented?
Implicitly through observed behaviour (visible adoption, informal
discussions, negotiation with staff). No explicit communication protocol
is modelled. Collusion is communicated through evolved trust relations.

II.vi.d If a coordination network exists, how does it affect behaviour?
The adjacency network (Fig. 4 in paper) links all six action situations.
Key linking parameters:
  - τ (transformer capacity): capacity shortfall from AS3 reduces payoffs
    in AS1 (insufficient capacity prevents DSM from working).
  - γ (pumping cost): rising groundwater extraction costs in AS6 increase
    electrical load, reducing capacity in AS3.
  - δ (social norms/exchange ties): collusion equilibrium in AS4 enables
    informal connections in AS5, creating the asymmetric payoff in AS3.
  - ι (information/cognitive constraint): social learning heuristic in
    AS2 transforms AS1 into a sequential game, blocking coordination.

The network is emergent from kinship ties, repeated interactions, and
evolved norms — not imposed by the modeller.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.vii Collectives
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.vii.a Do individuals form or belong to aggregations?
Yes. Farmers belong to:
  - Transformer service groups (defined by shared physical infrastructure).
  - Village social networks (kinship, solidarity norms).
  - Informal collusion groups with sub-station personnel.

These aggregations are emergent from location, kinship ties, and
institutional arrangements. No explicit collective agent type is modelled.

II.vii.b How are collectives represented?
Implicitly through shared infrastructure externalities, coordinated or
collusive behaviour, and village-level norms. The paper notes the role
of coalitions and asymmetries of power (Knight, 1992) in norm emergence.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.viii Heterogeneity
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.viii.a Are agents heterogeneous?
Yes. Farmers differ in:
  - Pumpset type (branded 67%; ISI-marked 37%; BEE-rated 6%).
  - Capacitor installation status (10% successfully installed).
  - Motor burn-out frequency and repair costs.
  - Authorisation status (estimated 20–30% unauthorised connections).
  - Groundwater depth at location.
  - Financial resources and indebtedness.
  - Strength of social network ties with sub-station personnel.

Sub-station personnel differ in:
  - Exposure to APERC vigilance monitoring.
  - Strength of established collusion ties with local farmers.

II.viii.b Are agents heterogeneous in decision-making?
Yes. Decision-making differs based on:
  - Access to informal exchange ties (determines AS4/AS5 options).
  - Financial capacity to pay formal connection charges (affects AS3).
  - Prior experience with DSM failure or success (affects AS1/AS2).
  - Perceived monitoring risk (affects AS4/AS5 collusion decisions).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.ix Stochasticity
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.ix.a What processes are modelled as random?
  - Transformer failures (stochastic; affected by load and voltage quality).
  - Motor burn-outs (stochastic; Poisson-like, mean 1.86/year).
  - APERC vigilance monitoring (stochastic audit probability; alters
    expected payoffs in AS4 and AS5).
  - Groundwater depletion is dynamic but treated as parametric (γ
    increases deterministically in the model, but actual recharge is
    stochastic depending on monsoon rainfall).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
II.x Observation
------------------------------------------------------------------------

II.x.a What data are collected for testing and understanding?
  - Annual DSM adoption rates (capacitors, ISI-marked and BEE-rated
    pumpsets) — collected via survey.
  - Frequency of motor and transformer burn-outs per year.
  - Proportion of authorised vs. unauthorised connections per transformer.
  - Groundwater depth (bore-well depth vs. age, Fig. 3 in paper).
  - Changes in pumping costs over time.
  - Informal payment prevalence in connection authorisation.

II.x.b What key results emerge?
  - Persistent low-equilibrium adoption of DSM (risk-dominant Nash in AS1):
    only 10% capacitor adoption despite APERC orders and campaigns.
  - Persistence of unauthorised connections and under-provision of
    transformer capacity (Nash in AS3: both farmers prefer ~A).
  - Collusion equilibrium between farmers and staff (AS4/AS5) sustains
    informal access and undermines formal governance.
  - Declining groundwater tables (AS6) increase electrical load,
    further constraining capacity provision (AS3) and coordination (AS1).
  - Five villages reported 1–3 maintenance-related farmer deaths within
    one year from self-repair of transformers (due to service delays).

III. DETAILS

------------------------------------------------------------------------
III.i Implementation Details
------------------------------------------------------------------------

III.i.a How is the model implemented?
Conceptually: a set of six interconnected game-theoretic bi-matrix models
(AS1–AS6), linked through soft parameters (τ, δ, ι, γ) as depicted in
Fig. 4 (adjacency network diagram). Each game describes strategic choices
and ordinal payoff rankings derived from empirical evidence.

No simulation platform is used. The model is analytical (equilibrium
derivation) rather than agent-based simulation. The paper notes that
"formal modelling of nested and interconnected games is primarily limited
to sequentially connected games" and that this gap "remains to be developed"
(Dutton et al., 2012, cited in paper Section 7).

III.i.b Is the model accessible?
Can be made available upon request (stated in the paper).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
III.ii Initialization
------------------------------------------------------------------------

III.ii.a What is the initial state of the model world?
  - Existing transformer infrastructure across agricultural villages.
  - Flat-rate electricity tariff in place.
  - Mix of authorised and unauthorised connections (~20–30% unauthorised).
  - Low DSM adoption (10% capacitor installation).
  - Established collusion networks between farmers and staff.
  - Declining groundwater table (multi-decade trend).

III.ii.b Is initialisation always the same?
No. Varies across:
  - Regions with different social network structures and collusion norms.
  - Villages with different transformer capacity adequacy.
  - Groundwater basin conditions (hard-rock vs. alluvial aquifers).

III.ii.c Are initial values based on data?
Yes. Based on cross-section survey (N=305), APERC reports, bore-well
depth records, and Andhra Pradesh state energy statistics.

------------------------------------------------------------------------
III.iii Input Data
------------------------------------------------------------------------

III.iii.a Does the model use external data inputs?
No dynamic external data files. Empirical inputs are used to:
  - Calibrate ordinal payoff ranks in each game matrix.
  - Estimate the parameter γ (pumping cost as function of depth).
  - Establish transformer capacity adequacy thresholds (survey data).
  - Derive social network tie intensity from interview evidence.

Groundwater decline and infrastructure deterioration are generated
endogenously within the game logic (AS6 feeds AS3).

------------------------------------------------------------------------
III.iv Submodels
------------------------------------------------------------------------

III.iv.a What are the submodels (Action Situations)?

AS1 — Coordination/Assurance (Core Action Situation)
  Agents: Two farmers (F1, F2) sharing a distribution transformer.
  Choices: Invest in DSM (I) vs. not invest (~I).
  Structure: Assurance/Stag Hunt — two Nash equilibria.
  (I,I): payoff-dominant (ordinal rank 2,2)
  (~I,~I): risk-dominant (ordinal rank 1,1)
  (I,~I) or (~I,I): unilateral investor bears cost, no quality gain (0,1) / (1,0)
  Empirical outcome: low Nash equilibrium (~I,~I) prevails; only 10%
  adoption despite legal orders.

AS2 — Social Learning in Technology Adoption (Adjacent, Non-Strategic)
  Agents: Farmer A (early adopter) and Farmer B (observer).
  Structure: Sequential game tree (Fig. 2). Technology (T) makes a move
  based on number of adopters. B imitates A only if A's outcome ranks
  higher. Since sequential adoption always leads to unilateral
  installation (AS1 low equilibrium), B observes failure and does not
  imitate. Diffusion stalls. This is an informational link, not a
  standalone strategic game.

AS3 — Infrastructure Capacity Provision (Adjacent, Asymmetric PD)
  Agents: Two farmers (F1, F2) — F1 has authorised connection; F2 seeks
  to connect.
  Choices: Authorise (A) vs. not authorise (~A).
  Structure: Asymmetric Prisoner's Dilemma.
  (~A) is dominant for both: (~A) gains more regardless of other's choice.
  Nash: (~A,~A) = (1,1) — both defect; capacity under-provided.
  Link to AS1: insufficient capacity (τ) reduces AS1 payoffs.
  Link to AS4/AS5: collusion norms (δ) enable unauthorised connections.

AS4 — Mutual Exchange and Collusion (Adjacent, Coordination Game)
  Agents: Farmer (F) and sub-station personnel (U).
  Choices: Exchange (E) vs. not exchange (~E).
  Structure: Coordination game with two Nash equilibria.
  (E,E) = (1,1): mutual exchange — Pareto-superior, collusion equilibrium.
  (~E,~E) = (0,0): formal channel — no extra benefit, formal compliance.
  Unilateral offer: offerer bears loss (0,1) or (1,0).
  Stochastic APERC monitoring shifts expected value of (E,E) downward.

AS5 — Authorization and Capacity Investment (Adjacent, Unique Nash)
  Agents: Farmer F2 (from AS3) and sub-station personnel (U).
  Choices: Farmer: Authorise (A) vs. not (~A);
           Staff: Invest in capacity (I) vs. not (~I).
  Structure: Asymmetric coordination dilemma.
  Nash: (~A,~I) = (1,1) — unique equilibrium.
  (~A) is dominant for farmer (3>2; 1≥1).
  (~I) is dominant for staff (2>1; 1>0).
  Collectively optimal: (A,I) = (2,1) — but individually unstable.
  Link to AS3: (~A,~I) outcome creates the asymmetric payoff in AS3
  by reducing available transformer capacity.

AS6 — Groundwater Extraction (Adjacent, Dynamic CPR Dilemma)
  Agents: Two farmers (F1, F2) sharing an aquifer.
  Choices: Extract (E) vs. restrain (~E).
  Structure: Dynamic Prisoner's Dilemma with cost parameter γ.
  (E,E) = (2-γ, 2-γ): mutual extraction — initially highest individual
  payoff, but γ increases as groundwater table drops, eventually making
  (E,E) < (1,1) = (~E,~E).
  (~E,E) = (0,3): restrainer loses; extractor gains most.
  (E,~E) = (3,0): extractor gains most.
  (~E,~E) = (1,1): mutual restraint — stable long-term but initially
  Pareto-inferior.
  Link to AS3: rising γ increases electrical load → worsens AS3
  capacity adequacy → reduces AS1 payoffs via τ.

III.iv.b What are the model parameters?
  - τ: transformer capacity parameter; links AS3 → AS1.
  - δ: social norms/collusion network strength; links AS4 → AS3/AS5.
  - ι: information/cognitive constraint; links AS2 → AS1 (transforms
    AS1 into sequential game, blocking high equilibrium).
  - γ: groundwater pumping cost (normalised); links AS6 → AS3 → AS1.

All parameters are ordinal (rank-based), not cardinal utility values.
Reference values are normalised ordinal ranks as in the paper's tables.

III.iv.c How were submodels designed and tested?
Derived from field interviews, APERC public hearing transcripts, survey
data, and secondary literature on electricity governance and groundwater
use in Andhra Pradesh. Payoff structures were parameterised based on
observed incentives, infrastructure conditions, and reported behaviours,
then tested for plausibility and equilibrium consistency against empirical
outcomes. Econometric analysis (Kimmich, in press) confirmed the low-Nash
equilibrium prediction for AS1.
