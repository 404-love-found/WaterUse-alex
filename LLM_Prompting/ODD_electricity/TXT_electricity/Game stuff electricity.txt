AS1 — THE CORE ACTION SITUATION: COORDINATION / ASSURANCE (Section 6.1)
Game type: Assurance Problem / Stag Hunt

CONTEXT (from paper):
Power quality is a shared resource within each transformer service area
(on average 17 pumpsets per transformer). Each farmer's pumpset choice
affects all others. Installing a standard-approved pumpset (ISI-marked)
or capacitor improves power quality collectively but requires ALL farmers
to invest simultaneously. If only one farmer invests, voltage remains low,
and their capacitor may even prevent motor start or burn out — creating
false negative inference. Economists confirmed (AS1 econometric analysis):
"under the given conditions, the rational strategy is not to adopt any DSM."

STRATEGIC TRIGGER:
Two farmers (F1, F2) sharing a transformer SIMULTANEOUSLY decide:
  I  = invest in DSM (capacitor / ISI-marked pumpset)
  ~I = do not invest

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 2 — ordinal ranks):

                AS1: F2
                 I        ~I
       F1  I   2, 2      0, 1
          ~I   1, 0      1, 1

PAYOFF INTERPRETATION:
  (I, I)   = 2, 2  → Both invest: Pareto-optimal. Shared power quality
                      improvement. Equipment damage and repair costs
                      drastically reduced. PAYOFF-DOMINANT Nash equilibrium.
  (~I, ~I) = 1, 1  → Neither invests: status quo. No improvement, but
                      no unilateral cost either. RISK-DOMINANT Nash equilibrium.
                      This is the EMPIRICALLY OBSERVED outcome (10% adoption).
  (I, ~I)  = 0, 1  → F1 invests alone: F1 bears cost, voltage stays low,
                      capacitor may fail or burn out. F2 free-rides passively.
  (~I, I)  = 1, 0  → F2 invests alone: F2 bears the loss, F1 benefits
                      slightly from no coordination cost.

NASH EQUILIBRIA: Two in pure strategies.
  1. (I, I) = (2, 2)  — payoff-dominant, Pareto-superior
  2. (~I, ~I) = (1, 1) — risk-dominant, empirically prevailing

GAME LOGIC RULES:
  ❗ This is an ASSURANCE PROBLEM, NOT a Prisoner's Dilemma.
     No farmer has an incentive to deviate from (I,I) ONCE REACHED
     (a standard pumpset reduces individual damage regardless of others).
  ❗ The risk-dominant equilibrium prevails because:
     (a) farmers do not know the coordination requirement (most unaware
         that simultaneous installation is necessary), and
     (b) AS2 (social learning) prevents coordinated experimentation.
  ❗ Do NOT model this as a PD. Unilateral defection is NOT advantaged
     in the way a PD requires — the payoff difference is informational
     and coordination-failure-driven.
  ❗ Linking parameters: τ (transformer capacity from AS3) reduces the
     value of (I,I) if capacity is insufficient — DSM cannot work when
     voltage is structurally too low.

AS2 — SOCIAL LEARNING IN TECHNOLOGY ADOPTION (Section 6.2)
Game type: NON-STRATEGIC LINK — Diffusion Mechanism

CONTEXT (from paper):
"Farmers resorted to a common practice in technology adoption: Only
selected farmers experimented with the use of a capacitor, and their
neighbours and peer network adapted to their experiences."
Since no farmer has experience of coordinated simultaneous adoption,
the sequential learning strategy always observes unilateral failure
(AS1 low equilibrium) and does NOT imitate.

STRUCTURE: Sequential game tree (Fig. 2 in paper)
  - F1 moves first (experiments with capacitor).
  - Technology (T) moves: if only F1 adopts → technology fails.
  - F2 observes F1's outcome: failure → F2 does NOT adopt.
  - Equilibrium of AS2: one farmer invests, technology fails,
    no observer invests. Diffusion stalls permanently.

INFORMATIONAL LINK (ι):
  AS2 transforms AS1 from a simultaneous game into a sequential game,
  blocking the path to the payoff-dominant (I,I) equilibrium.
  "Sequential adoption even undermines simultaneous experimentation
  and the emergence of successful variation."

GAME LOGIC RULES:
  ❗ AS2 is a DIFFUSION MECHANISM, not a standalone game.
     Do NOT generate a payoff matrix for AS2.
  ❗ AS2 is the link between AS1 and AS3 via the parameter ι.
  ❗ The bottleneck: if no coordinated successful trial occurs,
     no beneficial experience is possible, and no imitation follows.
  ❗ Policy implication from paper: a demonstration project showing
     coordinated investment could shift social learning trajectory,
     with impact "far beyond the demonstration project itself."

AS3 — INFRASTRUCTURE CAPACITY PROVISION (Section 6.3)
Game type: Asymmetric Prisoner's Dilemma

CONTEXT (from paper):
Providing additional transformer capacity for each new connection requires
a one-time connection payment. Farmers who have already paid (authorised
connections) have contributed to capacity; new farmers can evade this
payment via informal arrangements with sub-station staff (AS4/AS5).
Estimated 20–30% of connections are unauthorised. For 11% of transformers,
calculated capacity is below average pumpset power (survey data).
"An additional farmer can evade contributing to that capacity."

STRATEGIC TRIGGER:
Two farmers (F1 has authorised; F2 seeks to connect) SIMULTANEOUSLY decide:
  A  = authorise (pay formal connection charge, contribute to capacity)
  ~A = not authorise (informal/unauthorised connection via AS4/AS5 ties)

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 3 — ordinal ranks):

                AS3: F2
                 A        ~A
       F1  A   2, 2      1, 3
          ~A   3, 0      1, 1

PAYOFF INTERPRETATION:
  (A, A)   = 2, 2  → Both authorise: sufficient capacity, voltage adequate
                      for both. Collectively optimal but individually tempting
                      to deviate.
  (~A, ~A) = 1, 1  → Both unauthorised: low capacity, low voltage, suboptimal
                      for both. NASH EQUILIBRIUM.
  (A, ~A)  = 1, 3  → F1 pays and provides capacity; F2 free-rides and gets
                      HIGHEST payoff (3) without contribution cost.
  (~A, A)  = 3, 0  → F1 free-rides (gets highest payoff); F2 bears full cost
                      with lowest payoff (0).

DOMINANT STRATEGY: ~A for BOTH farmers (3>2 and 1≥1; 3>2 and 1≥1).
NASH EQUILIBRIUM: (~A, ~A) = (1, 1).

ASYMMETRY: Whoever authorises first bears full cost; the other gains more.
Payoffs are asymmetric between (A,~A) = (1,3) and (~A,A) = (3,0).

GAME LOGIC RULES:
  ❗ This is an ASYMMETRIC PD. Both players have dominant strategy ~A.
  ❗ Asymmetry comes from who authorises first, not spatial position.
  ❗ Do NOT make payoffs symmetric. (A,~A) ≠ (~A,A) in the paper.
  ❗ Linking parameters:
     - δ (social norms from AS4): collusion equilibrium enables ~A option.
       Without AS4 tie, ~A option may not be available to F2.
     - τ (capacity shortfall): (~A,~A) outcome reduces τ, reducing AS1
       payoffs (DSM cannot work at insufficient voltage).
     - AS6 (γ): rising pumping costs increase load, worsening capacity
       constraint and lowering effective capacity for all farmers.

AS4 — MUTUAL EXCHANGE AND COLLUSION (Section 6.4)
Game type: Coordination Game (two Nash equilibria)

CONTEXT (from paper):
"The established relations between farmers and sub-station personnel,
and also solidarity among farmers with those who cannot afford the
connection charge, play a role in shaping capacity provision in AS3."
"Obtaining access without authorization is an 'open secret' in many cases."
Kinship and evolved personal relations create exchange ties.
"A network link is established if the Pareto-superior equilibrium of
mutual exchange is reached." APERC vigilance monitoring is stochastic and
alters expected payoff of the (E,E) cell.

STRATEGIC TRIGGER:
Farmer (F) and sub-station personnel (U) SIMULTANEOUSLY decide:
  E  = exchange (engage in mutual informal exchange — payment, favour)
  ~E = not exchange (formal channel only)

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 4 — ordinal ranks):

                AS4: U
                 E        ~E
       F   E   1, 1      0, 1
          ~E   1, 0      0, 0

PAYOFF INTERPRETATION:
  (E, E)   = 1, 1  → Mutual exchange: both gain from informal arrangement.
                      F gets faster/better service; U receives informal
                      compensation. PARETO-SUPERIOR Nash equilibrium.
                      Collusion equilibrium.
  (~E, ~E) = 0, 0  → No exchange: formal channel only. No extra benefit.
                      FORMAL COMPLIANCE Nash equilibrium.
  (E, ~E)  = 0, 1  → F engages, U abstains: F bears loss (wasted offer,
                      still gets formal delays); U saves effort with no cost.
  (~E, E)  = 1, 0  → U engages, F abstains: U bears effort cost; F gets
                      benefit without reciprocation (rare structurally).

NASH EQUILIBRIA: Two in pure strategies.
  1. (E, E) = (1, 1)   — Pareto-superior, collusion equilibrium
  2. (~E, ~E) = (0, 0) — formal compliance equilibrium

GAME LOGIC RULES:
  ❗ This is a COORDINATION / MUTUAL EXCHANGE game — NOT a PD.
     Unilateral engagement LOSSES for the engaging party.
  ❗ Two Nash equilibria: equilibrium selection depends on:
     - Strength of social/kinship ties (parameter δ).
     - APERC monitoring intensity (stochastic): high monitoring reduces
       expected value of (E,E) below (0,0), pushing toward formal equilibrium.
  ❗ This AS enables the ~A option in AS3 by establishing the
     institutional precondition for informal connections.
  ❗ The payoff values in the paper (0,1 scale) are minimal ordinal ranks;
     in practice, the informal compensation can be substantial.

AS5 — AUTHORIZATION AND CAPACITY INVESTMENT (Section 6.5)
Game type: Asymmetric Coordination with Unique Nash Equilibrium

CONTEXT (from paper):
"The formal procedure of receiving an electricity connection is to apply
and then pay a fixed connection fee to the distribution company."
"However, the sub-station personnel can exert discretionary agency power."
"In addition to formal payments, informal payments are often expected."
Sub-station personnel can delay service even after authorisation.
"This setting is only feasible if the farmer has the choice to get a
connection without authorization, which depends on established connections
as in AS4." Without AS4 collusion tie, the (~A) option for the farmer
does not exist.

STRATEGIC TRIGGER:
Farmer F2 (from AS3) chooses: Authorise (A) vs. not (~A).
Sub-station personnel U chooses: Invest in capacity (I) vs. not (~I).

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 5 — ordinal ranks):

                AS5: U
                 I        ~I
       F2  A   2, 1      0, 2
          ~A   3, 0      1, 1

PAYOFF INTERPRETATION:
  (A, I)   = 2, 1  → Formal + investment: optimal collective outcome.
                      F2 pays fee and gets connection; U invests effort
                      and receives formal compensation. Grid integrity maintained.
  (A, ~I)  = 0, 2  → Formal request + withhold: F2 pays fee, gets nothing
                      (capacity not provided despite authorisation); U saves
                      effort AND avoids formal penalty. F2 bears maximum loss.
  (~A, I)  = 3, 0  → Informal + invest: F2 gets connection without fee
                      (best outcome for F2 = 3); U bears full investment
                      cost without formal compensation (worst for U = 0).
  (~A, ~I) = 1, 1  → Informal + withhold: F2 gets nothing (uses diesel or
                      delays); U saves effort. This is the Nash equilibrium.

DOMINANT STRATEGY ANALYSIS:
  F2: ~A dominates A (3>2 when U plays I; 1>0 when U plays ~I).
  U: ~I dominates I (2>1 when F plays A; 1>0 when F plays ~A).
UNIQUE NASH EQUILIBRIUM: (~A, ~I) = (1, 1).

GAME LOGIC RULES:
  ❗ This has a UNIQUE Nash equilibrium, unlike AS1 and AS4.
  ❗ (~A) is dominant for farmer; (~I) is dominant for staff.
  ❗ Collectively optimal (A,I)=(2,1) is individually unstable.
  ❗ The (~A,~I) equilibrium creates the asymmetric payoff in AS3:
     it reduces the transformer capacity available to ALL farmers,
     lowering the effective value of the (A,A) cell in AS3.
  ❗ APERC enforcement can shift payoffs: effective monitoring raises
     penalty of (~A) for farmer and (~I) for staff, potentially making
     (A,I) incentive-compatible. Paper notes this requires APERC capacity
     and political will beyond the boundary of the adjacency network.

AS6 — GROUNDWATER EXTRACTION DILEMMA (Section 6.6)
Game type: Dynamic Prisoner's Dilemma (CPR — Common Pool Resource)

CONTEXT (from paper):
"Certainly the most visible link is located between power utilisation
and groundwater exploitation." Groundwater tables in the Deccan plateau
case study area have declined drastically over two decades (Fig. 3).
"Decreasing groundwater tables require deepened wells, which steadily
increases energy use and the respective loads on the electricity grid."
This makes AS3 capacity constraint worse and AS1 coordination harder.
"The extraction equilibrium (E;E) initially creates the highest outcome,
but with a decreasing groundwater level, the costs of pumping (γ)
increase and the equilibrium falls below the payoffs for restraining."

STRATEGIC TRIGGER:
Two farmers (F1, F2) sharing an aquifer SIMULTANEOUSLY decide:
  E  = extract (pump at unconstrained rate)
  ~E = restrain (limit pump hours, conserve groundwater)

PAYOFF MATRIX (Table 6 — ordinal ranks with cost parameter γ):

                AS6: F2
                 E           ~E
       F1  E   2-γ, 2-γ    3, 0
          ~E   0, 3         1, 1

PAYOFF INTERPRETATION:
  (E, E)   = 2-γ, 2-γ → Both extract: initially highest individual payoff
                          (2-γ when γ is small). As groundwater table drops,
                          γ increases, and this payoff eventually falls BELOW
                          (1,1). The equilibrium is DYNAMICALLY UNSTABLE.
  (~E, ~E) = 1, 1       → Mutual restraint: stable long-term payoff.
                          Aquifer recharges at natural rate. Initially
                          Pareto-inferior but eventually superior as γ rises.
  (E, ~E)  = 3, 0       → F1 extracts, F2 restrains: F1 gets maximum gain
                          (3); F2 suffers lowest payoff (0) — deeper well,
                          higher costs. STRONGEST INDIVIDUAL INCENTIVE.
  (~E, E)  = 0, 3       → F2 extracts, F1 restrains: reverse of above.

DYNAMIC LOGIC:
  - Short term (γ small): (E) is dominant strategy. Nash = (E,E) = (2-γ, 2-γ).
  - Long term (γ large): (E,E) payoff < (1,1). Mutual extraction eventually
    yields worse outcome than mutual restraint — but individual defection
    incentive (3>1) persists. Classic tragedy of the commons.

GAME LOGIC RULES:
  ❗ This is a DYNAMIC CPR PRISONER'S DILEMMA. Dominant strategy is
     extraction (E) throughout, but the collective cost compounds over time.
  ❗ Parameter γ is normalised in the model but empirically observable
     via bore-well depth data (Fig. 3: depth increasing with well age).
  ❗ No monitoring of individual pump hours exists — open-access commons.
  ❗ Physical links to other AS:
     - Rising γ increases electrical load per farm → worsens AS3 capacity
       shortfall → reduces τ parameter → degrades AS1 payoffs.
     - Paradox noted in paper: successful coordination in AS1 (efficient
       pumpsets) could AGGRAVATE groundwater exploitation by reducing
       pumping cost per unit water extracted.

SECTION E: ADJACENCY NETWORK STRUCTURE
(Fig. 4, Kimmich 2013)

AS2 [ι — informational link]
  ↓ transforms AS1 into sequential game
AS1 (Core) ← τ ← AS3 ← δ ← AS4
                 ↑             ↓
                 AS6 [γ]      AS5 [δ,actors]
                 ↑
               (γ rises as groundwater drops)

Links by type (Link Proposition 1 in paper):
  AS2 → AS1: information (ι) — cognitive/learning constraint
  AS3 → AS1: biophysical (τ) — transformer capacity constraint
  AS4 → AS3: institution (δ) — collusion norms enable unauthorized access
  AS5 → AS3: institution + actors — (~A,~I) Nash reduces capacity in AS3
  AS6 → AS3: biophysical (γ) — increased load degrades capacity
  AS4 ↔ AS5: actors — same farmer F2 and staff U involved in both

Bidirectional causation (Interdependency Proposition 3):
  AS3 ↔ AS6: sufficient capacity (AS3) can intensify groundwater
              exploitation (AS6); AS6 aggravates AS3 capacity constraint.
  AS1 ↔ AS3: successful coordination (AS1) reduces electric load,
              improving effective capacity (AS3); but AS3 is a prerequisite
              for AS1 to work (τ must be adequate first).

SECTION G: CONSTRAINTS FROM ODD (for payoff grounding)

Physical constraints:
  - Average 17 pumpsets connected per transformer.
  - 11% of transformers operate below average pumpset power capacity.
  - Only 10% of farmers successfully use capacitors (survey).
  - Branded pumpsets: 67%; ISI-marked: 37%; BEE-rated: 6%.
  - Estimated 20–30% unauthorised connections across the study area.
  - Five villages: 1–3 farmer deaths per year from DIY transformer repair.

Institutional constraints:
  - APERC monitoring is stochastic (random audit), not continuous.
  - Flat-rate electricity tariff: no price signal for conservation.
  - No metering of individual pump hours (open-access groundwater).
  - No external water trading or groundwater market.
  - Social networks are village-scale and kinship-based.

Payoff constraints:
  - All payoffs are ORDINAL RANKS (0, 1, 2, 3) as in paper's tables.
  - Do NOT use cardinal utility values or invent numerical scales.
  - Payoff ordering must respect the equilibrium logic of each game type.
  - Parameter γ in AS6 must be treated as a dynamic variable
    (initially small; increases over time as groundwater table drops).
