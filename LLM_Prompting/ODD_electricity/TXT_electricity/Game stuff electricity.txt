Mathematical Logic & Payoff Structures (The Rules of the Game)

1. TRANSFORMER CAPACITY LOGIC (τ)
   - Constraint: An average of 17 pumpsets are connected per distribution transformer.
   - Physical Flow: Under-provision of capacity (driven by AS3 and AS5) degrades the voltage stability for the entire group.
   - Structural Linkage: If capacity parameter τ is insufficient, Demand-Side Measures (DSM) in AS1 physically cannot function, making coordination impossible.

2. DSM EFFECTIVENESS & FALSE LEARNING (ι)
   - Synchronisation Rule: Capacitors only correct the power factor if ALL connected farmers adopt them simultaneously.
   - Cognitive Constraint (ι): Farmers incorrectly attribute unilateral capacitor failure to "bad technology" rather than a "lack of coordination", establishing a negative feedback loop in social learning.

3. INSTITUTIONAL COLLUSION (δ)
   - Sub-station staff have discretionary gatekeeping power.
   - If social ties or reciprocal norms (δ) exist, informal connections are enabled.
   - Regulatory Variable: APERC's random audit probability. High monitoring probability reduces the expected utility of collusion, pushing actors back to formal compliance.

4. GROUNDWATER & ENERGY NEXUS (γ)
   - Dynamic Cost Parameter (γ): Pumping costs increase linearly as the groundwater table depth increases.
   - Physical Linkage: Deeper wells require more electrical load -> Worsens the transformer capacity constraint (τ) -> Degrades AS1 payoffs.
   - The Efficiency Paradox: If farmers successfully coordinate on efficient pumpsets (AS1), the pumping cost per unit of water drops, which actually accelerates groundwater depletion (AS6).

5. UTILITY / PAYOFF FUNCTIONS
   - Metric: All payoffs are measured in Ordinal Ranks (0 = worst, 1 = baseline, 2 = good, 3 = best). Cardinal utility should not be inferred.
   - Structural Dominance:
     - Free-riding on infrastructure (~A) and withholding maintenance effort (~I) strictly dominate.
     - Groundwater extraction (E) strictly dominates restraint, inevitably driving the system towards ecological depletion unless costs (γ) exceed a critical threshold.
