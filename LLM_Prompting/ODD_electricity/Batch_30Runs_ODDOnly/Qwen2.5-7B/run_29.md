### Title: Distinct Action Situations in the Irrigation Electricity Governance Model

#### 1. Farmer-Farmer Capacitor Adoption Assurance Game (AS1)

**Tension:** Coordination vs. Free-Rider

**Matrix:**

|           | Farmer 2: Adopt | Farmer 2: No Adopt |
|-----------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Farmer 1: Adopt** | (3, 3) | (1, 2) |
| **Farmer 1: No Adopt** | (2, 1) | (0, 0) |

**Justification:** Both farmers can mutually benefit from adopting capacitors, but if one farmer adopts while the other does not, the non-adopting farmer benefits without incurring costs. This creates a coordination problem where mutual cooperation Pareto-dominates, but is risky due to the free-rider incentive.

#### 2. Sequential Social-Learning Process in Capacitor Adoption (AS2)

**Tension:** Social Learning vs. Risk Aversion

**Sequential Representation (Game Tree):**

```
1. Farmer 1: Adopt (3) / No Adopt (0)
  / \
2. Farmer 2: Adopt (3) / No Adopt (0)
    / \
3. Outcome: (3, 3) / (0, 0)
```

**Justification:** Each farmer observes the outcome of a peer's decision and imitates only if the outcome ranks higher. This creates a sequential process where diffusion of capacitor adoption occurs only after a successful coordinated trial has been observed. The risk of imitation is a key tension.

#### 3. Asymmetric Transformer-Capacity Authorization Dilemma (AS3)

**Tension:** Authorization vs. Free-Rider

**Matrix:**

|           | Farmer 2: Authorize (1) | Farmer 2: No Authorize (0) |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Farmer 1: Authorize (1)** | (2, 1) | (1, 0) |
| **Farmer 1: No Authorize (0)** | (1, 2) | (0, 0) |

**Justification:** One farmer’s authorization benefits both by raising voltage quality, but the cost falls solely on the authorizer. This creates a free-rider incentive where mutual cooperation Pareto-dominates, but is undermined by the uneven payoffs.

#### 4. Mutual-Exchange Coordination Game between Farmer and Sub-Station Staff (AS4)

**Tension:** Informal Exchange vs. Formal Compliance

**Matrix:**

|           | Staff: Invest (1) | Staff: No Invest (0) |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Farmer: Offer Exchange (1)** | (1, 1) | (0, 0) |
| **Farmer: No Offer (0)** | (0, 1) | (0, 0) |

**Justification:** Reciprocal benefit arises only when both engage in informal exchange. If either party abstains, the offerer incurs a loss while the abstainer reverts to baseline. This creates a coordination problem where mutual cooperation is necessary but risky due to the potential for opportunism.

#### 5. Asymmetric Coordination Game between Farmer and Sub-Station Staff (AS5)

**Tension:** Legal Cooperation vs. Opportunism

**Matrix:**

|           | Staff: Invest (1) | Staff: No Invest (0) |
|-----------|------------------|---------------------|
| **Farmer: Formal Request (1)** | (1, 1) | (0, 0) |
| **Farmer: Informal Request (0)** | (1, 0) | (0, 0) |

**Justification:** Mutual formal cooperation is collectively optimal, but staff gain modestly even under formal cooperation due to the investment burden. This creates asymmetric incentives between legality and opportunism, where staff may prefer to save effort over formal fees.

#### 6. Groundwater Extraction Prisoner’s Dilemma (AS6)

**Tension:** Collective Restraint vs. Short-Term Gain

**Matrix:**

|           | Farmer 2: Restrain (1) | Farmer 2: Over-Extract (0) |
|-----------|-----------------------|--------------------------|
| **Farmer 1: Restrain (1)** | (2, 2) | (1, 3) |
| **Farmer 1: Over-Extract (0)** | (3, 1) | (0, 0) |

**Justification:** Mutual restraint sustains yields, but unilateral over-extraction offers short-term gain and accelerates depletion. This creates a prisoner’s dilemma where collective restraint is beneficial but risky due to the temptation for short-term gains.

### Summary
The distinct action situations in the model capture various strategic tensions in irrigation electricity governance, including farmer-farmer coordination, farmer-staff interaction, transformer capacity management, capacitor adoption, authorization/enforcement, informal exchange, and groundwater extraction. These action situations are grounded in empirical data and reflect the complexities of resource governance in Andhra Pradesh, India.
