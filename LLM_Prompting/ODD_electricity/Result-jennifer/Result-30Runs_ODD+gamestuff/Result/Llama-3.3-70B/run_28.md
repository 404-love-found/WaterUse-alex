# Run 28 — meta-llama/Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct-Turbo

### Action Situations

1. **Transformer Capacity Game**
	* Title: Transformer Capacity Game
	* Location: Transformer group level
	* Players: Farmers, Substation Staff
	* Roles: Electricity consumers, Service providers
	* Actions: Farmers - Invest in transformer capacity, Do not invest; Staff - Approve or reject investment
	* Control Rules: Investment decisions affect transformer capacity and reliability
	* Information: Farmers have information on past voltage quality and peer investment decisions; Staff have information on connection records and maintenance needs
	* Outcomes: Transformer capacity increase or decrease, Reliability improvement or degradation
	* Payoffs: 
		|  | Invest | Do not invest |
		| --- | --- | --- |
		| Approve | 3, 2 | 1, 3 |
		| Reject | 0, 1 | 2, 0 |
	* Strategic Tension: This is a **Capacity Provision Game**, where farmers face a dilemma between contributing to transformer capacity and free-riding on others' investments. Staff must balance approval and rejection of investment requests.
	* Temporal Structure: Repeated annually
	* Relevant Rules: Choice rules for investment decisions, control rules for transformer capacity and reliability

		| Extract | 2, 2 | 0, 3 |

	* Temporal Structure: Continuous over time

### Analysis and Comparison

The strategic core of each situation is a distinct governance interaction:

* Transformer Capacity Game: Capacity provision dilemma
* Groundwater Extraction Game: Common pool resource dilemma
* Capacitor Adoption Game: Coordination dilemma
* Authorization Game: Authorization dilemma
* Collusion Exchange Game: Collusion dilemma
* Social Learning Game: Non-strategic sequential process (observation and imitation)

Comparison of strategic action situations reveals distinct player roles, decision types, and payoff structures. Revision or replacement of action situations is not necessary, as each situation reflects a different governance interaction.

Each game complies with the ODD+D description, and payoff matrices are grounded in the electricity-irrigation governance context. Payoff values reflect the likely consequences of each combined action, and actions are rational, context-appropriate, and economically or behaviorally realistic.
