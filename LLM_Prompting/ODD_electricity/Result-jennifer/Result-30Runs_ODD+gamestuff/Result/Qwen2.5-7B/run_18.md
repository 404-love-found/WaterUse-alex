# Run 18 — Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct-Turbo

### Action Situations

#### 1. Capacitor Adoption Game
**Title**: Capacitor Adoption Game
**Location**: Transformer group level
**Players**: Farmers
**Roles**: Electricity consumers, technology adopters
**Actions**: 
- Invest in capacitor (C)
- Do not invest in capacitor (NC)
**Control Rules**: Capacitors improve voltage stability and pump efficiency, but benefits are strongest when adopted by multiple farmers.
**Information**: Farmers observe visible adoption by neighbors and may imitate successful peers. Farmers have incomplete knowledge about the exact cause of observed voltage improvements.
**Outcomes**: 
- Voltage quality improves (VQ)
- Voltage quality remains unchanged (VU)
**Payoffs**:
| Player 2 | Invest (C) | Do Not Invest (NC) |
|----------|------------|--------------------|
| Invest (C) | 3, 3        | 1, 2               |
| Do Not Invest (NC) | 2, 1        | 0, 0               |
**Strategic Tension**: This is a coordination game. The payoff matrix reflects the free-rider problem where unilateral investment might not yield significant benefits, but coordinated investment can improve voltage quality for all.
**Temporal Structure**: Repeated annually.
**Relevant Rules**: Social norms, learning constraints, and the collective benefit of capacitor adoption.

#### 2. Farmer-Staff Connection Game
**Title**: Farmer-Staff Connection Game
**Location**: Transformer group level
**Players**: Farmers, Sub-station Staff
**Roles**: Electricity consumers, service providers
**Actions**: 
- Seek formal connection (FC)
- Seek informal connection (IC)
**Control Rules**: Farmers can choose between formal and informal connections. Sub-station staff can either invest in formal connections or tolerate informal connections.
**Information**: Farmers have incomplete information about staff enforcement and the potential risks and costs of formal vs. informal connections.
**Outcomes**: 
- Formal connection established (FC)
- Informal connection established (IC)
**Payoffs**:
| Player 2 | Formal Connection (FC) | Informal Connection (IC) |
|----------|-----------------------|-------------------------|
| Formal Connection (FC) | 2, 2                   | 0, 3                    |
| Informal Connection (IC) | 3, 0                   | 1, 1                    |
**Strategic Tension**: This is a game of trust and reciprocity. The payoff matrix reflects the mutual benefits of formal connections but also the risk of free-riding.
**Temporal Structure**: Repeated annually.
**Relevant Rules**: Staff enforcement, discretionary power, and the collective benefit of formal connections.

#### 3. Groundwater Extraction Game
**Title**: Groundwater Extraction Game
**Location**: Village-level groundwater basin
**Players**: Farmers
**Roles**: Water users, environmental stewards
**Actions**: 
- Extract groundwater (G)
- Restrict extraction (R)
**Control Rules**: Farmers decide how much groundwater to extract based on electricity availability, groundwater depth, and pumping costs.
**Information**: Farmers have incomplete knowledge about the effects of their extraction on the groundwater table and the overall impact on the aquifer.
**Outcomes**: 
- Aquifer depth remains stable (S)
- Aquifer depth depletes (D)
**Payoffs**:
| Player 2 | Extract Groundwater (G) | Restrict Extraction (R) |
|----------|------------------------|------------------------|
| Extract Groundwater (G) | 1, 1                   | 3, 3                   |
| Restrict Extraction (R) | 3, 3                   | 0, 0                   |
**Strategic Tension**: This is a common pool resource game. The payoff matrix reflects the tragedy of the commons where individual high extraction can dominate in the short run but leads to long-term depletion.
**Temporal Structure**: Repeated annually.
**Relevant Rules**: Aquifer recharge, rainfall, and the collective impact of extraction on the groundwater table.

#### 4. Transformer Capacity Game
**Title**: Transformer Capacity Game
**Location**: Transformer group level
**Players**: Sub-station Staff, Farmers
**Roles**: Service providers, electricity consumers
**Actions**: 
- Increase transformer capacity (IC)
- Maintain current capacity (MC)
**Control Rules**: Farmers can contribute to transformer capacity upgrades, while sub-station staff decide whether to invest in capacity improvements.
**Information**: Farmers have incomplete information about the impact of their contributions on transformer reliability and the costs of formal vs. informal connections.
**Outcomes**: 
- Transformer capacity increased (IC)
- Transformer capacity maintained (MC)
**Payoffs**:
| Player 2 | Increase Capacity (IC) | Maintain Capacity (MC) |
|----------|-----------------------|-----------------------|
| Increase Capacity (IC) | 1, 1                  | 3, 3                  |
| Maintain Capacity (MC) | 3, 3                  | 0, 0                  |
**Strategic Tension**: This is a capacity provision game. The payoff matrix reflects the free-rider problem where one farmer's contribution can benefit the group but at a cost to the individual.
**Temporal Structure**: Repeated annually.
**Relevant Rules**: Transformer load, voltage stability, and the collective benefit of increased capacity.

#### 5. Social Learning Game
**Title**: Social Learning Game
**Location**: Transformer group level
**Players**: Farmers
**Roles**: Technology adopters, social learners
**Actions**: 
- Imitate successful peers (I)
- Do not imitate (NI)
**Control Rules**: Farmers observe the outcomes of their neighbors' technology adoption decisions and update their own strategies.
**Information**: Farmers have incomplete knowledge about the exact causes of observed outcomes and may misattribute the reasons for success or failure.
**Outcomes**: 
- Technology adoption successful (SA)
- Technology adoption unsuccessful (UA)
**Payoffs**:
| Player 2 | Imitate (I) | Do Not Imitate (NI) |
|----------|-------------|---------------------|
| Imitate (I) | 2, 2        | 0, 3                |
| Do Not Imitate (NI) | 3, 0        | 1, 1                |
**Strategic Tension**: This is a social learning game. The payoff matrix reflects the path dependency of technology adoption and the risk of imitating failed strategies.
**Temporal Structure**: Repeated annually.
**Relevant Rules**: Social norms, imitation barriers, and the visibility of successful adoption.

### Strategic Core Analysis

1. **Capacitor Adoption Game**: This is a coordination game where the payoff matrix reflects the free-rider problem. The coordination of multiple farmers adopting capacitors can lead to better voltage quality, but unilateral adoption might not yield significant benefits.

2. **Farmer-Staff Connection Game**: This is a game of trust and reciprocity. The payoff matrix reflects the mutual benefits of formal connections but also the risk of free-riding. The tension arises from the discretionary power of sub-station staff and the risk of informal connections.

3. **Groundwater Extraction Game**: This is a common pool resource game where individual high extraction can dominate in the short run but leads to long-term depletion. The payoff matrix reflects the tragedy of the commons.

4. **Transformer Capacity Game**: This is a capacity provision game where the payoff matrix reflects the free-rider problem. The tension arises from the cost of formal vs. informal connections and the collective benefit of increased capacity.

5. **Social Learning Game**: This is a social learning game where the payoff matrix reflects the path dependency of technology adoption and the risk of imitating failed strategies. The tension arises from the incomplete knowledge and misattribution of causes.

### Revised Games to Ensure Strategic Diversity
