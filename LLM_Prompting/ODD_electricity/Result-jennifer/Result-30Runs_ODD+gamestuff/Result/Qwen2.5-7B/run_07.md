# Run 7 — Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct-Turbo

### Action Situation 1: Capacitor Adoption Coordination

**1. Title:** Capacitor Adoption Coordination

**2. Location:** Transformer group level

**3. Players:** 
- **Farmer**: Decision-maker for capacitor adoption
- **Substation Staff**: Decision-maker for transformer capacity

**4. Roles:** 
- **Farmer**: Electricity consumer, technology adopter
- **Substation Staff**: Service provider, capacity allocator

**5. Actions:** 
- **Farmer**: Choose to adopt or not adopt a capacitor
- **Substation Staff**: Decide to invest in transformer capacity or not

**6. Control Rules:** 
- If farmers invest in capacitors, the local voltage quality improves.
- If substation staff invest in capacity, the transformer can handle more load.
- Capacitor adoption and capacity investment are linked: coordination yields the best outcome.

**7. Information:** 
- Farmers have imperfect information about the current voltage quality and the benefits of capacitor adoption.
- Staff have imperfect information about the current transformer capacity and the benefits of investment.

**8. Outcomes:** 
- Improved voltage quality, reduced transformer failure risk, and lower pumping costs.

**9. Payoffs:** 
- Capacitor adoption and capacity investment improve outcomes for both players.
- Unilateral investment by either player results in a less efficient outcome.
- No investment results in the worst outcome.

| Farmer \ Substation Staff | Not Invest | Invest |
|---------------------------|------------|--------|
| Not Invest                | 1          | 2      |
| Invest                    | 2          | 3      |

**10. Strategic Tension:** 
This is a **Cooperation, Coordination, and Conflict Game**. The farmers and substation staff must coordinate to invest in both capacitors and transformer capacity to achieve the best outcome. However, if one player invests unilaterally, the benefits are less than when both invest together.

**11. Temporal Structure:** 
Repeated annually.

**12. Relevant Rules:** 
- **Boundary Rule**: Farmers are part of a transformer group.
- **Position Rule**: Substation staff have authority over transformer capacity.
- **Choice Rule**: Farmers can adopt a capacitor, and substation staff can invest in capacity.
- **Control Rule**: Capacitor adoption and capacity investment are linked.

### Action Situation 2: Authorization Game

**1. Title:** Authorization Game

**2. Location:** Substation

**3. Players:** 
- **Farmer**: Seeker of formal connection
- **Substation Staff**: Authorizer of formal connection

**4. Roles:** 
- **Farmer**: Applicant for formal connection
- **Substation Staff**: Decision-maker for formal authorization

**5. Actions:** 
- **Farmer**: Choose to seek formal connection or informal access
- **Substation Staff**: Decide to authorize formal connection or tolerate informal access

**6. Control Rules:** 
- Formal authorization requires costs for the farmer and effort for the substation staff.
- Informal access is cheaper for the farmer but increases the risk of detection and penalties.

**7. Information:** 
- Farmers have imperfect information about the cost of formal connections and the risk of detection.
- Staff have imperfect information about the farmer's compliance and the risk of detection.

**8. Outcomes:** 
- Formal authorization provides better service reliability but incurs costs.
- Informal access is cheaper but increases the risk of detection and penalties.

**9. Payoffs:** 
- Formal authorization provides better service reliability but incurs costs.
- Informal access is cheaper but increases the risk of detection and penalties.

| Farmer \ Substation Staff | Formal Connection | Informal Access |
|----------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Formal Connection          | 2                 | 1               |
| Informal Access            | 1                 | 3               |

**10. Strategic Tension:** 
This is an **Authorization Game**. The farmer and substation staff must decide whether to seek formal authorization or informal access. Formal authorization provides better service reliability but incurs costs, while informal access is cheaper but increases the risk of detection and penalties.

**11. Temporal Structure:** 
Repeated annually.

**12. Relevant Rules:** 
- **Boundary Rule**: Farmers can seek formal connections.
- **Position Rule**: Substation staff have authority over formal authorization.
- **Choice Rule**: Farmers can seek formal connections, and substation staff can authorize or tolerate informal access.
- **Control Rule**: Formal authorization requires costs for the farmer and effort for the substation staff.

### Action Situation 3: Groundwater Extraction Game

**1. Title:** Groundwater Extraction Game

**2. Location:** Village-level groundwater basin

**3. Players:** 
- **Farmer**: Decision-maker for groundwater extraction

**4. Roles:** 
- **Farmer**: Groundwater user

**5. Actions:** 
- **Farmer**: Choose to pump at full rate or restrain extraction

**6. Control Rules:** 
- Excessive pumping depletes the aquifer and increases pumping costs.
- Restraint reduces immediate costs but may reduce crop yield.

**7. Information:** 
- Farmers have imperfect information about the current groundwater level and the impacts of their extraction decisions.
- The groundwater level and pumping costs dynamically shift based on the collective extraction decisions.

**8. Outcomes:** 
- Full rate pumping increases current crop yield but depletes the aquifer and increases future pumping costs.
- Restraint reduces immediate costs but may reduce current crop yield.

**9. Payoffs:** 
- Full rate pumping yields higher immediate benefits but depletes the aquifer and increases future costs.
- Restraint reduces immediate costs but may reduce current crop yield.

| Farmer \ Farmer | Full Rate | Restrain |
|-----------------|-----------|----------|
| Full Rate       | 2         | 1        |
| Restrain        | 1         | 3        |

**10. Strategic Tension:** 
This is a **Common Pool Resource Game**. The farmers must decide whether to pump at full rate or restrain extraction. Full rate pumping increases current crop yield but depletes the aquifer and increases future pumping costs. Restraint reduces immediate costs but may reduce current crop yield.

**11. Temporal Structure:** 
Repeated annually.

**12. Relevant Rules:** 
- **Boundary Rule**: Farmers share a groundwater basin.
- **Position Rule**: Farmers are decision-makers for groundwater extraction.
- **Choice Rule**: Farmers can pump at full rate or restrain extraction.
- **Control Rule**: The groundwater level and pumping costs dynamically shift based on the collective extraction decisions.

### Action Situation 5: Demand-Side Management (DSM) Coordination Game

**1. Title:** Demand-Side Management (DSM) Coordination Game

**2. Location:** Transformer group level

**3. Players:** 
- **Farmer**: Decision-maker for demand-side management technology adoption

**4. Roles:** 
- **Farmer**: Technology adopter and demand manager

**5. Actions:** 
- **Farmer**: Choose to adopt or not adopt demand-side management (DSM) technology

**6. Control Rules:** 
- DSM technology improves voltage quality and reduces pumping costs.
- The benefit of adopting DSM technology depends on how many neighbors also adopt it.

**7. Information:** 
- Farmers have imperfect information about the benefits of adopting DSM technology.
- The benefits of DSM technology are path-dependent: early failed or isolated adoption can discourage later uptake, while visibly successful coordinated adoption can spread through the social network.

**8. Outcomes:** 
- DSM technology improves voltage quality and reduces pumping costs.
- Unilateral adoption results in less efficient outcomes.
- Coordinated adoption yields the best outcome.

**9. Payoffs:** 
- Coordinated adoption of DSM technology yields the best outcome.
- Unilateral adoption results in less efficient outcomes.
- No adoption results in the worst outcome.

| Farmer \ Farmer | Not Adopt DSM | Adopt DSM |
|-----------------|---------------|-----------|
| Not Adopt DSM   | 1             | 2         |
| Adopt DSM       | 2             | 3         |

**10. Strategic Tension:** 
This is a **DSM Coordination Game**. The farmers must decide whether to adopt demand-side management (DSM) technology. Coordinated adoption of DSM technology yields the best outcome, while unilateral adoption results in less efficient outcomes. The path-dependent nature of adoption can discourage early uptake and encourage coordinated adoption.

**11. Temporal Structure:** 
Repeated annually.

**12. Relevant Rules:** 
- **Boundary Rule**: Farmers share a transformer group.
- **Position Rule**: Farmers are decision-makers for DSM technology adoption.
- **Choice Rule**: Farmers can adopt or not adopt DSM technology.
- **Control Rule**: The benefits of DSM technology are path-dependent and depend on coordinated adoption.
