**Action Situation 2: Collusion Bribery Game**
- Title: Collusion Bribery Game
- Location: Informal interaction between farmer and substation staff, typically at the transformer or substation level.
- Players: One farmer and one substation staff member.
- Roles: Farmer: electricity user seeking informal advantages; Staff: utility employee with discretionary enforcement power.
- Actions: Farmer: Offer Bribe (B) to collude for favors (e.g., lenient enforcement, unauthorized connection tolerance) or Not Offer (NB). Staff: Accept (A) the bribe and provide collusive favors, or Enforce/Reject (E) and possibly report the farmer.
- Control Rules: If farmer offers and staff accepts, a collusive tie forms, yielding mutual benefits. If farmer offers and staff enforces, the farmer faces a penalty (e.g., fine, disconnection), and the staff receives a reward for enforcement. If farmer does not offer, no collusion occurs; staff may still enforce or not, but no bribe is exchanged. The staff's enforcement when no bribe is offered yields standard duty payoff; accepting when no bribe yields no extra benefit.
- Information: Both players know the general payoff structure and the local risk of detection. They do not know the other's simultaneous choice.
- Outcomes: Formation of collusive tie, penalties, or status quo.
- Payoffs: Ordinal ranks:
  - (B, A): Farmer gets collusive benefit (cheaper access, leniency) (3); Staff gets bribe (2) (note: staff's best is 3 from enforcement reward, so 2 is second best).
  - (B, E): Farmer penalized (0); Staff gets enforcement reward (3).
  - (NB, A): Farmer safe, no bribe (2); Staff gets no bribe, maybe slight risk or wasted willingness (1).
  - (NB, E): Farmer safe (2); Staff performs duty, gets standard payoff (2).
- Strategic Tension: Trust game. Staff has a dominant strategy to Enforce (E) because 3 > 2 when farmer plays B, and 2 > 1 when farmer plays NB. Farmer, anticipating this, chooses Not Offer (2 > 0). The unique Nash equilibrium (NB, E) yields (2,2), which is Pareto inferior to (B, A) with (3,2). This represents a social dilemma where lack of trust prevents mutually beneficial collusion. The tension is strategic.
- Temporal Structure: Annual, as farmers and staff are matched yearly.
- Relevant Rules: Boundary rules: farmer and staff are matched based on transformer and existing ties. Choice rules: willingness moderated by detection risk and corruption level. Control rules: tie forms only if both choose B and A.

Payoff matrix:
|       | Accept (A) | Enforce (E) |
|-------|------------|-------------|
| Offer Bribe (B) | (3,2) | (0,3) |
| Not Offer (NB) | (2,1) | (2,2) |

Compliance with ODD: The ODD states that collusive ties form only when both are willing, and willingness is moderated by detection risk. This matrix captures a scenario where enforcement incentives (e.g., high detection risk or rewards for reporting) make staff prefer to enforce, creating a trust problem. The ordinal payoffs reflect the asymmetric benefits and risks. Note: The ODD does not explicitly fix the payoff ordering; it allows variation based on corruption level and detection risk. This game represents one plausible configuration that ensures strategic diversity.

**Action Situation 3: Groundwater Extraction Game**
- Title: Groundwater Extraction Game
- Location: Shared aquifer underlying the transformer service area.
- Players: Two farmers sharing the same groundwater resource.
- Roles: Groundwater users.
- Actions: Each farmer chooses to Restrain (R) extraction (pump less) or Pump Full (P).
- Control Rules: If both restrain, the aquifer is used sustainably, keeping pumping energy costs low. If both pump full, the aquifer depletes rapidly, increasing pumping costs for both. If one restrains and the other pumps full, the restainer bears the cost of restraint while the pumper benefits from higher extraction and imposes depletion costs on both, but the pumper gains more water immediately.
- Information: Farmers sense groundwater depth and past extraction rates. They know the general relationship between extraction and pumping costs.
- Outcomes: Aquifer level change, pumping costs, crop yields.
- Payoffs: Ordinal ranks:
  - (R, R): Sustainable use, low costs, good yields (3,3)
  - (R, P): Restrainer gets low yield and still faces depletion (0); Pumper gets high yield, lower costs in short term (2)
  - (P, R): (2,0)
  - (P, P): Over-extraction, high
