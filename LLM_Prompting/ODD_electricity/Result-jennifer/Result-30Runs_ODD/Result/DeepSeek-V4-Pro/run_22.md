# Action Situations in the Electricity‑Irrigation Governance Model

## 1. DSM Coordination Game  
**Type:** Strategic (Assurance / Stag Hunt)

### IAD Description
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Location** | Transformer service area – farmers sharing the same distribution transformer |
| **Players** | Two representative farmers (A and B) who have not yet adopted capacitor/DSM equipment |
| **Roles** | Potential adopters of demand‑side management technology |
| **Actions** | Each farmer chooses **Invest** (adopt capacitor/DSM) or **Not Invest** |
| **Control Rules** | The shared benefit (voltage quality improvement) materialises **only if both invest** in the same cycle. If only one invests, the investment cost is paid but no benefit is realised. Adoption cost is paid at most once per farmer. |
| **Information** | Farmers know the adoption status of neighbours and past grid performance, but cannot observe the simultaneous choice of the other player. Perceptions of voltage quality are noisy. |
| **Outcomes** | Voltage quality improves (both invest) or remains poor; individual adoption cost incurred or not. |
| **Payoffs** | Both invest → high net benefit (3,3). One invests, one does not → investor loses cost with no benefit (0), non‑investor keeps status quo (1). Neither invests → status quo (1,1). |
| **Strategic Tension** | **Assurance game** – mutual investment is Pareto‑optimal but risky because unilateral investment yields the worst payoff. Players must coordinate to reach the good equilibrium. |
| **Temporal Structure** | Repeated annually; farmers may re‑enter the adoption pool until they succeed once. |
| **Relevant Rules** | Choice rule: farmers use heuristic and social‑learning rules; boundary rule: only farmers on the same transformer are paired; control rule: threshold of simultaneous adoptions required for benefit. |
