### Strategic Dilemmas in Water Use Model
#### 1. Irrigation Decision Dilemma
**Title:** Upstream vs Downstream Irrigation
**Tension:** Balancing individual irrigation needs with downstream water availability
**Matrix:**

|  | Upstream (High Irrigation) | Upstream (Low Irrigation) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Downstream (High Irrigation)** | Upstream: 8, Downstream: 2 | Upstream: 6, Downstream: 6 |
| **Downstream (Low Irrigation)** | Upstream: 9, Downstream: 1 | Upstream: 5, Downstream: 8 |

**Justification:** The matrix reflects the spatial asymmetry, where upstream farmers have priority access to water. If both upstream and downstream farmers irrigate heavily, the downstream farmer suffers from water scarcity (2 units). If the upstream farmer irrigates less, the downstream farmer can irrigate more (6 units). Ecological thresholds are implicit, as excessive irrigation can lead to water stress and affect yields.

#### 2. Water Allocation Dilemma
**Title:** National Authority vs Farmer Water Allocation
**Tension:** Balancing national authority's water allocation with individual farmer's needs
**Matrix:**

|  | National Authority (Restrictive) | National Authority (Permissive) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Farmer (High Demand)** | National Authority: 6, Farmer: 4 | National Authority: 3, Farmer: 9 |
| **Farmer (Low Demand)** | National Authority: 8, Farmer: 2 | National Authority: 5, Farmer: 6 |

**Justification:** The matrix reflects the tension between the national authority's allocation and the farmer's demand. If the national authority is restrictive and the farmer has high demand, the farmer suffers (4 units). If the national authority is permissive, the farmer benefits (9 units), but the national authority's allocation is less effective (3 units). Ecological thresholds are implicit, as excessive water allocation can lead to water stress and affect yields.

#### 3. Fishing Dilemma
**Title:** Upstream vs Downstream Fishing
**Tension:** Balancing individual fishing needs with downstream fish population sustainability
**Matrix:**

|  | Upstream (High Fishing) | Upstream (Low Fishing) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Downstream (High Fishing)** | Upstream: 5, Downstream: 1 | Upstream: 3, Downstream: 4 |
| **Downstream (Low Fishing)** | Upstream: 6, Downstream: 2 | Upstream: 4, Downstream: 6 |

**Justification:** The matrix reflects the spatial asymmetry, where upstream farmers have priority access to fish. If both upstream and downstream farmers fish heavily, the downstream fish population suffers (1 unit). If the upstream farmer fishes less, the downstream fish population can recover (4 units). Ecological thresholds are implicit, as excessive fishing can lead to fish population decline and affect sustainability.

#### 4. Budget Allocation Dilemma
**Title:** Farmer Budget Allocation
**Tension:** Balancing individual budget allocation between irrigation and fishing
**Matrix:**

|  | Irrigation (High Allocation) | Irrigation (Low Allocation) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Fishing (High Allocation)** | Irrigation: 7, Fishing: 3 | Irrigation: 4, Fishing: 7 |
| **Fishing (Low Allocation)** | Irrigation: 9, Fishing: 1 | Irrigation: 6, Fishing: 4 |

**Justification:** The matrix reflects the tension between allocating budget to irrigation or fishing. If a farmer allocates highly to irrigation and also fishes heavily, irrigation yields more (7 units), but fishing suffers (3 units). If the farmer allocates less to irrigation, fishing can yield more (7 units), but irrigation suffers (4 units). Ecological thresholds are implicit, as excessive allocation to irrigation can lead to water stress, and excessive fishing can lead to fish population decline. Max fields = 10 is reflected in the limited budget allocation options.
