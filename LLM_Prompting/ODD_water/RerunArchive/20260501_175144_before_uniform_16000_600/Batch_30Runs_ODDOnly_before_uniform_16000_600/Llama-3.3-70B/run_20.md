### Action Situation 1: Upstream Farmer's Irrigation Decision
#### Tension: Over-Irrigation vs. Conservation
#### Matrix:

|  | Downstream Farmer Cooperates (Conserves Water) | Downstream Farmer Defects (Over-Irrigates) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Upstream Farmer Cooperates (Conserves Water)** | 8, 8 | 5, 9 |
| **Upstream Farmer Defects (Over-Irrigates)** | 9, 5 | 3, 3 |

#### Justification: 
In the decentralized case, each farmer decides on the number of fields to irrigate based on their prediction of water flows and available budget. An upstream farmer's decision to over-irrigate can lead to reduced water availability for downstream farmers, causing tension. The matrix reflects the strategic dilemma, where cooperation (conserving water) leads to a higher payoff for both farmers (8, 8), while defection (over-irrigating) results in a lower payoff for the downstream farmer (5, 9) if the upstream farmer defects, and an even lower payoff for both (3, 3) if both defect.

### Action Situation 3: Farmer's Irrigation Decision Near Ecological Threshold
#### Tension: Irrigation Expansion vs. Risk of Water Scarcity
#### Matrix:

|  | Water Inflow is Above Threshold | Water Inflow is Below Threshold |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Farmer Irrigates Maximum Fields (10)** | 9, 9 | 1, 1 |
| **Farmer Limits Irrigation to Available Water** | 6, 6 | 8, 8 |

#### Justification: 
Near ecological thresholds, such as when water inflow is critical for maintaining the fish population or agricultural productivity, a farmer's decision to irrigate maximum fields (10) can lead to water scarcity, affecting their own and others' future water availability. The matrix reflects the strategic dilemma, where irrigating maximum fields when water inflow is above the threshold leads to a high payoff (9, 9), but results in a very low payoff (1, 1) if the water inflow is below the threshold. Limiting irrigation to available water leads to a moderate payoff (6, 6) when water is plentiful but a higher payoff (8, 8) when water is scarce, emphasizing the importance of conservation near ecological thresholds.

### Action Situation 4: Upstream vs. Downstream Farmer's Investment Decision
#### Tension: Investment in Irrigation vs. Investment in Fishing
#### Matrix:

|  | Downstream Farmer Invests in Fishing | Downstream Farmer Invests in Irrigation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Upstream Farmer Invests in Irrigation** | 5, 8 | 9, 5 |
| **Upstream Farmer Invests in Fishing** | 8, 5 | 4, 4 |

#### Justification: 
The decision of whether to invest in irrigation (expanding agricultural production) or fishing (enhancing subsistence activities) reflects a strategic tension between upstream and downstream farmers. The matrix shows that if the downstream farmer invests in fishing, the upstream farmer's investment in fishing leads to a moderate payoff for both (8, 5), while investment in irrigation results in a lower payoff for the downstream farmer (5, 8). If the downstream farmer invests in irrigation, the upstream farmer's investment in irrigation leads to a high payoff for the upstream farmer but a lower payoff for the downstream farmer (9, 5), indicating the spatial asymmetry in benefits from irrigation investments.
