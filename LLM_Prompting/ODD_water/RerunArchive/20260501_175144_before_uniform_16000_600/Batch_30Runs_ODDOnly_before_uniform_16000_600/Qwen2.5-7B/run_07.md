### Distinct Action Situations in the Decentralized Version (DV) of the Model

#### 1. Strategic Tension: Trade-off Between Risk and Reward

**Title:** Risk Aversion vs. Risk Taking

**Tension:** Farmers must decide whether to risk increasing the number of irrigated fields based on uncertain water availability, balancing the potential for higher yields against the risk of water stress.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                     | Downstream Farmer (Risk Averse) | Downstream Farmer (Risk Taker) |
|---------------------|---------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Upstream Farmer (Risk Averse)** | (50, 50)                         | (40, 60)                       |
| **Upstream Farmer (Risk Taker)**  | (60, 40)                         | (70, 70)                       |

**Justification:** In the decentralized version, each farmer makes independent decisions based on their income and past water availability. The upstream farmer is more risk-averse and may not increase the number of fields even if the downstream farmer does, leading to a lower but safer payoff. Conversely, the downstream farmer might take a risk by increasing the number of fields, expecting higher yields if water is available but facing potential losses if not.

#### 2. Strategic Tension: Allocation of Water Resources

**Title:** Efficient vs. Equitable Water Allocation

**Tension:** Farmers must decide whether to allocate water resources efficiently (maximizing total yield) or equitably (ensuring all farmers get a fair share).

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                     | Downstream Farmer (Efficient)    | Downstream Farmer (Equitable)  |
|---------------------|----------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Upstream Farmer (Efficient)** | (65, 65)                         | (60, 60)                       |
| **Upstream Farmer (Equitable)** | (55, 55)                         | (50, 50)                       |

**Justification:** The upstream farmer might choose an efficient allocation, maximizing total yield, while the downstream farmer might opt for an equitable allocation, ensuring all farmers receive a fair share. The efficient allocation leads to higher total yields but may not be perceived as fair by downstream farmers, while the equitable allocation ensures fairness but may result in lower total yields.

#### 3. Strategic Tension: Ecological Sustainability vs. Economic Profit

**Title:** Sustainable vs. Profit Maximization

**Tension:** Farmers must decide whether to prioritize sustainable practices that ensure the long-term health of the fish population or to maximize short-term economic profits.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                     | Downstream Farmer (Sustainable)  | Downstream Farmer (Profit Max) |
|---------------------|----------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Upstream Farmer (Sustainable)** | (45, 45)                         | (50, 40)                       |
| **Upstream Farmer (Profit Max)**  | (50, 40)                         | (55, 55)                       |

**Justification:** The upstream farmer might prioritize sustainability, ensuring the long-term health of the fish population, while the downstream farmer might focus on maximizing short-term profits. The sustainable approach ensures a balanced ecosystem but may result in lower short-term yields, while the profit-maximizing approach prioritizes immediate economic gains at the potential cost of ecological sustainability.

#### 4. Strategic Tension: Spatial Asymmetry

**Title:** Upstream vs. Downstream Farmer's Water Access

**Tension:** Upstream and downstream farmers face different water access challenges due to their spatial positions.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                     | Downstream Farmer (Increase Fields) | Downstream Farmer (Maintain Fields) |
|---------------------|-------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Upstream Farmer (Increase Fields)** | (60, 50)                            | (50, 50)                            |
| **Upstream Farmer (Maintain Fields)** | (50, 60)                            | (55, 55)                            |

**Justification:** The upstream farmer has a limited water supply and might choose to maintain a stable number of fields to ensure sustainable water use, while the downstream farmer, having a potentially more reliable water supply, might choose to increase the number of fields to maximize yields. The spatial asymmetry creates a strategic tension where the upstream farmer must balance water conservation with the downstream farmer's need for water.

#### 5. Strategic Tension: Ecological Thresholds

**Title:** Maintaining Ecological Balance vs. Maximizing Yields

**Tension:** Farmers must decide whether to maintain the ecological balance to avoid tipping points (e.g., overfishing leading to population collapse) or to maximize yields in the short term.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                     | Downstream Farmer (Maintain Balance) | Downstream Farmer (Maximize Yields) |
|---------------------|--------------------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Upstream Farmer (Maintain Balance)** | (55, 55)                            | (50, 60)                            |
| **Upstream Farmer (Maximize Yields)**  | (60, 50)                            | (55, 55)                            |

**Justification:** The upstream farmer might prioritize maintaining the ecological balance to avoid tipping points, while the downstream farmer might choose to maximize yields in the short term, risking the long-term health of the fish population. The trade-off between maintaining ecological balance and maximizing yields creates a strategic tension that affects the sustainability of the system.

These action situations reflect the unique strategic dilemmas faced by farmers in the decentralized version of the model, considering spatial asymmetry and ecological thresholds.
