### Title: Distinct Action Situations in the Decentralized Version (DV) of the Water Use Model

#### 1. Strategic Tension: **Upstream vs. Downstream in Water Allocation and Resource Scarcity**

**Justification**: In the decentralized version, the spatial asymmetry between upstream and downstream farmers leads to a strategic conflict over water allocation. Upstream farmers have more water at their disposal initially but may face scarcity downstream if they overuse their water allocations. Downstream farmers, who rely on upstream water flows, may face severe water stress and lower yields.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix**:

| Upstream Farmer | Downstream Farmer |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Increase Fields** | **Increase Fields** | **(2, 1)** |
| **Increase Fields** | **Decrease Fields** | **(1, 2)** |
| **Decrease Fields** | **Increase Fields** | **(1, 2)** |
| **Decrease Fields** | **Decrease Fields** | **(2, 2)** |

**Explanation of Payoffs**:
- **(2, 2)**: Both farmers decrease fields, leading to a balanced outcome with both achieving a moderate return.
- **(1, 2)**: Upstream increases fields, and downstream decreases fields. Upstream may face some scarcity, while downstream avoids it, leading to a lower return for upstream and a higher return for downstream.
- **(2, 1)**: Upstream decreases fields, and downstream increases fields. Downstream may face scarcity, while upstream avoids it, leading to a higher return for upstream and a lower return for downstream.

#### 2. Strategic Tension: **Resource Scarcity and Water Stress**

**Justification**: The ecological thresholds and the risk of water stress during the irrigation season create a strategic tension between farmers. If water stress occurs, it can lead to lower yields and, consequently, lower returns for farmers. The decision to increase or decrease the number of fields irrigated involves balancing the risk of water stress against potential higher returns.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix**:

| Upstream Farmer | Downstream Farmer |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Increase Fields** | **Increase Fields** | **(1, 1)** |
| **Increase Fields** | **Decrease Fields** | **(0, 2)** |
| **Decrease Fields** | **Increase Fields** | **(2, 0)** |
| **Decrease Fields** | **Decrease Fields** | **(2, 2)** |

**Explanation of Payoffs**:
- **(2, 2)**: Both farmers decrease fields, avoiding water stress and achieving a balanced outcome with both achieving a moderate return.
- **(1, 1)**: Both farmers increase fields. This leads to a balanced outcome but with a risk of water stress, resulting in lower returns for both.
- **(0, 2)**: Upstream increases fields, and downstream decreases fields. Upstream faces water stress, resulting in a lower return, while downstream avoids it, leading to a higher return for downstream.
- **(2, 0)**: Upstream decreases fields, and downstream increases fields. Downstream faces water stress, resulting in a lower return, while upstream avoids it, leading to a higher return for upstream.

#### 3. Strategic Tension: **Fish Population and Resource Sharing**

**Justification**: The fish population is a shared resource, and the risk of overfishing can lead to ecological thresholds and tipping points, affecting the long-term sustainability of the fish population. Upstream and downstream farmers have to decide how much fish to catch, balancing the immediate benefits against the risk of depleting the fish population.

- **(2, 0)**: Upstream decreases catch, and downstream increases catch. Downstream faces the risk of overfishing, resulting in a lower return, while upstream avoids it, leading to a higher return for upstream.

### Summary
- **Spatial Asymmetry**: The spatial arrangement of farmers influences their decisions and outcomes.
- **Ecological Thresholds**: The risk of ecological thresholds (e.g., water stress, overfishing) creates strategic tensions.
- **Max Fields**: The maximum number of fields (10) limits the strategic options available to farmers.

These action situations reflect the strategic tensions faced by farmers in the decentralized version of the water use model, considering the spatial asymmetry and ecological thresholds.
