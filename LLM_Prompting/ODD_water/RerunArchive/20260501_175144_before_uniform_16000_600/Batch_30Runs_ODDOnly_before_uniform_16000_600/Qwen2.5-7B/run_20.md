### Analysis of Distinct Action Situations in the Decentralized Version (DV) Using the IAD Framework

#### Title: Spatial and Ecological Tensions in Decentralized Water Use

#### Tension 1: Spatial Asymmetry and Uncertainty in Water Availability

**Justification**: In the decentralized version, farmers make decisions based on their location along the river, which affects their access to water and the ecological thresholds they face. Downstream farmers are more constrained by water availability and ecological thresholds compared to upstream farmers.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix**:

| Downstream Farmer | Upstream Farmer |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Increase fields   | Increase fields | (1, 1)           |
| Increase fields   | No increase     | (0, 2)           |
| No increase       | Increase fields | (2, 0)           |
| No increase       | No increase     | (1.5, 1.5)       |

- **Increase fields**: Farmers increase the number of fields they irrigate, potentially leading to water stress and ecological degradation.
- **No increase**: Farmers do not increase the number of fields they irrigate, maintaining the current number of fields.
- **(1, 1)**: Both farmers increase fields, leading to mutual water stress and potential ecological degradation.
- **(0, 2)**: Downstream farmer increases fields, upstream farmer does not. Downstream farmer faces water stress, upstream farmer avoids stress.
- **(2, 0)**: Upstream farmer increases fields, downstream farmer does not. Upstream farmer faces water stress, downstream farmer avoids stress.
- **(1.5, 1.5)**: Both farmers do not increase fields, leading to a balanced but slightly lower yield for both.

#### Tension 2: Ecological Thresholds and Water Stress

**Justification**: Ecological thresholds, such as the survival of juvenile fish, can lead to tipping points where the fish population collapses if water stress is too high. Downstream farmers, closer to the lake, are more directly affected by these ecological thresholds.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix**:

| Downstream Farmer | Upstream Farmer |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Maintain yield    | Maintain yield  | (1, 1)           |
| Maintain yield    | Reduce yield    | (0, 2)           |
| Reduce yield      | Maintain yield  | (2, 0)           |
| Reduce yield      | Reduce yield    | (1.5, 1.5)       |

- **Maintain yield**: Farmers maintain their current yield levels, which may lead to water stress and potential ecological degradation.
- **Reduce yield**: Farmers reduce their yield levels, avoiding water stress and ecological degradation.
- **(1, 1)**: Both farmers maintain yield, leading to mutual water stress and potential ecological degradation.
- **(0, 2)**: Downstream farmer maintains yield, upstream farmer reduces yield. Downstream farmer faces water stress, upstream farmer avoids stress.
- **(2, 0)**: Upstream farmer maintains yield, downstream farmer reduces yield. Upstream farmer faces water stress, downstream farmer avoids stress.
- **(1.5, 1.5)**: Both farmers reduce yield, leading to a balanced but slightly lower yield for both.
