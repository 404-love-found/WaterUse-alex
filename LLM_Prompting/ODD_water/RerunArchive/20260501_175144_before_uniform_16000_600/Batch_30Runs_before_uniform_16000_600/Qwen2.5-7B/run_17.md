### Title: Distinct Action Situations in the Decentralized Version (DV) of the Water Use Model

### 1. Strategic Tension: Risk and Reward in Water Allocation

**Tension:** Farmers must decide whether to risk increasing the number of irrigated fields based on uncertain water availability, balancing potential rewards against the risk of water stress.

**Justification:** In the decentralized version, farmers decide individually on the number of fields to irrigate. The decision is based on their past income, current budget, and water flow predictions. The strategic tension arises from the uncertainty in water availability and the potential for high rewards from increased irrigation versus the risk of water stress and reduced yields.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                | Upstream Farmer (U) | Downstream Farmer (D) |
|----------------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| **U: Increase** | (3, 3)              | (2, 2)                |
| **U: No Increase** | (4, 4)             | (5, 5)                |

**Explanation:**
- **Increase:** Farmer increases the number of irrigated fields.
- **No Increase:** Farmer does not increase the number of irrigated fields.
- **Payoffs:** The payoffs are in terms of the total number of fields irrigated, which directly translates to the total yield. The payoffs are symmetric, but the risk of water stress is greater for downstream farmers due to the sequential access to water.

### 2. Strategic Tension: Spatial Asymmetry in Water Access

**Tension:** Upstream and downstream farmers have different access to water resources, leading to potential conflicts over water allocation.

**Justification:** Upstream farmers have access to the water inflow first, while downstream farmers have access to the fishing lake first. This creates a spatial asymmetry where upstream farmers can potentially over-allocate water, leading to water stress for downstream farmers.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                | Upstream Farmer (U) | Downstream Farmer (D) |
|----------------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| **U: Over-allocate** | (3, 1)              | (2, 4)                |
| **U: Balanced Allocation** | (2, 2)             | (3, 3)                |

**Explanation:**
- **Over-allocate:** Upstream farmer allocates more water than needed, risking water stress for downstream farmers.
- **Balanced Allocation:** Upstream farmer allocates water in a balanced manner, ensuring sufficient water for downstream farmers.
- **Payoffs:** The payoffs reflect the total number of fields irrigated, which is lower for downstream farmers if the upstream farmer over-allocates. The payoffs are lower for the upstream farmer if they over-allocate, as it leads to water stress and reduced yields for downstream farmers.

### 3. Strategic Tension: Ecological Thresholds in Fish Population

**Tension:** The fish population growth is dependent on water inflow, and there is a risk of ecological thresholds being crossed, leading to catastrophic reductions in fish populations.

**Justification:** The fish population is age-structured and dependent on water inflow for survival. If the water inflow is insufficient, the fish population can suffer significant losses, creating a strategic tension between short-term gains and long-term sustainability.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                | Upstream Farmer (U) | Downstream Farmer (D) |
|----------------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| **U: Low Water Inflow** | (1, 1)              | (1, 1)                |
| **U: High Water Inflow** | (5, 5)             | (3, 3)                |

**Explanation:**
- **Low Water Inflow:** Water inflow is insufficient, leading to a risk of ecological thresholds being crossed. Both farmers suffer reduced yields.
- **High Water Inflow:** Water inflow is sufficient, allowing for the growth of the fish population and higher yields for both farmers.
- **Payoffs:** The payoffs reflect the total number of fields irrigated, which is higher for both farmers if the water inflow is sufficient. The payoffs are lower if the water inflow is insufficient, leading to ecological thresholds being crossed.
