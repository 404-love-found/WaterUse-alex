### Distinct Action Situations for the Decentralized Case (DV)

#### 1. Strategic Tension: Water Allocation vs. Economic Risk Aversion
**Tension**: Upstream farmers face a trade-off between conserving water for future uncertain conditions and maximizing immediate economic gains by increasing the number of irrigated fields.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix**:

| Upstream Farmer's Choice | Downstream Farmer's Choice | Upstream Farmer's Payoff | Downstream Farmer's Payoff |
|--------------------------|----------------------------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| 1 Field (Safe)           | 1 Field (Safe)             | 100                      | 100                        |
| 1 Field (Safe)           | 2 Fields (Risk)            | 120                      | 80                         |
| 2 Fields (Risk)          | 1 Field (Safe)             | 80                       | 120                        |
| 2 Fields (Risk)          | 2 Fields (Risk)            | 150                      | 150                        |

**Justification**: In the decentralized version, each farmer independently decides on the number of fields to irrigate. Upstream farmers have to consider the potential impact of their water allocation on downstream farmers. This tension arises because increasing fields can lead to higher yields but also higher water stress, which could affect downstream farmers. The matrix reflects the potential payoffs for both farmers, considering the risk aversion of upstream farmers and the potential for economic gain by taking risks.

#### 2. Strategic Tension: Water Stress and Yield Trade-off
**Tension**: Farmers must balance the risk of water stress during the growing season with the need to maximize yields.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix**:

| Upstream Farmer's Choice | Downstream Farmer's Choice | Upstream Farmer's Payoff | Downstream Farmer's Payoff |
|--------------------------|----------------------------|--------------------------|----------------------------|
| No Stress (10 Fields)    | No Stress (10 Fields)      | 1000                     | 1000                       |
| No Stress (10 Fields)    | Stress (8 Fields)          | 1100                     | 800                        |
| Stress (8 Fields)        | No Stress (10 Fields)      | 800                      | 1100                       |
| Stress (8 Fields)        | Stress (8 Fields)          | 900                      | 900                        |

**Justification**: The matrix reflects the trade-off between the risk of water stress and the potential for higher yields. Upstream farmers have to decide whether to risk water stress to maximize yields, knowing that downstream farmers might also be facing similar choices. The payoffs represent the yields achieved under different scenarios of water stress.

#### 3. Strategic Tension: Fish Population Survival vs. Economic Gain
**Tension**: Farmers must balance the need to maintain the fish population for future harvests with the immediate economic gain from fishing.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix**:

| Upstream Farmer's Choice | Downstream Farmer's Choice | Upstream Farmer's Payoff | Downstream Farmer's Payoff |

| High Fish Harvest        | Moderate Fish Harvest      | 400                      | 600                        |
| High Fish Harvest        | High Fish Harvest          | 700                      | 700                        |

**Justification**: The matrix reflects the tension between the economic gain from high fish harvests and the risk of overfishing, which could lead to a collapse of the fish population and future economic losses. The payoffs represent the immediate economic gains versus the potential long-term ecological and economic costs.

### Spatial Asymmetry and Ecological Thresholds
- **Spatial Asymmetry**: Upstream farmers have access to water first, which gives them a strategic advantage in terms of water allocation and decision-making. Downstream farmers, who rely on the water flow from upstream, face the risk of water stress and lower yields.
- **Ecological Thresholds**: The fish population survival depends on the threshold water inflow during May. If the inflow is below the threshold, the fish population can collapse, leading to a significant economic impact on downstream farmers.

### Conclusion
The decentralized case (DV) presents several strategic tensions that highlight the complexity of water resource management in a decentralized setting. These tensions are influenced by spatial asymmetry and ecological thresholds, making it crucial for farmers to balance economic gains with ecological sustainability.
