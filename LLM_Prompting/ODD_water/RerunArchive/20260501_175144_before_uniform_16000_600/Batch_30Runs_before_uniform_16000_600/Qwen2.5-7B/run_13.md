### Analysis of Distinct Action Situations in the Decentralized Version (DV)

#### 1. Strategic Tension: Risk Aversion vs Risk Taking
**Title:** Risk Aversion vs Risk Taking in Water Allocation

**Justification:** In the decentralized version, farmers must decide on the number of fields to irrigate based on their income, past water availability, and budget. Upstream farmers have less access to water, creating a strategic tension between risk-averse and risk-taking behaviors.

**Matrix:**
```
|                | Upstream Farmer (U) - Risk Averse | Upstream Farmer (U) - Risk Taking |
|----------------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Downstream Farmer (D) - Risk Averse | (5, 5)                            | (6, 4)                            |
| Downstream Farmer (D) - Risk Taking | (4, 6)                            | (7, 7)                            |
```
- **Upstream Farmer (U) - Risk Averse:** If the upstream farmer is risk-averse, they will irrigate fewer fields (5) to ensure a stable income.
- **Upstream Farmer (U) - Risk Taking:** If the upstream farmer is risk-taking, they will irrigate more fields (6) to maximize potential gains.
- **Downstream Farmer (D) - Risk Averse:** The downstream farmer will also irrigate fewer fields (5) to avoid water stress and ensure a stable income.
- **Downstream Farmer (D) - Risk Taking:** The downstream farmer will irrigate more fields (6) to maximize potential gains.

**Spatial Asymmetry:** Upstream farmers have less access to water, making them more risk-averse due to potential water stress. Downstream farmers have more access to water, allowing them to be more risk-taking.

**Ecological Thresholds:** If the water allocation is not sufficient, it can lead to a tipping point where downstream farmers cannot access enough water, resulting in a significant decrease in yields.

#### 2. Strategic Tension: Budget Constraints vs Water Availability
**Title:** Budget Constraints vs Water Availability in Water Allocation

**Justification:** Farmers must balance their budget constraints with the available water to determine the number of fields to irrigate. This creates a strategic tension between prioritizing budget constraints and maximizing water use.

**Matrix:**
```
|                | Farmer with Budget Constraints (B) - Low Budget | Farmer with Budget Constraints (B) - High Budget |
|----------------|-------------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| Farmer with Water Availability (W) - Low Water Availability | (3, 3)                                          | (4, 4)                                          |
| Farmer with Water Availability (W) - High Water Availability | (4, 4)                                          | (5, 5)                                          |
```
- **Farmer with Budget Constraints (B) - Low Budget:** If the farmer has a low budget, they will irrigate fewer fields (3) to ensure they can afford irrigation.
- **Farmer with Budget Constraints (B) - High Budget:** If the farmer has a high budget, they will irrigate more fields (4) to maximize water use.
- **Farmer with Water Availability (W) - Low Water Availability:** The farmer will irrigate fewer fields (3) due to limited water availability.
- **Farmer with Water Availability (W) - High Water Availability:** The farmer will irrigate more fields (4) to maximize water use.

**Spatial Asymmetry:** Upstream farmers have less water availability, making them more constrained by budget. Downstream farmers have more water availability, allowing them to be more flexible with budget constraints.

**Ecological Thresholds:** If water availability is critically low, it can lead to a tipping point where farmers cannot irrigate enough fields, resulting in a significant decrease in yields.

#### 3. Strategic Tension: Individual vs Collective Resilience
**Title:** Individual Resilience vs Collective Resilience in Water Allocation

**Justification:** Farmers must decide whether to focus on individual resilience by maximizing their own fields or on collective resilience by ensuring fair water distribution. This creates a strategic tension between individual and collective interests.

**Matrix:**
```
|                | Farmer (F) - Individual Resilience | Farmer (F) - Collective Resilience |
|----------------|-----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Other Farmers (O) - Individual Resilience | (6, 6)                            | (5, 5)                            |
| Other Farmers (O) - Collective Resilience | (5, 5)                            | (4, 4)                            |
```
- **Farmer (F) - Individual Resilience:** If the farmer focuses on individual resilience, they will irrigate more fields (6) to maximize their own yield.
- **Farmer (F) - Collective Resilience:** If the farmer focuses on collective resilience, they will irrigate fewer fields (5) to ensure fair water distribution.
- **Other Farmers (O) - Individual Resilience:** The other farmers will also focus on individual resilience, irrigating more fields (6) to maximize their own yield.
- **Other Farmers (O) - Collective Resilience:** The other farmers will focus on collective resilience, irrigating fewer fields (5) to ensure fair water distribution.

**Spatial Asymmetry:** Upstream farmers have less access to water, making them more focused on individual resilience. Downstream farmers have more access to water, allowing them to consider collective resilience.

**Ecological Thresholds:** If the focus on individual resilience is too strong, it can lead to a tipping point where water distribution becomes unfair, resulting in a decrease in collective resilience and yields.

### Summary
The decentralized version of the model presents distinct strategic tensions between risk aversion and risk taking, budget constraints and water availability, and individual versus collective resilience. These tensions are critical for understanding the dynamics of water allocation in the context of spatial asymmetry and ecological thresholds.
