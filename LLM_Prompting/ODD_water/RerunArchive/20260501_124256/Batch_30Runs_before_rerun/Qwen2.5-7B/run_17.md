### Title: Distinct Action Situations in the Decentralized Version (DV) of the Water Use Model

### 1. Strategic Tension: Yield vs. Risk Aversion (Upstream vs. Downstream Farmers)

**Tension:**
Upstream farmers have less information about water availability due to the cumulative effect of upstream water withdrawals. Downstream farmers have more information but also face the risk of reduced water availability due to upstream withdrawals. The strategic dilemma is whether to take a risk and irrigate more fields, knowing that this could lead to water stress and reduced yields, or to maintain a conservative approach to ensure sustainable yields.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                 | Downstream Farmers (Conservative) | Downstream Farmers (Risk Averse) |
|-----------------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Upstream Farmers (Conservative)** | (Y1, Y1)                          | (Y2, Y3)                        |
| **Upstream Farmers (Risk Averse)**  | (Y3, Y2)                          | (Y4, Y4)                        |

**Justification:**
- **(Y1, Y1):** Both farmers irrigate conservatively, leading to stable but lower yields.
- **(Y2, Y3):** Upstream farmer irrigates riskily, leading to higher yields if water is available, but downstream farmer irrigates conservatively, leading to lower yields if water is scarce.
- **(Y3, Y2):** Upstream farmer irrigates conservatively, leading to lower yields if water is scarce, but downstream farmer irrigates riskily, leading to higher yields if water is available.
- **(Y4, Y4):** Both farmers irrigate riskily, leading to higher but uncertain yields.

**Critical Constraints:**
- Spatial Asymmetry: Upstream farmers have less information and face a higher risk of water stress.
- Ecological Thresholds: If water stress exceeds a certain threshold, yields could drop significantly.

### 4. Strategic Tension: Short-term Gain vs. Long-term Sustainability (Upstream vs. Downstream Farmers)

**Tension:**
Upstream farmers face the temptation to maximize short-term gains by irrigating more fields, risking long-term sustainability and potential ecological thresholds. Downstream farmers rely on the long-term sustainability of the ecosystem, which is sensitive to short-term over-extraction. The strategic dilemma is whether to prioritize short-term gains or long-term sustainability.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                 | Downstream Farmers (Conservative) | Downstream Farmers (Risk Averse) |
|-----------------|-----------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| **Upstream Farmers (Conservative)** | (Y1, S1)                          | (Y2, S2)                        |
| **Upstream Farmers (Risk Averse)**  | (Y2, S1)                          | (Y3, S3)                        |

**Justification:**
- **(Y1, S1):** Both farmers prioritize long-term sustainability, leading to stable yields and ecosystem health.
- **(Y2, S2):** Upstream farmer prioritizes short-term gains, leading to higher yields if water is available, but downstream farmer prioritizes long-term sustainability, leading to stable ecosystem health.
- **(Y3, S1):** Upstream farmer prioritizes long-term sustainability, leading to lower yields if water is scarce, but downstream farmer prioritizes short-term gains, leading to higher but uncertain yields.
- **(Y4, S4):** Both farmers prioritize short-term gains, leading to higher but uncertain yields and ecosystem health.

**Critical Constraints:**
- Spatial Asymmetry: Upstream farmers have more control over short-term gains, while downstream farmers rely on the long-term sustainability of the ecosystem.
- Ecological Thresholds: If short-term gains exceed a certain threshold, long-term sustainability could be compromised.
