### Title: Distinct Action Situations in the Decentralized Version (DV) of the Water Use Model

### 1. Spatial Asymmetry: Upstream vs Downstream Farmers

#### Tension: Strategic Tension Between Upstream and Downstream Farmers

**Justification:** Upstream farmers have a better initial position to access water, which can lead to a strategic advantage in terms of water availability and yield. Downstream farmers, on the other hand, face the risk of reduced water flow after upstream farmers have taken their share. This creates a tension where upstream farmers might over-exploit water resources, leading to a negative impact on downstream farmers.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

|                | Downstream Irrigate 10 | Downstream Irrigate 9 |
|----------------|-----------------------|----------------------|
| **Upstream Irrigate 10** | (10, 10)                | (10, 9)               |
| **Upstream Irrigate 9**   | (9, 10)                 | (9, 9)                |

**Matrix Explanation:**
- If both upstream and downstream farmers irrigate 10 fields, both get 10 units of yield.
- If upstream irrigates 10 fields and downstream irrigates 9 fields, both get 9 units of yield.
- If both irrigate 9 fields, both get 9 units of yield.

**CRITICAL CONSTRAINTS:**
- The maximum number of fields any farmer can irrigate is 10.
- Downstream farmers face reduced water availability due to upstream farmers' actions.

### 2. Ecological Thresholds: Tipping Points in Water Flow

#### Tension: Strategic Tension at Ecological Tipping Points

**Justification:** The ecological thresholds, particularly the migration of larvae into the lake, can create tipping points where the fish population dynamics shift dramatically. If water flow is too low, larvae cannot survive, leading to a significant reduction in the fish population. This can create a strategic tension where farmers must balance their immediate water demands with the long-term ecological health of the fish population.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

- If upstream irrigates 10 fields and downstream irrigates 9 fields, both get 9 units of yield, but the fish population may still face a tipping point.
- If both irrigate 9 fields, both get 9 units of yield, but the fish population is less likely to face a tipping point.

**CRITICAL CONSTRAINTS:**
- The fish population is vulnerable to ecological thresholds, particularly the migration of larvae.
- Downstream farmers face the risk of reduced fish population if water flow is too low due to upstream farmers' actions.

### 4. Strategic Tension in Yield and Budget

#### Tension: Strategic Tension Between Yield and Budget Constraints

**Justification:** Farmers must decide how many fields to irrigate based on their budget and expected water availability. This creates a strategic tension where farmers might over-exploit their budget or water availability to maximize short-term yield, but this could lead to long-term negative consequences.

- If upstream irrigates 10 fields and downstream irrigates 9 fields, both get 9 units of yield, but this is within budget constraints.
- If both irrigate 9 fields, both get 9 units of yield, and this is within budget constraints.

**CRITICAL CONSTRAINTS:**
- Farmers must balance their immediate yield with long-term budget constraints.
- Downstream farmers face the risk of budget constraints if they over-exploit their water availability.

### 5. Strategic Tension in Water Stress

#### Tension: Strategic Tension in Managing Water Stress

**Justification:** Water stress occurs when the amount of water delivered is less than the amount needed to irrigate all of the planned fields. This can create a strategic tension where farmers must decide how many fields to irrigate to avoid water stress, which can negatively impact yields.

**2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**

- If upstream irrigates 10 fields and downstream irrigates 9 fields, both get 9 units of yield, and this avoids water stress.
- If both irrigate 9 fields, both get 9 units of yield, and this avoids water stress.

**CRITICAL CONSTRAINTS:**
- Farmers must manage water stress to avoid negative impacts on yields.
- Downstream farmers face the risk of water stress if upstream farmers over-exploit water resources.
