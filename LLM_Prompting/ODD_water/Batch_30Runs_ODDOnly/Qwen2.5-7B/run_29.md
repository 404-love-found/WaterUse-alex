### Analysis of Distinct Action Situations in the Decentralized Case (DV) Using the IAD Framework

#### Title: Strategic Tension in Water Allocation and Fishing Decisions

#### Tension: Spatial Asymmetry and Ecological Thresholds in Water Allocation and Fishing

In the decentralized version (DV) of the model, farmers make decisions based on their local water availability and their memory of past water flows. This leads to a strategic tension between upstream and downstream farmers, as well as a potential ecological threshold in the fish population dynamics.

#### 2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix

**Players:**
- **Upstream Farmer (U)**
- **Downstream Farmer (D)**

**Strategies:**
- **Upstream Farmer (U):** Increase or Decrease the number of irrigated fields.
- **Downstream Farmer (D):** Increase or Decrease the number of irrigated fields.

**Payoff Matrix:**

|            | Upstream Farmer (U) Decreases | Upstream Farmer (U) Increases |
|------------|------------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Downstream Farmer (D) Decreases** | (2, 2)                       | (1, 3)                        |
| **Downstream Farmer (D) Increases** | (3, 1)                       | (1, 1)                        |

#### Justification

1. **Spatial Asymmetry:**
   - **Upstream Farmer (U) Decreases:**
     - If the upstream farmer decreases the number of irrigated fields, the downstream farmer has more water available, which is beneficial for fishing and agriculture. Both farmers benefit from a more balanced water distribution.
     - **Payoff (2, 2)**: Both upstream and downstream farmers have a moderate benefit.
   - **Upstream Farmer (U) Increases:**
     - If the upstream farmer increases the number of irrigated fields, it reduces the water available downstream. This could lead to water stress for the downstream farmer, affecting fishing and agriculture.
     - **Payoff (1, 3)**: The upstream farmer benefits more, while the downstream farmer is at a disadvantage.

2. **Ecological Thresholds:**
   - **Downstream Farmer (D) Decreases:**
     - If the downstream farmer decreases the number of irrigated fields, it allows more water to flow into the lake, potentially leading to a higher fish population. However, this may not be sustainable if the fish population exceeds the ecological threshold.
     - **Payoff (3, 1)**: The downstream farmer benefits more, but there is a risk of overfishing and ecological imbalance.
   - **Downstream Farmer (D) Increases:**
     - If the downstream farmer increases the number of irrigated fields, it further reduces the water available for the lake, potentially leading to a decline in the fish population. This could trigger an ecological threshold, leading to a sudden decline in fish abundance.
     - **Payoff (1, 1)**: Both farmers are at a disadvantage, as the ecological threshold is reached, leading to a decline in the fish population and reduced yields.

This matrix captures the strategic tension between upstream and downstream farmers, reflecting the importance of spatial asymmetry and ecological thresholds in the decentralized water use model.
