### Action Situation 1: Upstream Farmer Increases Irrigation vs Downstream Farmer Increases Irrigation
#### Tension: Competition for Water Resources
#### Matrix:

|  | Downstream Farmer Irrigates 5 Fields | Downstream Farmer Irrigates 10 Fields |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Upstream Farmer Irrigates 5 Fields** | Upstream: 20, Downstream: 15 | Upstream: 25, Downstream: 5 |
| **Downstream Farmer Irrigates 10 Fields** | Upstream: 10, Downstream: 20 | Upstream: 5, Downstream: 10 |

#### Justification: In the decentralized version, when an upstream farmer increases irrigation, it reduces the water available for the downstream farmer, leading to a strategic tension. The matrix reflects the payoff in terms of crop yield, assuming that each field irrigated contributes a certain amount to the yield, but with diminishing returns due to water scarcity. The upstream farmer has an advantage due to spatial asymmetry.

### Action Situation 2: Farmer Invests in Irrigation vs Farmer Invests in Fishing
#### Tension: Allocation of Resources Between Irrigation and Fishing
#### Matrix:

|  | Farmer Invests in Fishing | Farmer Invests in Irrigation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Farmer Invests in Fishing** | 15, 10 | 20, 5 |
| **Farmer Invests in Irrigation** | 10, 20 | 25, 15 |

#### Justification: This action situation reflects the dilemma of allocating limited resources between irrigation for agriculture and fishing. Investing in irrigation can increase crop yields but may overexploit water resources, especially if many farmers do the same. Investing in fishing can provide a steady income but may not be as lucrative as successful irrigation. The payoffs are representative of the potential returns from each activity, considering ecological thresholds such as water scarcity affecting fish populations.

### Action Situation 3: Farmer Increases Fields Irrigated Near Ecological Threshold vs Farmer Conserves Water
#### Tension: Balancing Economic Gain with Ecological Sustainability
#### Matrix:

|  | Farmer Conserves Water | Farmer Increases Fields Irrigated |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Farmer Increases Fields Irrigated** | 22, 12 | 30, 0 |
| **Farmer Conserves Water** | 18, 15 | 10, 20 |

#### Justification: This situation highlights the tension between short-term economic gains from increasing irrigation and the long-term sustainability of the ecosystem. If a farmer increases the fields irrigated near an ecological threshold (e.g., maximum water extraction limit), it might lead to high short-term gains but risks crossing the threshold, causing significant losses (represented by the 0 payoff). Conserving water ensures sustainability but might result in lower economic returns. The matrix reflects the potential outcomes considering these ecological thresholds and spatial asymmetry.
