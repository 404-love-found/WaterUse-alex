# Run 3 - deepseek-ai/DeepSeek-V4-Pro

### Action Situation 1: Queue Bypass
**Tension:** Individual incentive to jump the queue versus collective interest in fair waiting order.  
**Matrix:**
| User A / User B | Respect Queue | Bypass Queue |
|---|---|---|
| Respect Queue | (Fair wait, Fair wait) | (Long wait, Short wait) |
| Bypass Queue | (Short wait, Long wait) | (Conflict, Conflict) |
**Justification:** The model explicitly states that users decide whether to bypass the queue (II.ii.a) and that bypassing causes the next user to wait longer (I.iii.a). This creates a social dilemma where individual gain from bypassing undermines collective fairness.

### Action Situation 2: Move‑out (Overstay)
**Tension:** Convenience of staying parked after charging versus the next user’s need for access.  
**Matrix:**
| Occupant / Waiting User | Wait Patiently | Complain |
|---|---|---|
| Move Promptly | (Minor inconvenience, Quick access) | (Minor inconvenience, Quick access) |
| Stay (Overstay) | (High convenience, Blocked) | (Risk penalty, Possible resolution) |
**Justification:** The model describes users deciding whether to move promptly after useful charging (II.ii.a) and notes that a vehicle remaining connected blocks the bay (I.iii.a). The waiting user may complain, creating an interdependent decision situation.

### Action Situation 3: Rule Enforcement
**Tension:** Staff’s cost of enforcement versus maintaining order; user’s benefit of violation versus risk of penalty.  
**Matrix:**
| Staff / User | Comply | Violate |
|---|---|---|
| Enforce | (Costly order, Normal) | (Successful enforcement, Penalized) |
| Not Enforce | (Saved effort, Normal) | (Rule erosion, Benefit) |
**Justification:** Staff decide whether to enforce posted rules, and users decide whether to comply (II.ii.a). This is a classic inspection game, central to the model’s study of compliance with posted rules.

### Action Situation 4: Complaint and Response
**Tension:** User’s desire to address violations versus staff’s willingness to respond.  
**Matrix:**
| User / Staff | Respond | Ignore |
|---|---|---|
| Complain | (Violation addressed, Effort cost) | (Frustration, Saved effort) |
| Not Complain | (Status quo, Status quo) | (Status quo, Status quo) |
**Justification:** Users decide whether to complain about violations, and staff decide whether to respond (II.ii.a). Complaint outcomes affect future compliance and trust (III.iv.a).

### Action Situation 5: Informal Priority Request
**Tension:** Favoritism versus formal rule adherence.  
**Matrix:**
| User / Staff | Grant Priority | Deny Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Request Priority | (Get priority, Favor gain/risk) | (Disappointment, Fairness maintained) |
| Not Request | (Status quo, Status quo) | (Status quo, Status quo) |
**Justification:** The model mentions users requesting informal priority and staff deciding to tolerate or deny such requests (I.iii.a). This creates a tension between informal relationships and posted queue rules.

### Action Situation 6: Reservation Commitment
**Tension:** Trust in reservations versus opportunistic slot‑taking.  
**Matrix:**
| Reserver / Other User | Honor Reservation | Take Slot |
|---|---|---|
| Show Up | (Successful charge, Fair wait) | (Conflict, Conflict) |
| No‑Show | (Wasted slot, Wasted opportunity) | (Slot taken, Successful charge) |
**Justification:** The model includes a reservation platform where users can reserve slots and others may honor or skip (III.iv.a). The reliability of the reservation system depends on mutual expectations.

### Action Situation 7: Resident vs Non‑resident Queue Priority
**Tension:** Resident entitlement (due to discount) versus non‑resident expectation of equal queue treatment.  
**Matrix:**
| Resident / Non‑resident | Insist on Equal Queue | Yield |
|---|---|---|
| Claim Priority | (Conflict, Conflict) | (Resident gets priority, Non‑resident waits) |
| Accept Queue | (Non‑resident gets slot, Resident waits) | (Fair queue, Fair queue) |
**Justification:** The model highlights that residents may expect priority because of their discounted rate, while non‑residents expect equal first‑come‑first‑served treatment (II.i.b, II.viii.b). This creates a distinct tension over queue order.
