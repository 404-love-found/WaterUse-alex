### Action Situation 1: Queue Bypassing Dilemma
**Tension:** Individual users gain faster access by bypassing the queue, but widespread bypassing erodes queue order and increases collective waiting time.
**Matrix:**
| User A / User B | Follow Queue | Bypass Queue |
|---|---|---|
| Follow Queue | (3, 3) | (1, 4) |
| Bypass Queue | (4, 1) | (2, 2) |
**Justification:** The ODD+D states that users “compete for fair queue access” and that “if a driver bypasses the queue … the assigned next user waits longer.” This creates a social dilemma where bypassing is individually rational (4) when others comply (1), but mutual compliance (3) yields a better collective outcome than mutual bypassing (2). The resident discount may increase residents’ perceived entitlement, shifting their subjective payoffs but not the underlying structure.

### Action Situation 2: Move‑out Compliance Dilemma
**Tension:** After useful charging, overstaying is convenient for the current user but blocks the bay for the next user, creating a tension between individual convenience and collective charger turnover.
**Matrix:**
| User A / User B | Move Promptly | Overstay |
|---|---|---|
| Move Promptly | (3, 3) | (1, 4) |
| Overstay | (4, 1) | (2, 2) |
**Justification:** The ODD+D notes that “a vehicle that remains connected after useful charging may block the bay without paying additional energy charges” and that this “creates pressure on queue fairness.” The payoff structure mirrors a Prisoner’s Dilemma: overstaying is a dominant strategy, but mutual overstaying leads to severe congestion and longer waits for all.

### Action Situation 3: Rule Enforcement Interaction
**Tension:** Staff must trade off the effort of enforcement against the risk of disorder, while users decide whether to comply based on the likelihood of being caught.
**Matrix:**
| Staff / User | Comply | Violate |
|---|---|---|
| Enforce | (2, 3) | (3, 1) |
| Not Enforce | (4, 3) | (1, 4) |
**Justification:** The ODD+D explains that “staff observe some violations and complaints. They decide whether to enforce posted rules … Users adapt to enforcement.” This is a classic inspection game: staff prefer order with minimal effort (4,3), but if they do not enforce, users will violate (1,4). Enforcement yields a moderate outcome for staff (2,3) or (3,1). Payoffs are ordinal (4=best, 1=worst) and inferred from the described incentives.

### Action Situation 4: Informal Priority Exchange
**Tension:** A user may request preferential treatment, and staff may grant it to maintain personal relationships, but this undermines queue legitimacy for others.
**Matrix:**
| User / Staff | Grant if asked | Deny if asked |
|---|---|---|
| Request | (4, 2) | (1, 3) |
| Not Request | (2, 4) | (2, 4) |
**Justification:** The ODD+D highlights that “parking staff may receive informal requests to hold a charging bay, overlook a queue violation … Informal priority can reduce waiting uncertainty for one driver, but it weakens queue legitimacy for others.” The matrix captures the private gain for the requesting user and the staff’s trade‑off between favour and rule integrity.

### Action Situation 5: Complaint Feedback Loop
**Tension:** Observers of violations decide whether to complain; staff decide whether to respond. Unresolved complaints erode trust and future compliance.
**Matrix:**
| Observer / Staff | Respond | Ignore |
|---|---|---|
| Complain | (3, 2) | (2, 1) |
| Not Complain | (1, 4) | (1, 3) |
**Justification:** The ODD+D includes a “complaint and legitimacy feedback” submodel where “users may complain when queue order is violated” and “staff decide whether to respond.” The payoff structure reflects that complaining is only worthwhile if staff respond (3 vs. 2), while staff prefer not to respond unless a complaint forces action (4 vs. 2). Inferred ordinal payoffs are shown.

### Action Situation 6: Capacity Expansion Game
**Tension:** Management decides whether to add costly chargers; residents decide whether to pressure for expansion or accept congestion.
**Matrix:**
| Management / Residents | Pressure | Accept |
|---|---|---|
| Expand | (3, 3) | (4, 2) |
| Not Expand | (1, 4) | (2, 1) |
**Justification:** The ODD+D describes “capacity planning” as a submodel where “management may evaluate whether to add chargers” and “residents can pressure management.” The matrix shows that expansion under pressure yields moderate payoffs for both (3,3), while unilateral expansion benefits residents more (4,2). Inaction with resident acceptance is the status quo (2,1). Payoffs are inferred ordinal rankings.
