### **Action Situation 1: The Upstream-Downstream Water Appropriation Dilemma (A Spatial Prisoner's Dilemma)**

*   **IAD Analysis (Biophysical & Community Attributes):** This situation occurs in the **Decentralised Version (DV)**. The action arena consists of two farmers at different locations along the river: an upstream (US) agent and a downstream (DS) agent. The biophysical condition is a limited and uncertain water flow. The community attribute is the lack of a coordinating authority or communication. The rules-in-use are that agents act sequentially (US first) to withdraw water for irrigation based on their individual predictions and heuristics.

*   **Strategic Dilemma:** This is a classic **Prisoner's Dilemma** with a spatial twist. The dominant strategy for the US farmer is to appropriate as much water as they need (Defect), as they bear no direct cost for the water scarcity they cause downstream. The DS farmer's payoff is heavily dependent on the US farmer's action. Mutual cooperation (water restraint) would lead to a better aggregate outcome, but it is unstable because the US farmer has a powerful incentive to defect.

*   **2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**
    *   **Players:** Upstream Farmer (Player 1) vs. Downstream Farmer (Player 2)
    *   **Strategies:**
        *   **Cooperate (C):** Irrigate a moderate number of fields (e.g., 5 fields), conserving water.
        *   **Defect (D):** Irrigate the maximum profitable number of fields (e.g., 9 fields), maximizing personal water use.
    *   **Payoffs (US Payoff, DS Payoff):** Representing net agricultural yield for the season.

| | **Downstream: Cooperate (5 fields)** | **Downstream: Defect (9 fields)** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Upstream: Cooperate (5 fields)** | (Medium, Medium) <br> e.g., (5, 5) | (Low, Very High) <br> e.g., (3, 8) |
| **Upstream: Defect (9 fields)** | (Very High, Very Low) <br> e.g., (8, 2) | (High, Low) <br> e.g., (6, 3) |

**Justification:** The matrix is **not symmetric**. The upstream player's defection cripples the downstream player's yield (8, 2), but the downstream player's defection only slightly reduces the upstream player's yield (3, 8 vs. 5, 5) because the upstream player acts first. Defection is the dominant strategy for the upstream player, leading to the Pareto-inferior outcome (6, 3).

### **Action Situation 2: The Fishery Access Game (A Stag Hunt Variant)**

*   **IAD Analysis (Biophysical & Community Attributes):** This situation is present in **both CV and DV**. The action arena is the fishing lake at the end of the river. The biophysical condition is a vulnerable, age-structured fish population that can be over-harvested. The community attribute is the rule that access to the lake is sequential, based on location (downstream farmers go first). The rules-in-use grant each farmer a fixed target catch level.

*   **Strategic Dilemma:** This resembles a **Stag Hunt**, where the superior payoff requires mutual restraint (hunting the stag), but a safe, inferior payoff is available individually (hunting a hare). Here, the "stag" is a sustainable fishery that provides long-term income, and the "hare" is maximizing short-term catch. If both players restrain themselves, the population remains viable (High, High). If one player defects (over-harvests), they get a very high short-term payoff but crash the population for both players in the future, modeled here as a low payoff in subsequent rounds.

*   **2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**
    *   **Players:** Downstream Farmer 1 (Player 1) vs. Downstream Farmer 2 (Player 2) (e.g., the two farmers closest to the lake).
    *   **Strategies:**
        *   **Restrain (R):** Catch below the maximum target, ensuring population sustainability.
        *   **Max Harvest (MH):** Catch the full target level every time.
    *   **Payoffs (P1 Payoff, P2 Payoff):** Representing fish catch income, where a crashed population leads to zero future income.

| | **Farmer 2: Restrain** | **Farmer 2: Max Harvest** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Farmer 1: Restrain** | (Sustainable High, Sustainable High) <br> e.g., (7, 7) | (Very Low, Temporary High) <br> e.g., (1, 9) |
| **Farmer 1: Max Harvest** | (Temporary High, Very Low) <br> e.g., (9, 1) | (Crash, Crash) <br> e.g., (2, 2) |

**Justification:** The matrix is **symmetric in payoffs but asymmetric in action** due to sequential access (Farmer 1 acts before Farmer 2, allowing them to grab a slightly higher temporary payoff (9 vs. 1). There are two Nash equilibria: (Restrain, Restrain) and (Max Harvest, Max Harvest). The former is Pareto superior but requires trust that the other player will also cooperate.

### **Action Situation 3: The Central Planner's Investment Dilemma (A Public Goods Game)**

*   **IAD Analysis (Rules & Attributes):** This situation is specific to the **Centralised Version (CV)**. The action arena is the national authority's budget planning. The authority collects the net returns from all farmers' agriculture (the public pot) and must decide how to reinvest it. The community is the entire group of nine farmers. The rule is that the authority must provide an equal number of irrigated fields to every farmer.

*   **Strategic Dilemma:** This is a **Public Goods Game** where the national authority is the sole player deciding on contribution levels. The "public good" is the shared budget for future agricultural investment, which benefits all farmers equally. The strategic dilemma for the authority is between investing heavily in the public good (irrigation capacity for all) for long-term resilience or conserving the budget (a form of defection) to protect against future uncertainty, which is a safer but less productive strategy.

*   **2-Player Normal Form Payoff Matrix:**
    *   **Players:** This is a game against Nature/Uncertainty. Player 1 is the **National Authority**. Player 2 represents **Water Availability** in the next season, which can be **High** or **Low**.
    *   **Strategies for Authority:**
        *   **Invest:** Use most of the budget to maximize the number of irrigated fields for all farmers (e.g., set to 8 fields each).
        *   **Conserve:** Be conservative, irrigate fewer fields to save budget (e.g., set to 4 fields each).
    *   **Payoffs (Authority's Budget Outcome, N/A):** The payoff is the change in the central budget.

| | **Nature: High Water** | **Nature: Low Water** |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Authority: Invest** | (Large Surplus, -) <br> e.g., (+8, -) | (Large Deficit, -) <br> e.g., (-5, -) |
| **Authority: Conserve** | (Modest Surplus, -) <br> e.g., (+3, -) | (Modest Deficit/Break-even, -) <br> e.g., (0, -) |

**Justification:** This matrix is **not symmetric** and reflects the core dilemma of the central planner. The choice is between a high-risk, high-reward strategy (Invest) and a low-risk, low-reward strategy (Conserve). The payoff is contingent on the exogenous state of nature (water flow), which the authority must predict with uncertainty. This captures the bounded rationality and heuristic-based decision-making described in the model.
